## Section 1: Regulatory Intelligence — The Rate Change Notice Gap

### The Core Opportunity

The fundamental insight powering a regulatory intelligence module is that **rate case filings are public information from Day 1** — every PUC docket is accessible to any stakeholder who monitors it — but almost no commercial energy buyer does. The gap is not information availability; it is information processing. Clear Current can build a systematic docket-monitoring engine that surfaces financially material rate changes months before they hit a commercial customer's bill.

### Rate Case Frequency and the Regulatory Landscape by State

The five target states represent meaningfully different regulatory environments, each creating distinct monitoring opportunities.

**Ohio (PUCO):** The PUCO must complete all rate cases within 275 days (approximately 9 months) by statute. AES Ohio filed in November 2020, received approval December 2022 — a 25-month process that included settlement negotiations. AES Ohio filed again in November 2024; all parties reached settlement August 2025, with the PUCO issuing its final order November 2025, resulting in a ~9% increase for a 1,000 kWh residential customer. AEP Ohio filed a 2025 base case, reached a settlement, and filed it at the PUCO in January 2026. FirstEnergy had two simultaneous rate cases decided in December 2024 involving ESP IV vs. ESP V withdrawal. The pattern for major Ohio utilities: formal base rate cases every 3-5 years, but multiple overlapping proceedings (ESP reviews, reconciliation cases, special riders) create near-continuous regulatory activity.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5]

**Illinois (ICC):** Illinois is meaningfully different from other states because the 2021 Clean Energy Jobs Act (CEJA) created a grid plan and annual reconciliation structure that replaces the traditional multi-year rate case cycle. Under this framework, ComEd and Ameren submit annual reconciliation petitions to recover capital expenses above budget. In December 2025, the ICC approved rate increases of $606 million for ComEd and $308.6 million for Ameren, both spread through 2027. The ICC reduced both requests — trimming $25.4 million from ComEd's ask and $11.2 million from Ameren's — on grounds of imprudent expenditures. Under CEJA, Illinois commercial customers now face annual rate adjustment proceedings rather than the traditional 3-5 year cycle, generating a continuous stream of monitored proceedings.[^6]

**Pennsylvania (PUC):** Pennsylvania's rate case structure requires utilities to notify customers at least 60 days in advance of filing a rate increase request. The rate request is then suspended for up to 7 months while the PUC investigates — making the total process approximately 9 months from filing to effective date. PPL Electric filed a rate case in October 2025; the final PUC decision is due by July 1, 2026. This ~9-month cycle means a commercial customer who monitors the PUC docket from Day 1 of filing has 9 months of advance intelligence before any rate change becomes effective.[^7][^8][^9]

**New York (NYPSC):** New York's major rate cases are among the most complex in the country. National Grid filed its Upstate NY rate case in May 2024; a multi-party joint settlement was submitted in May 2025 (12 months), with final approval coming in 2025. ConEd filed a rate case in early 2025. NYSEG has requested a 39.4% increase in delivery revenues, which would translate to a 33.5% increase to a typical total bill. New York cases frequently involve 1,600+ information requests and multiple public hearings over 12-18 months, giving commercial customers substantial advance warning — if they are monitoring the docket.[^10][^11][^12]

**Texas (PUCT):** Texas is structurally distinct. Approximately 85% of the state operates under ERCOT deregulation, where commercial customers choose retail electric providers and face continuously fluctuating market-based supply rates — not traditional rate cases. TDU delivery charges (e.g., CenterPoint) update twice annually, in March and September. The regulatory intelligence opportunity in Texas is therefore different: it is monitoring ERCOT capacity auction results, TDU tariff filings, and supplier contract expiration intelligence rather than traditional PUC base rate cases. CenterPoint received PUCT approval in early 2026 to recover Hurricane Beryl and Winter Storm Enzo costs over 14 years — a multi-year tariff rider that affects all commercial customers in its territory.[^13][^14][^15][^16]

### Rate Change Magnitude: The Structural Shift

The scale of rate increases now hitting commercial customers is materially larger than what corporate budgets assume. Arcadia's 2026 Commercial Electricity Rate Report — which analyzed 81 utilities and 5 building types across 2020–2025 using 30,000+ tariff combinations — found that **97.5% of commercial facilities experienced electricity rate increases** during that five-year period, with **71% outpacing inflation**. The median compound annual growth rate for commercial electricity rates was **5.9%** — nearly double the 3% escalator still embedded in most corporate budgets.[^17][^18][^19]

The EIA confirms the trend: the commercial sector average retail price was 12.75 cents/kWh in 2024, rising to 13.63 cents/kWh in December 2025 — a **7.83% year-over-year increase**. In January 2026, commercial sector electricity revenues per kWh rose 6.4% compared to January 2025. Select state examples are more severe: New Jersey commercial customers saw 9.1% increases, Rhode Island 8.6%, New Hampshire 8.3%, Maine 7.3%.[^20][^21][^22][^23]

The most acute structural shock is in PJM's capacity market. The 2026-2027 PJM Base Residual Auction cleared at a record **$329.17/MW-day**, a 22% increase from the prior year. The prior year had already seen PJM capacity prices surge 833% from 2024 rates. For a typical 1-MW commercial load, Arcadia estimates annual capacity costs will rise from approximately $10,000 in 2024 to more than $120,000 by 2027 — a structural cost shift that efficiency improvements alone cannot offset. For PJM-territory commercial customers (spanning 13 eastern states from Illinois to New Jersey), this represents an **unbudgeted $110,000 annual liability on a single line item** for a standard commercial asset.[^24][^25][^26][^17]

EIA projects further increases: U.S. wholesale day-ahead electricity prices rose across most major hubs in 2025 versus 2024, with ISO-NE posting the largest increase at $29/MWh. ERCOT wholesale prices are forecast up approximately 45% in 2026 versus 2025 at the summer peak hours.[^15][^27]

### When Does Information Become Actionable?

The regulatory calendar creates a predictable advance-notice window at each stage:

| Stage                                        | Timing                            | Public Accessibility                 |
| -------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| Utility files Notice of Intent (some states) | 30-60 days before filing          | Yes — formal notice to commission    |
| Utility files rate case application          | Day 0                             | Yes — docket opened immediately      |
| Staff report/initial analysis                | Months 4-5                        | Yes — filed in docket                |
| Public hearings                              | Months 6-8                        | Yes — scheduled and noticed publicly |
| Proposed Decision / ALJ recommendation       | Month 7-8                         | Yes — filed in docket                |
| Final Commission order                       | Month 9 (Ohio) to 18+ months (NY) | Yes                                  |
| Rates effective                              | After order + notice period       | —                                    |

**The entire process is publicly accessible.** Pennsylvania requires utilities to notify customers in billing notices at least 60 days before filing; Ohio makes the docket accessible from filing day and the 275-day clock is public; the PUCT publishes "Notice of Proposed Rate Change" documents that any company can subscribe to. The actionable intelligence gap is not regulatory — it is that no commercial energy software currently aggregates, processes, and translates these filings into facility-level financial impact models.[^5][^8][^28]

### What Competitors Currently Offer (and Don't)

**Energy Toolbase** is the closest analog with a rate intelligence function, but it is fundamentally a solar/storage project development tool — not a commercial energy management platform. Its utility rate database covers 5,500+ rate records across 35 states, updated within 1-2 business days of changes. However, it does not monitor PUC dockets for proposed changes, does not alert commercial buyers to rate cases in progress, and is positioned entirely for solar/storage developers building proposals — not for commercial energy managers tracking budget exposure.[^29][^30][^31]

**EnergyCAP, WatchWire/Tango, and Schneider Resource Advisor** offer utility bill management, benchmarking, and historical analytics, but none provide forward-looking PUC docket monitoring or rate change impact modeling as a product feature. The regulatory intelligence module is a **genuine white space** in the current competitive landscape.

### The Budget Timing Gap

The regulatory intelligence opportunity is most acute at universities, where budget timelines and rate case timelines are structurally misaligned. Boston University's budget process illustrates the pattern: Stage I (next fiscal year budget) development begins in late summer/fall, with Board of Trustees approval in March — before the July 1 fiscal year start. Pennsylvania's PPL rate case filed October 2025 is scheduled for final decision by July 1, 2026 — the exact date a university's new fiscal year begins. A university that submitted its FY2027 energy budget in March 2026 cannot know its actual July 2026 rates until after the budget is locked.[^32][^9]

Corporate energy budgets face the same structural problem. Arcadia's report found that the median CAGR of 5.9% is "nearly double the 3% escalator still baked into many corporate budgets" — not because finance teams are negligent, but because they lack facility-level, tariff-specific rate change forecasting. The PJM capacity surge alone creates a $110,000 unbudgeted line-item exposure per MW of commercial load, invisible to any organization using a flat percentage escalator.[^19][^24]

## Section 2: IT/Security as an Enterprise Buying Gate

### The Core Dynamic

The pattern surfaced in practitioner interviews — field-level energy managers want AI-powered tools, IT/security blocks adoption even when business users are enthusiastic — is heavily documented in enterprise SaaS literature. It is not an edge case; it is the dominant dynamic in enterprise software adoption.

### Prevalence of IT-Blocked SaaS Adoption

The scale of security-driven purchase friction is striking:

- **50% of U.S. businesses** cite security as the most influential factor when buying software — the single highest-ranked factor, above functionality (49%) and ease of use (46%)[^33][^34][^35]
- **45% of U.S. businesses have stopped using a software platform** due to security concerns — demonstrating that IT gatekeeping occurs not just pre-purchase but post-deployment[^35]
- **Security teams hold veto power in 94% of software purchases** per Forrester 2024[^36]
- **94% of enterprise organizations have abandoned vendor partnerships** over data security concerns (Forrester 2024)[^36]
- **91% of enterprise clients evaluate vendors' data practices** in the initial selection phase, before any product evaluation occurs (McKinsey 2023)[^36]
- **55% of employees adopt SaaS without security's involvement** (CSA 2025) — meaning shadow IT is common, but any vendor seeking enterprise-wide adoption and billing-data access must go through formal security review[^37]

The energy/facilities management context amplifies this. Operational technology (OT) environments — including building management systems and utility meter integrations — carry elevated security sensitivity because they bridge IT and physical infrastructure. Facilities SaaS platforms require integration with utility accounts, billing portals, and increasingly with building automation systems, all of which trigger heightened security review scrutiny.

### Security Review Timelines

Enterprise IT security reviews for SaaS vendors vary significantly by company size, data sensitivity, and compliance requirements. Based on available evidence:

- **Cloud CMMS deployment** (facilities management equivalent): 3-5 days for cloud, versus 3-6 months for on-premise — illustrating how cloud SaaS dramatically compresses deployment, but security review timelines are separate from deployment timelines[^38]
- **SOC 2 Type II audit timeline**: 6-12 months to complete — vendors must complete this before large enterprise buyers will approve[^39][^40]
- **Sales cycles shortened by 3.2 months** after implementing robust data security practices (PwC 2024), implying that without certification, sales cycles are 3+ months longer on average[^36]
- **62% higher win rate** for vendors with strong data security credentials (Gartner 2023)[^36]
- Typical enterprise security review for a new SaaS vendor requiring data integration: 30-90 days for security questionnaire review; up to 180 days for formal SOC 2 audit review, penetration testing verification, and legal/procurement sign-off at large institutions

The healthcare and higher education verticals are the most demanding. Hospitals operate under HIPAA requirements and often apply healthcare-grade security scrutiny to all vendors, even those handling non-patient data. Universities increasingly impose IT security review processes modeled on state government standards, particularly for cloud platforms that interact with financial data (utility billing charges directly to departmental cost centers).

### Which Certifications Accelerate Approval

The evidence is clear on prioritization:

| Certification     | Enterprise Impact                                                                           | Cost / Timeline                                             |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- |
| SOC 2 Type II     | Most universally required; 62% higher win rate; 3.2 months faster sales (Gartner/PwC) [^36] | $20K-$80K total; 6-12 months [^39][^40]                     |
| ISO 27001         | Required by many international/global enterprise buyers                                     | Similar cost and timeline; additive to SOC 2                |
| CSA Certification | 59% of buyers more attracted to CSA-certified software [^33]                                | Lower cost; signals cloud-specific security maturity        |
| FedRAMP           | Required for government customers; not required for commercial CRE/healthcare/higher ed     | Very high cost ($1M+); only if targeting federal government |

For Clear Current Technologies targeting commercial energy managers in CRE, healthcare, and higher education, **SOC 2 Type II is the minimum required certification** before institutional enterprise procurement will approve the platform. The investment ($20K-$80K for initial certification for a startup; $35K-$130K annually ongoing) pays back quickly: companies report SOC 2 certification produces $100K+ increase in average deal size and enables 50% faster enterprise sales cycles within the first year.[^41]

### The Champion vs. Gatekeeper Strategy

The documented best practice for navigating champion/gatekeeper tension in enterprise SaaS:

1. **Arm the champion early.** Provide the energy manager/sustainability director with a security one-pager, SOC 2 report, and pre-completed security questionnaire before they raise it internally. The champion cannot advocate without collateral.
2. **Design for IT-friendly architecture.** SSO (SAML 2.0/OpenID Connect), role-based access controls, and data encryption at rest/in transit are the specific features IT evaluates first. Building these in early removes the most common objection vectors.[^42][^43]
3. **Limit data exposure scope.** Energy billing data — utility account numbers, meter IDs, cost records — is sensitive. Architecturing Clear Current to ingest only what it needs (read-only API access to utility portal data, not full account credentials) significantly reduces the security surface area IT must evaluate.
4. **Use trust marks proactively.** 76% of organizations are increasing SaaS security budgets. IT teams are actively looking for vendors who have already done the compliance work. Leading with SOC 2 Type II in all marketing and proposal materials, not waiting for it to be asked about, signals enterprise-readiness and accelerates the security review queue.[^37]

### Data Sensitivity in Commercial Energy Management

Commercial utility data — billing histories, consumption patterns, demand peaks, contracted rates, PPA terms — is competitively sensitive business information. Energy spend patterns can reveal operational schedules, production volumes, occupancy fluctuations, and strategic facility expansions. At REITs and institutional CRE firms, energy cost data is material to financial reporting and investor disclosure. At universities with federal research grants, energy allocation data intersects with grant accounting.

The sensitivity creates a genuine IT gatekeeper concern that is not irrational. However, it also means that vendors who have _already_ demonstrated data governance rigor — through SOC 2 Type II, clear data use policies, and opt-in data sharing — will find that concern becomes a competitive advantage rather than a barrier once documentation is in hand.

## Section 4: Commercial Real Estate Buyer Behavior

### Staffing and Energy Management Function in CRE

Large institutional CRE firms approach energy management through a layered structure: property-level operations (chief engineer, facilities manager) handle day-to-day utility management; portfolio-level sustainability teams (Director of Sustainability, ESG Manager) drive reporting, benchmarking, and vendor selection; and executive/investor oversight (CFO, asset manager, fund manager) sets policy and approves capital allocation.

WatchWire by Tango — the leading energy management SaaS specifically designed for institutional CRE — serves 500+ customers in 140+ countries and counts Brookfield as a named customer. Tango is Berkshire Partners-backed, signaling institutional confidence in CRE energy management as a durable software category. The GRESB 2025 Real Estate Assessment attracted 1,002 fund managers submitting 2,382 assessments — this is the global benchmark that institutional CRE firms track their energy performance against, and GRESB data management creates direct demand for energy management platforms.[^65][^66][^47][^45]

Key CRE energy software tools in use:

- **WatchWire by Tango**: Enterprise-grade energy/sustainability data management; deep GRESB reporting integration; primary tool for major REITs and institutional landlords[^65]
- **Measurabl**: Free tier launched July 2025, onboarded 11,000+ buildings in 4 weeks; targets ESG/sustainability reporting layer; premium products include real-time asset optimization and BEPS compliance[^48]
- **ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager**: Free government benchmarking tool; baseline required for LL97 compliance in NYC; ENERGY STAR future uncertain due to federal policy changes, which Measurabl specifically positioned its free tool to address[^67][^48]
- **Gridium**: Per-building pricing ($470-$760+/month) targeting commercial property managers[^44]
- **JLL/CBRE proprietary tools**: Major property management firms have built internal data management capabilities, creating a captive tool ecosystem that independent software vendors must compete against or integrate with

### CRE-Specific Pain Points

Commercial real estate energy management differs from higher education and healthcare in several important ways:

**Portfolio breadth vs. single-campus depth**: A REIT may manage thousands of buildings across dozens of markets, each with different utilities, rate structures, and lease types. The energy management challenge is less about a single complex campus and more about normalizing data across an enormously heterogeneous portfolio.

**Tenant billing complexity**: In gross lease structures, the landlord pays utilities and seeks reimbursement from tenants — requiring allocation methodologies. In NNN (triple-net) structures, tenants theoretically pay utilities directly, but shared metering in multi-tenant buildings creates allocation complexity. Over 30% of commercial landlords misinterpret cost responsibilities in commercial leases. Submetering vendor failures — including lack of transparency, missed accounting deadlines, incorrect demand charge billing, and errors stretching back years — are documented as the most common property management pain points.[^68][^69][^70]

**GRESB reporting obligations**: GRESB's 2024 Real Estate Assessment covered $10 trillion in global real estate assets; 2025 saw 1,002 fund managers participate at an all-time high. GRESB data submission deadlines in August-September, with results released in October, create a defined annual urgency spike. The 2024 GRESB standard shifted to country-level benchmarking, increasing the need for utility data accuracy at the asset level.[^66][^71][^72][^73][^74]

**ESG executive accountability**: 51% of Nareit corporate REIT members linked executive compensation to ESG performance in 2021 (up from 34% in 2020); 90% have board-level ESG oversight. This governance structure means ESG/energy data quality is a C-suite and board issue, not just an operational one — elevating the decision authority and urgency for energy management software.[^75]

### NYC Local Law 97 as the Dominant Buying Trigger

NYC Local Law 97 (LL97) represents the most powerful single regulatory catalyst for CRE energy management software adoption currently in the market:

- Applies to buildings over **25,000 gross square feet** — covering most institutional commercial real estate in NYC[^76][^77]
- Penalties: **$268 per ton** of excess carbon emissions above the cap[^78][^76]
- First compliance period: **2024-2029**; first annual report was due May 1, 2025 (with extensions to December 31, 2025)[^79][^67][^78]
- Current compliance projections: 88% of covered buildings are on track for 2024 limits, but only 35% are aligned with the tighter 2030 limits[^78]
- By 2030, approximately **13,500 of 50,000 covered properties** are projected to be out of compliance, generating cumulative fines of **$900 million annually**[^78]

LL97 is not just a compliance cost — it is a data collection mandate. Accurate annual emissions reporting requires granular utility data at the building level, certified by a registered design professional. Many NYC commercial buildings had been using ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager for voluntary benchmarking under Local Law 84; LL97 converts that data into the basis for legally enforceable penalties. Local Law 88 adds a parallel mandate: electrical sub-meters for all tenant spaces ≥5,000 sq ft — generating interval consumption data that energy management software can analyze.[^67][^78]

The combination of 65% non-compliance with 2030 targets, $900M in projected annual fines, and the May 1 annual reporting deadline creates a recurring buying trigger in Q4 and Q1 of each year as property owners assess their compliance trajectory and seek software to close the gap.

### CRE Buying Cycle and Decision Authority

The CRE buying process for energy management software follows a distinct pattern from higher education or healthcare:

**Champion**: Sustainability Director, Chief Engineer, or Energy Manager at the portfolio/platform level — not individual property managers[^75]

**Evaluators**: IT security (data integration), Finance (cost justification), Legal (data sharing agreements)

**Decision-maker**: CFO or Asset Manager; Investment Committee for multi-million-dollar commitments

**Primary triggers** (in order of frequency and urgency):

1. GRESB reporting cycle (Q3/Q4 deadline urgency)
2. LL97/BEPS compliance deadlines (annual May reporting)
3. Portfolio energy cost spikes (rate changes, capacity surcharges)
4. ESG investor pressure / net zero commitment obligations
5. Acquisition of new assets requiring integration into portfolio-wide reporting

**Procurement format**: Institutional CRE firms make **portfolio-level** purchasing decisions — they do not buy energy software property-by-property. A single software contract covers the entire portfolio, which is why contract values for REIT-level customers are substantially higher than individual building pricing would suggest. This also means the sales process is more complex but the winner-takes-portfolio dynamic creates high switching costs and durable revenue.

### Tenant Utility Billing: The Structural Error Problem

The sub-metering and tenant utility billing challenge in CRE represents a high-value, underserved problem with significant error rates:

- Submetering vendors frequently fail on five dimensions: (1) no transparency in data collection methodology, (2) missed accounting deadlines (buildings are ruled by billing cycles), (3) incorrect demand charge billing (demand charges represent 30-70% of a commercial electric bill and are frequently misunderstood), (4) opaque vendor processes creating a "black box" for property managers, and (5) billing errors that compound and "stretch back years"[^69]
- Property managers who outsource submetering have "little visibility into how the vendor operates, which makes it difficult to confirm the accuracy of their work"[^69]
- In NNN structures with single master meters and multiple tenants, landlords must pro-rate utility costs — and the methodology (square footage allocation, submetering, utility company direct billing) creates ongoing dispute potential[^70][^68]

Clear Current's AI-powered bill analysis capability directly addresses this pain point. The ability to detect billing errors retroactively, validate meter multipliers, confirm rate class accuracy, and identify demand charge calculation errors across a portfolio — with audit-ready documentation — fills a specific gap that neither WatchWire nor EnergyCAP's core bill processing addresses at the granularity CRE firms need.
