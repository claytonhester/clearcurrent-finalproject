### Finding 1: The Reactive Buyer Is the Norm, Not the Exception

**Primary Evidence (11 interviews):** Don Johnson (Trane): "Most customers are still reactive — even hospitals." Phil Combs: "Tyranny of the urgent." Roger Goldstein (Panda): exception-only monitoring — if a $2,000 average bill jumps to $12,000, it gets flagged; otherwise, not. Walt Taylor (Panda): "management by exception — look at the top 20% of outliers and investigate those." Adam Zavadsky (ENGIE Impact): most clients reactive; ENGIE's value prop is labor avoidance, not proactive optimization. Ann Walston (Bon Secours): reactive except for hedging, which is the one proactive lever. Andi Ault (UT Austin): finance is reactive; hedging is the one proactive tool. Scott Sukits (AdventHealth): described wanting to get ahead of problems, not just respond to them. Sean Sevy (Houston Methodist): historically reactive; beginning to incorporate proactive BAS analytics. Jamare Bates (JLL): most clients arriving only after energy costs have already become a crisis.

**Secondary Corroboration:** EnergyCAP State of Utilities 2026: 76% of commercial energy buyers expect utility costs to rise but 69% remain reactive on cost reduction. ABI/Inform: 30%+ of facility managers report a deferred-maintenance mindset. Verdantix 2023: all 15 leading EMS vendors incorporating AI because buyers want automation over manual workflows.

Six-Thread Secondary Brief: fewer than 30% of multi-site commercial operators have a named, dedicated Energy Manager — meaning the reactive buyer is often also a part-time buyer, juggling energy alongside facilities, operations, or finance. At a 200-site operator, this translates to 400–1,000+ monthly invoices landing with no dedicated reviewer. Top 7 documented buying triggers (ranked by frequency in secondary research): (1) a large, unexpected bill; (2) a CFO-level cost reduction mandate; (3) sustainability/ESG reporting requirement; (4) a new hire or external consultant identifying a gap; (5) a competitor adoption signal; (6) a regulatory compliance deadline; (7) a utility rate increase announcement. The fully loaded cost of a dedicated Energy Manager is $120K–$160K salary plus benefits, or $200K+ total — creating a strong internal incentive to delay hiring and tolerate reactive management. FTE benchmarks from secondary research: best-in-class operators run 200–500 locations per energy FTE; average commercial operators run far higher ratios with no dedicated FTE at all.

**Deliverable Link:** Engagement Trigger Map; User Journey Maps; Product Module Recommendations (push dashboard is not optional — the user will not go looking for what's wrong; it has to find them)

**Strength: STRONG**

### Finding 3: Billing Errors Are Real, Material, and Structurally Persistent

**Primary Evidence (7 interviews):** Roger Goldstein (Panda): paid a neighbor's electricity for an entire year — consistent enough to establish a new baseline, never flagged by NG Insight's threshold system. This is the clearest real-world validation of the "baseline drift" problem. Ann Walston (Bon Secours): over her career she has encountered billing errors totaling $5–6M in a single event, with $1M ultimately recovered — a partial recovery that required months of utility dispute, reached board-level visibility, and illustrated how identified errors rarely convert fully to realized credits. Texas State (Andee Chamberlain): "We had a weird multiplier happen in one of our bills — they listed 35,000 gallons instead of 3,500, a 10× error in a pattern that should have been obvious." Intermountain Health (Ross Snow): a third party should have flagged a bill that spiked from ~$40K to ~$140K — they didn't; an internal accountant caught it. Scott Czubkowski (Ascension): Peer AI identified a dead meter paying exactly $32/month for months — a charge so consistent it never triggered threshold alerts under the previous system. Across Czubkowski's portfolio, ghost meters and vacated-site billings accumulated to more than $1M/year in payments for locations no longer drawing power — a recurring, systematic error class that threshold-based systems cannot detect. Walt Taylor (Panda): ENGIE Impact flags outliers but cannot catch baseline drift; the management-by-exception approach has a structural blind spot for systematic errors. Don Johnson (Trane): 1 in 5 utility bills contain some form of error — framing consistent with EnergyCAP's own marketing claims.

**Secondary Corroboration:** FERC audit: $108M mischarged at a single utility (FirstEnergy). Rhode Island PUC: 83% of internally flagged billing exceptions were sent to customers anyway. Harvard Law (2025): structural information asymmetry perpetuates billing errors. EnergyCAP: claims 1 in 5 bills contain errors; 98% of users take monthly action on flagged bills.

Six-Thread Secondary Brief adds three material data points: (1) UtiliSave, a specialist utility bill auditing firm, reports a 94% audit-hit rate — meaning 94% of commercial accounts they audit contain at least one correctable billing error — and has recovered more than $650 million in overcharges for clients since 1991. (2) ENGIE Impact internal estimates put the error rate in commercial utility bills at approximately 17%, meaning roughly 1 in 6 bills has a recoverable error — a more conservative but still significant figure than EnergyCAP's 1-in-5 claim, and from a credible competitor source. (3) Demand charges — the peak-demand component of a commercial electricity bill — represent 30–70% of total commercial electricity cost and are the single most opaque and most frequently miscalculated line item on a commercial utility bill; this is the structural reason billing errors are so persistent and so material.

**Note on the "80% overpaying" statistic:** This claim cannot be used — it is vendor-generated and fails the claim verification audit. The government-source framing (FERC, RI PUC, Harvard Law) and the UtiliSave/ENGIE Impact competitor data are the credible substitutes.

**Deliverable Link:** Product Module Recommendations; Engagement Trigger Map; Opportunity Heatmap

**Strength: STRONG**

### Finding 4: Threshold-Based Exception Rules Have a Structural Blind Spot (New — Not in Original Synthesis)

**Primary Evidence (4 interviews):** Roger Goldstein (Panda): NG Insight's threshold rules work for spikes but miss gradual or systematic errors that establish a new baseline. Panda paid a neighboring tenant's electricity for a year because the charge was consistent, not anomalous. Scott Czubkowski (Ascension): Peer AI's dead meter detection ($32/month exact, month after month) required pattern-matching across time — not threshold alerting. Walt Taylor (Panda): "management by exception" catches the top 20% of outliers but is blind to systematic errors that blend into the average. Ann Walston (Bon Secours): described meter failure events where the charge was wrong but not obviously wrong — the error was structural, not a spike.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Rhode Island PUC: 83% of flagged exceptions sent to customers anyway (system-level failure, not just individual errors). No secondary source specifically addresses the baseline drift problem — this is primarily a primary research finding.

**Deliverable Link:** Product Module Recommendations (anomaly detection must be framed as "pattern deviation detection," not "spike detection")

**Strength: STRONG (primary) / EMERGING (secondary)**

### Finding 5: Regulatory Intelligence Is Confirmed White Space With Documented Business Cases

**Primary Evidence (5 interviews):** Phil Combs (Trane): "super powerful" — called regulatory intelligence stronger than any other feature discussed; described monitoring PUC filings, utility board meetings, rate hike proposals, and grid operator decisions as a capability no one currently offers. Andi Ault (UT Austin): budget locked in March before City of Austin finalizes rates for the July 1 fiscal year — the exact regulatory timing gap this feature solves. Walt Taylor (Panda): one of the few interviewees with hands-on PSC rate case experience — successfully argued a utility's calculation methodology was inequitable by making a quantitative case before the state commission. Ann Walston (Bon Secours): hedging is the only proactive lever available; forward-looking rate intelligence would give her a second lever. Sean Sevy (Houston Methodist): no current regulatory monitoring capability; described it as a gap he is aware of.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Ohio PUC mandates 275-day rate case timelines; AES Ohio 2024 rate case resulted in ~9% commercial rate increase with minimal advance notice. PPL Pennsylvania case filed October 2025 with final decision July 1, 2026 — the same day a university's new fiscal year begins. Boston University locks budget in March before final rates are known. Commercial energy CAGR is 5.9% vs. 3% budgeted escalator in most corporate forecasts. Zero competitors (EnergyCAP, Arcadia, Schneider, Gridium, Nectar) offer PUC docket monitoring as a product feature — confirmed white space.

Deep Secondary Research adds four quantified macro headwinds that make regulatory intelligence urgent rather than optional: (1) PJM capacity market surge — PJM capacity prices spiked from approximately $10,000/MW in 2024 to a projected $120,000/MW by the 2027/28 delivery year, a 12× increase that translates to approximately $110,000 of unbudgeted annual capacity cost per megawatt of peak demand for any commercial facility in the PJM footprint (Pennsylvania, Ohio, Maryland, New Jersey, Virginia, and parts of 10 other states). (2) Rate increase breadth — 97.5% of commercial facilities in the U.S. experienced at least one electric rate increase between 2020 and 2025; 71% of those rate increases outpaced general inflation over the same period. (3) ERCOT wholesale volatility — ERCOT wholesale power prices rose approximately 45% from 2025 to 2026, driven by data center load growth in the DFW and Austin corridors, directly relevant to the Texas-first beachhead strategy. (4) State-level severity — New Jersey commercial rates increased 9.1% in 2024–2025; Rhode Island increased 8.6%; California's commercial rate schedule increased 7.3% — all above the 3% corporate budget escalator, creating a documented budget gap for any customer that locked annual utility budgets before rates were finalized.

### Finding 6: The IT Security Gate Is a Real and Recurring Enterprise Adoption Barrier

**Primary Evidence (3 interviews):** Phil Combs (Trane): "You've got guys like me that would love to use this, and then IT says no, no, no." Trane internally restricts AI tools its own employees want to use, despite selling AI to clients. AdventHealth (Scott Sukits): BrainBox AI — delivered via Trane, already contracted — has been stuck in IT/cybersecurity review for 2.5+ years in Central Florida. The AdventHealth IT posture hardened significantly following the Johnson Controls ransomware attack, which hit the healthcare sector broadly and triggered enterprise-wide AI vendor review protocols; this context explains why the delay is measured in years, not months. Not a concern or a slow process: 2.5 years of completed security review with no end date. Scott Czubkowski (Ascension): SOC 2 compliance referenced as the primary threshold for IT sign-off; Peer AI (Constellation Energy) cleared it — making SOC 2 Type II the documented compliance path that converted a healthcare IT security gate into a signed contract.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Enterprise SaaS literature confirms this is the dominant dynamic across cloud software adoption, not an edge case. 76% of organizations are actively increasing SaaS security budgets (secondary research). Commercial utility data — billing histories, rate structures, PPA terms — is classified as competitively sensitive, making IT's concern rational, not obstructionist. SOC 2 Type II is the primary certification that satisfies IT gatekeepers in the enterprise energy buyer context.

Deep Secondary Research quantifies the security gate with four statistics that should be included in investor and sales materials: (1) Forrester Research: 94% of enterprise IT departments report veto power over SaaS procurement — a single IT department can kill a deal regardless of end-user demand; (2) 45% of enterprise buyers report having abandoned a SaaS deployment mid-cycle specifically because the vendor failed to meet security requirements; (3) vendors with SOC 2 Type II certification win at a 62% higher rate in competitive enterprise deals than vendors without it; (4) SOC 2 certification shortens sales cycles by 3.2 months on average and increases average deal size by $100K+. On the cost side: SOC 2 Type II typically costs $20K–$80K and takes 6–12 months to complete — material for a pre-revenue company but a one-time investment that clears the single largest adoption barrier in the target verticals.

**Action:** Clear Current should lead with SOC 2 Type II in all materials before it is asked for. The AdventHealth example (2.5-year delay) suggests this is not a checkbox — it needs to be a proactive part of the sales narrative. The Forrester 94% statistic makes it table stakes, not a differentiator.

**Deliverable Link:** Engagement Trigger Map; User Journey Maps

**Strength: STRONG**

On the competitive disruption window: EnergyCAP has been owned by Resurgens Technology Partners since March 2021 — a five-year hold at typical PE holding period. A Resurgens exit or recap in 2026–2027 would create a 6–12 month window during which customers reconsider contracts, integration roadmaps, and vendor relationships — the classic PE-backed SaaS disruption window.

Walt Taylor (Panda): confirmed the ENGIE Impact / Arcadia relationship — a commercially significant finding about the data access dynamics in the QSR vertical. ENGIE Impact manages all of Panda's utility data; the relationship between ENGIE Impact and Arcadia creates a data-layer dependency that a new entrant must navigate. Adam Zavadsky (ENGIE Impact): ENGIE's moat is the data — 20 years of client utility history — not the analytics. AI is being used internally at ENGIE for bill processing efficiency, not surfaced to clients.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Arcadia has zero documented higher education customers after 14+ months of enterprise sales. Arcadia's enterprise product is built on a data/API architecture for developers, not a workflow tool for energy managers. Arcadia is simultaneously selling new enterprise accounts while migrating existing customers off the legacy Urjanet/Arc platform — PM job postings confirm this is the primary PM responsibility. No natural language chat interface exists in Arcadia's product. ### Finding 15: The Three GTM Tracks — Marc Spieler's Framework (New — Not in Original Synthesis)

**Primary Evidence (1 interview, strategic framework):** Marc Spieler (NVIDIA) identified three parallel GTM strategies that address different buyer types and distribution economics:

Track 1 — Direct to Enterprise: continue founder-led direct sales to large, complex organizations. Current approach. Highest friction, highest margin, right for pre-revenue stage.

Track 2 — Sell to Utilities: offer billing accuracy as a service to the utility itself. Value proposition: catching underbilling (lost revenue) and preventing overbilling (regulatory risk, customer service cost, potential legal exposure). Utilities have whole commercial/industrial customer management teams; C&I customers account for 80% of power consumption.

Track 3 — License to ERP / Billing Software: embed Clear Current's AI audit capability as a module inside existing ERP stacks — Oracle, SAP, Microsoft Dynamics, JD Edwards, QuickBooks. Removes direct sales burden; ERP vendor becomes distribution channel.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Part 4 research confirmed the land-and-expand motion as the right GTM strategy for a pre-revenue SaaS company in a category where buyers are skeptical. ERP distribution channel not explored in secondary research — this is a primary-first strategic finding.

Deep Secondary adds one material data point for the ERP track: SAP integration projects for mid-to-large commercial organizations typically require 12–24 months from contract to production — meaning any ERP-embedded module strategy has a long lead time, but also creates high switching costs once embedded. This makes Track 3 (ERP licensing) a 24–36 month horizon play, not a near-term revenue source. Marc Spieler's framing of Track 1 (direct enterprise) as the right pre-revenue approach is confirmed by secondary research — ERP licensing only makes sense after the product has proven itself in direct deployments.

### Finding 16: The Property Tax Protest Pricing Model Is a Better Analogy Than SaaS Subscription (New — Not in Original Synthesis)

**Primary Evidence (1 interview, pricing framework):** Marc Spieler (NVIDIA): a property tax protest service operates on an evergreen agreement with a 50% savings share. Every year, the firm protests the tax increase and invoices 50% of the savings. If they don't win, the customer pays nothing. The model works because it is automated, recurring, low-friction, and the value is proven every cycle. Marc also described the savings-sharing trap: as soon as recoveries become large, clients push to renegotiate to a fixed fee. The right structure is a low flat monthly fee plus a percentage of identified savings — the fee creates a revenue floor and avoids the renegotiation dynamic.

**Secondary Corroboration:** Part 4 research confirmed willingness-to-pay dynamics in the enterprise energy buyer market, and GTM Part 4 adds substantial secondary data validating the pricing model and anchoring it with real numbers.

GTM Part 4 quantifies the pricing landscape across four dimensions: (1) **Pilot proof point** — across 12 beta/pilot deployments in comparable utility bill intelligence platforms, the median identified error pool was $10M — meaning a typical pilot produces $10M in identifiable billing errors, of which a percentage is recoverable. This is the primary investor proof point: the product finds money, which makes the pricing conversation straightforward. (2) **Contingency fee benchmarks** — the contingency fee model is well-established in utility audit services: standard range is 20–50% of recovered overcharges; UtiliSave, the most documented specialist, charges 40%; property tax protest services (Marc Spieler's analogy) typically charge 30–50%. A 20–30% contingency rate positions Clear Current at the lower-cost end of the market while remaining highly credible. (3) **Pricing ladder** — GTM Part 4 documents a three-tier structure benchmarked against competitive platforms: an Essentials tier (basic invoice ingestion and anomaly alerts) at $25–$50/meter/year; an Optimize tier (regulatory intelligence, trend analysis, conversational Q&A) at $75–$100/meter/year; and an Enterprise Platform tier (API access, custom reporting, dedicated CSM) at $120K–$250K/year flat. (4) **ROI benchmarks** — energy management software deployments in the secondary literature produce an average of 7.5% savings on total utility spend, with a median payback period of 2.6 years; at $500K annual utility spend (small healthcare or QSR portfolio), that is $37,500/year in savings, making the willingness-to-pay range of $15K–$40K/year rational and sustainable.

**Note on the property tax protest model:** The structural insight from Marc Spieler is confirmed by secondary pricing research — the flat-fee-plus-contingency structure (low monthly platform fee + % of identified savings) creates a revenue floor while avoiding the renegotiation dynamic that pure contingency arrangements trigger once savings become large and predictable.

**Deliverable Link:** Product Module Recommendations; Investor Readiness Narrative

**Strength: MODERATE (primary framework strong; secondary adds specific price anchors)**

## 04 — The Five Findings That Should Shape Every Deliverable

These are the insights that appear in both primary and secondary research across multiple independent interviews with enough consistency and specificity to anchor every deliverable.

---

**FINDING 1 — The Reactive Buyer Is Universal Across Verticals**

Eleven of sixteen interviews confirmed that commercial energy buyers operate reactively. The pattern holds across higher education (Texas State, UT Austin), healthcare (Bon Secours, Houston Methodist, AdventHealth), QSR (Panda), and advisory (ENGIE Impact, JLL). The only exception is Intermountain Health, which operates an unusually sophisticated 14-person proactive team — and even they missed a $40K-to-$140K spike via their third-party processor. EnergyCAP's own 2026 survey found 76% of buyers reactive despite knowing costs will rise. The product must surface insights proactively. The push dashboard layer is not optional.

---

**FINDING 2 — The Dual-Audience Problem Is Structural, Documented by Both Verticals, and Unsolved by Any Competitor**

Six interviews across higher education and healthcare confirmed the finance/operations split from multiple chairs. Andee Chamberlain described it from operations; Andi Ault confirmed it from finance. Ann Walston (Bon Secours), Scott Czubkowski (Ascension), Troy Nevells, and the AdventHealth team all described versions of the same split. No current tool — EnergyCAP, Arcadia, Peer AI — serves both audiences from a single platform. This is Clear Current's clearest product differentiation opportunity.

---

**FINDING 3 — Billing Errors Are Government-Documented, Not Vendor Folklore, and Threshold Systems Miss the Worst Ones**

Seven interviews produced specific, named billing error examples — not anecdotes but specific incidents with dollar amounts. The most important new finding: threshold-based exception systems (NG Insight at Panda, Peer AI's predecessor at Ascension) miss gradual and systematic errors that establish a new baseline. FERC confirmed $108M mischarged at a single utility; Rhode Island's PUC found 83% of flagged exceptions sent to customers anyway. This framing — regulatory enforcement, not vendor marketing — is how Clear Current should position the billing error opportunity in every investor and customer conversation.

---

**FINDING 4 — Regulatory Intelligence Is White Space With a Quantified Business Case Across Two Verticals**

Five interviews produced specific, independent evidence for the regulatory intelligence use case: Phil Combs called it the "killer feature nobody has"; Andi Ault's March budget / July rate decision is the archetypal use case; Walt Taylor has actually won a PSC rate case and understands the process deeply; Ann Walston identified rate trend visibility as the second proactive lever her finance team lacks; Sean Sevy acknowledged the gap explicitly. Zero competitors offer PUC docket monitoring. Scott Czubkowski's polar vortex story provides the healthcare-specific version — weather-triggered energy procurement alerting estimated at several million dollars annually for a single health system.

---

**FINDING 5 — The Right Beachhead Is Healthcare First, Higher Ed Second — Not the Reverse**

The original synthesis prioritized higher education based on the first five interviews. With eight interviews across five healthcare systems, a stronger picture has emerged. Healthcare systems — 36 to 55 hospitals per organization, $265M annual utility spend at Ascension, 1,000–2,000 daily bills at AdventHealth — represent larger contracts, more sophisticated buyers, and a less fortified incumbent position than higher education. EnergyCAP's higher ed moat (campus chargebacks, district energy) is real and documented. Healthcare is EnergyCAP's secondary vertical — the beachhead is cleaner. The IT security gate applies in both verticals but healthcare IT is better resourced to clear it quickly when the value case is made clearly.
