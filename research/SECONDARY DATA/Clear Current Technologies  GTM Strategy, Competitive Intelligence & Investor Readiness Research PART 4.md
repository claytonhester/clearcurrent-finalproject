## Part 1: GTM Motion — Founder-Led Direct Enterprise Sales

### The "Land with Audit, Expand to Platform" Model

Clear Current's current motion — founder-led direct enterprise sales targeting billion-dollar-plus organizations with 50+ locations — is the highest-friction but highest-margin path available. It is also the right path for a pre-revenue SaaS company in a category where buyers are skeptical, switching costs are high, and the value proposition requires demonstration, not description.

The pilot-to-paid conversion dynamic is well-studied in enterprise SaaS. According to SaaStr's dataset across thousands of B2B companies, pilot to annual contract conversion rates run 60–90%, with 70%+ achievable at companies that have refined their sales motion. Clear Current's 12 pilots represent a pipeline where the expected conversion, even at the low end, produces 7–8 paying customers — enough to demonstrate repeatable sales at the target $10K/month price point.[^4]

The revenue recognition problem identified by John is material and standard in enterprise SaaS. A $100K+ deal typically requires 6–18 months from first contact to close, and complex procurement at universities or large commercial portfolios extends toward the 12–18 month end. The five-month revenue recognition lag that John observed (deals signed in January not generating revenue until June) is consistent with enterprise SaaS norms, not an anomaly. The financial model needs to reflect this reality explicitly before going into fundraising conversations.[^5]

### The Three GTM Tracks — Prioritized

**Track 1 (Active): Direct Enterprise** is the right initial motion. The proof is simple: $10M in identified errors across 12 pilots creates real urgency for the buyers who have already seen the platform. Converting those pilots to paid contracts before May is the single most important near-term goal, because it gives investors a revenue number, not just a pipeline number.

**Track 2 (Proposed): ERP Licensing (SAP/Oracle/QuickBooks)** is strategically compelling but operationally distant. The average SAP integration partnership timeline is 12–24 months from initiation to live customers, and the go-to-market dependency on a large ERP vendor's sales team creates control problems for an early-stage company. This track is worth pursuing in parallel conversations but should not be counted in the May fundraise narrative.

**Track 3 (Proposed): Utilities as Customers** is the least understood and potentially most scalable of the three tracks. Utilities have regulatory and reputational incentives to bill correctly in both directions. FERC oversight and state PUC proceedings document real consequences for systematic billing errors. A utility that proactively deploys Clear Current's audit logic to catch its own errors before customers complain would be positioning itself favorably ahead of regulatory scrutiny. This track deserves a dedicated exploratory conversation with one or two utility partners in deregulated Texas, where ERCOT's competitive structure creates the most billing complexity.[^6][^7]

## Part 2: Beachhead Vertical — Higher Education

### Why Higher Ed First

The case for higher education as the primary beachhead is not just data-supported — it is structurally compelling. Three forces converge:

1. **EnergyCAP is most entrenched here, and also most vulnerable here.** Higher ed represents 19% of EnergyCAP's customer base, and the campus chargeback capability that makes EnergyCAP irreplaceable is simultaneously the feature users complain about most — described in user reviews as "hard to understand," lacking documentation, and requiring extensive training. This is the classic incumbent trap: the feature that keeps customers stuck is the feature users hate most.[^8][^9]

2. **District energy complexity creates problems no AI-native competitor has solved.** Of 660+ U.S. district energy systems, the vast majority serve university and hospital campuses. The cost allocation formula problem — distributing central plant steam/chilled water costs across 50–150 metered buildings — requires a combination of utility bill intelligence, tariff logic, and financial system integration that neither Arcadia nor Verse nor Gridium offers.[^10]

3. **Primary research confirms active pain.** Andee Chamberlain at Texas State (1,500 meters, 98 buildings) and Andi Ault at UT Austin both confirmed EnergyCAP as incumbent and both described specific unresolved problems: rate anomaly detection gaps, financial system integration failures, and budget timing mismatches. These are not hypothetical problems.

### The University of Wisconsin RFP as a Baseline

The University of Wisconsin System issued RFP 2025-UW-00732-RFP in September 2024, seeking energy management software for an estimated $500,000–$2,000,000 contract value. The stated requirements are an exact match for Clear Current's positioning: real-time data analytics, reporting capabilities, integration with existing systems, and user-friendly interfaces across multiple campuses.[^11][^12]

This single RFP establishes three important facts for Clear Current's investor narrative: (1) public universities issue formal RFPs for energy management software; (2) contract values at the system level run $500K–$2M — significantly above Clear Current's current $120K/year pricing; (3) user-friendly interface is an explicit evaluation criterion — directly addressed by Clear Current's AI-native design and the shortcoming most consistently cited against EnergyCAP.

### The Texas State Displacement Case

Texas State University contracted EnergyCAP through the YearOut Energy reseller, piggybacking on the UTSA EnergyCAP agreement starting in August 2012. Texas State's public purchasing records show EnergyCAP software spend of approximately $17,900 per purchase order in late 2024, suggesting an annual spend in the range of $50,000–$75,000 for their 1,500-meter installation. This is a documented incumbent relationship where Clear Current has already conducted primary research conversations with the energy management team — and where EnergyCAP's reported UX weaknesses are most likely to be confirmed.[^13][^14][^15]

The displacement thesis for Clear Current at Texas State is: demonstrate $10M in billing errors across similar-scale portfolios → propose a parallel deployment that handles billing audit and rate optimization → prove value in 90 days → displace EnergyCAP on chargeback once that module ships. This is a 12–18 month enterprise displacement cycle, not a 30-day close, but it is a realistic sequence.

### The University of New Mexico Cost Benchmark

University of New Mexico's EnergyCAP cost on record is $9,600/year via its Lobo Energy reseller agreement. For a campus of UNM's scale, this implies pricing at approximately $15–20/meter/year through a reseller channel — dramatically below Clear Current's $120K/year target. This gap reveals both an opportunity and a challenge: Clear Current must justify 5–10x the per-meter cost of the incumbent by demonstrating proportionally greater value in error recovery and rate optimization. The $10M in identified errors across 12 pilots is exactly the proof point needed to justify that premium.[^16]

### Healthcare as Second Vertical

The structural parallels between university and hospital energy management make healthcare the natural second vertical. The specific overlaps are: district energy systems (more than 20% of hospital buildings use district heating systems); complex cost allocation across patient care, research, and administrative functions; compliance reporting requirements (Joint Commission, state health department); and high energy intensity (hospitals consume 2.5x more energy per square foot than office buildings). Both Andee Chamberlain (Texas State) and Don Johnson independently named hospitals as the closest analogue to university energy management complexity — peer validation from the field.[^17][^18]

## Part 3: Deregulated Energy Market Geography

### The 18-State Opportunity Map

As of 2025, 18 states plus Washington D.C. have some form of retail electricity choice for commercial customers. The degree of deregulation — and therefore the degree of billing complexity — varies significantly:[^19]

| State         | Status                    | Key Feature                                     | Relevance to Clear Current                                |
| ------------- | ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Texas         | Fully deregulated (ERCOT) | 100+ REPs, mandatory choice, 87% switching[^19] | Highest billing error exposure; Clear Current's home base |
| Pennsylvania  | Deregulated since 1997    | Dozens of suppliers via PA Power Switch[^20]    | Large commercial real estate + university market          |
| Ohio          | Deregulated since 2001    | PUCO-certified CRES providers[^20]              | Strong municipal aggregation                              |
| Illinois      | Deregulated since 1997    | ComEd + Ameren territories[^21]                 | Major metro commercial RE                                 |
| New York      | Deregulated since 1996    | Choice with utility-owned delivery[^21]         | LL97 compliance driving software demand                   |
| New Jersey    | Deregulated               | Full residential + commercial                   | Dense commercial market                                   |
| Maryland      | Deregulated               | Active competitive market                       | Mid-Atlantic CRE                                          |
| Massachusetts | Deregulated since 1998    | Full competition[^21]                           | University-heavy (Boston)                                 |
| California    | Partial                   | Large C&I only[^21]                             | Tech campus and healthcare                                |

The strategic implication for Clear Current's sales territory planning is clear: **deregulated states produce more billing errors** (from supplier contract disputes, overlapping billing cycles during switches, and complex pass-through charges) **and more savings opportunities** (from competitive rate negotiation). The initial sales territory should prioritize Texas (home base, 100+ REPs), Pennsylvania (large university + commercial RE market), and New York (LL97 compliance urgency).

### ERCOT Billing Complexity as a Moat

Texas ERCOT is structurally unique: there is no default utility service, all customers must actively choose a provider, and over 100 retail electricity providers compete. This produces billing complexity that non-deregulated markets do not see: overlapping billing cycles when customers switch REPs, TDSP (transmission/distribution) charges from the wires utility sitting alongside REP supply charges, ERCOT wholesale market price exposure for commercial customers without fixed-price contracts, and frequent discrepancies between REP-invoiced amounts and ERCOT settlement data. Clear Current's home-court advantage in Texas is a genuine competitive moat that is difficult for a Boston- or DC-headquartered competitor to replicate quickly.[^20][^19]

## Part 4: Competitive Intelligence — Updated

### EnergyCAP — The Incumbent in Detail

EnergyCAP's institutional history defines both its strength and its vulnerability. Founded in the 1980s and acquired by Resurgens Technology Partners in March 2021 (when the company had approximately 2,100 customers), it has grown to 10,000+ users by 2026 through a combination of organic growth and add-on acquisitions including Wattics (August 2022, interval analytics). Resurgens' investment thesis was straightforward: deepen sales, expand marketing, pursue add-ons. The customer growth from 2,100 to 10,000 is real, but the underlying platform architecture reflects its origins — a utility bill accounting system that has been extended, not rebuilt.[^22][^23][^24][^25][^26]

The Wattics acquisition is particularly instructive for Clear Current's positioning. EnergyCAP acquired Wattics specifically because its own platform lacked interval analytics capabilities — building-level real-time monitoring that customers were demanding. This is not a weakness EnergyCAP fixed organically; it is a weakness they papered over with a tuck-in acquisition. Clear Current should expect that if campus chargeback AI becomes a visible market requirement, EnergyCAP will try to acquire its way to that capability rather than build it.[^26]

**EnergyCAP Pricing Reference Points:**

- Published pricing: $40–$52/meter/year (Express and Enterprise tiers)[^27]
- Texas State actual spend: ~$50,000–$75,000/year for ~1,500 meters (via reseller)[^15]
- UNM actual spend: $9,600/year via Lobo Energy reseller (small campus)[^16]
- Implementation: $5,000–$100,000+ depending on complexity[^28]

The reseller channel (YearOut Energy, Lobo Energy, and others) creates a pricing opacity that benefits EnergyCAP in customer retention but creates an opportunity for Clear Current to compete on value transparency.

Its acquisition by Tango (a lease management and real estate operations platform) repositions it as an integrated real estate operations tool rather than a pure energy play. ### Brightly Software — The Siemens Asset Management Play

Siemens acquired Brightly Software in June 2022 for $1.575 billion at approximately 10x its $160M ARR. Brightly serves 12,000 customers in education, healthcare, and manufacturing — directly overlapping Clear Current's beachhead verticals — with asset lifecycle management, CMMS, and maintenance management. Understanding this dual-incumbent landscape is important for deal qualification.[^36][^37][^38][^39]

### The ENERGY STAR Disruption — Risk and Opportunity

The Trump administration's FY2026 budget proposal allocates $0 to the Atmospheric Protection Program, which houses ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager. The program serves 330,000+ buildings and is used by state and local governments to enforce building performance standards including NYC Local Law 97. As of August 2025, a bipartisan House committee approved $32M in funding and the Senate approved $36M, and legal analysis indicates ENERGY STAR cannot be eliminated without an act of Congress.[^40][^41][^2][^1]

For Clear Current, this regulatory situation creates a two-scenario analysis:

**Scenario A (most likely): ENERGY STAR survives with reduced budget.** The 330,000 buildings already in Portfolio Manager continue benchmarking there. Clear Current remains additive — billing audit and rate optimization layer that Portfolio Manager cannot provide.

**Scenario B (tail risk): Portfolio Manager is substantially disrupted or discontinued.** This is a major catalyst event for the energy management software market. 330,000 buildings need an alternative infrastructure for energy data management, benchmarking, and compliance reporting. Any company with pre-existing utility data connectivity and energy management capabilities — including Clear Current — stands to benefit significantly. This scenario also accelerates the buyer urgency that is currently a growth constraint.

## Part 5: Pricing Strategy

### Contingency Fee Benchmarks

The utility bill audit industry's standard contingency model is well-documented: UtiliSave charges 40% of savings recovered for the first 36 months, then 0%. Other firms in the sector charge 20%–50% depending on deal size and recovery difficulty. This model has the advantage of zero perceived risk for the buyer and creates asymmetric upside for the auditor — but it creates the exact problem Marc Spieler identified: as discovered savings scale, the percentage becomes uncomfortable.[^42][^43][^44][^45]

The property tax protest model Marc cited is directionally instructive but not a precise analogue. The better comparable is the utility bill audit firm that charges 40% of recovered refunds — but caps the engagement at 18–36 months and transitions the relationship to subscription. This is the transition Clear Current needs to engineer deliberately: use contingency pricing to prove value on the first engagement (billing error recovery), then migrate to subscription pricing for ongoing rate optimization, tariff monitoring, and procurement advisory.

### The Per-Meter Pricing Ladder

EnergyCAP's $40–$52/meter/year pricing creates a ceiling against which Clear Current can position. The strategic pricing options are:[^27]

| Tier                  | Positioning                 | Price Point                | Target Customer               |
| --------------------- | --------------------------- | -------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Audit (Entry)         | Contingency on errors found | 20–30% of recovered amount | Any 50+ location org          |
| Optimize (Core SaaS)  | Per-meter subscription      | $75–$100/meter/year        | EnergyCAP upgraders           |
| Platform (Enterprise) | Flat enterprise license     | $120K–$250K/year           | University systems, large CRE |

The $120K/year anchor that John is currently targeting is directionally appropriate for an organization with 130 locations — that implies approximately $923/location/year or (at an average of 5–10 meters per location) $90–$185/meter/year. This is a 2–4x premium over EnergyCAP's public pricing. The premium is defensible on error recovery ROI, but it must be proven in the first 90 days of each deployment.

The UW System RFP's estimated $500K–$2M contract value signals that system-level university contracts can support significantly larger deal sizes than Clear Current's current anchor pricing. A University of Texas System (16 institutions, Austin flagship alone has 1,000+ meters) or California State University System (23 campuses) level deal could represent $500K–$1M+ ARR from a single procurement event.[^12]

### Pilot-to-Paid Conversion and the "Realized vs. Identified" Problem

Marc Spieler's concern that identified errors ≠ recovered refunds is the most important operational risk in Clear Current's current go-to-market. In enterprise energy management, the error identification step (which Clear Current's platform can do today) and the error recovery step (which requires the utility to issue a credit or refund) are separated by a 30–180 day process that involves dispute filing, utility investigation, and credit issuance. Some errors are recovered in full; some are recovered partially; some utilities deny the claim entirely.

This creates a credibility gap that investors will probe in diligence: "How much of the $10M in identified errors has actually been recovered as cash?" Clear Current needs to track and report this metric explicitly. The SaaStr benchmark of 70%+ pilot-to-paid conversion assumes the pilot has demonstrated clear, realized value — not identified but unrealized value.[^4]

## Part 8: Green Button Data Access — Product Implications

### What Green Button Connect Is and Is Not

Green Button Connect My Data (GBCMD) is the federal standard for automated utility data API access, enabling customers to authorize third-party access to their meter data. It was launched in 2012, and the DOE committed 50+ utilities to the initiative covering 60M+ homes and businesses. The UtilityAPI platform provides a commercial implementation that aggregates Green Button Connect access across multiple utilities.[^52][^53][^54]

For Clear Current's product architecture, Green Button Connect is the ideal data ingestion pathway for utilities that support it — it eliminates manual PDF upload and enables automated, real-time data feeds. The problem is coverage gaps: Green Button Connect is strongest for residential accounts; commercial and industrial account support varies widely by utility. Oregon, for example, supports only Green Button Download My Data (file download) rather than the API-based Connect My Data standard for commercial accounts. Many smaller municipal utilities and rural cooperatives do not support Green Button at all.[^55]

### New York's IEDR as a Model for What's Coming

New York State is building the Integrated Energy Data Resource (IEDR) — a centralized statewide energy data platform that includes Green Button Connect access for all New York investor-owned utilities. Phase I was completed March 2024; Phase II targeting July 2026 will expand Green Button Connect to all nine NY IOUs and add 40+ use cases. UtilityAPI (a Green Button Alliance sponsor) is building Phase II.[^56]

This is the policy trajectory that Clear Current should monitor: states requiring their IOUs to provide standardized API data access as a condition of AMI deployment. As this spreads from New York to other states (Connecticut, Massachusetts, Maine, and New Hampshire are all implementing or considering similar frameworks), Clear Current's data ingestion layer becomes dramatically easier to build and maintain. Until then, the manual PDF scraping / OCR workflow remains necessary for a significant share of commercial accounts.[^56]

## Part 9: Regulatory Tailwinds — Building Performance Standards

### NYC Local Law 97 — Active Compliance Urgency

Local Law 97 of 2019 requires buildings over 25,000 square feet to meet GHG emissions limits beginning in 2024, with an annual carbon emissions report due May 1 of each year. The first report covering 2024 data was due May 1, 2025. Non-compliance fines are $268 per metric ton of CO₂e above the building's limit. The 2030 compliance period brings significantly more stringent limits.[^57][^58][^59]

Local Law 88 requires lighting upgrades and sub-metering installation in spaces over 25,000 sq ft, with sub-meter data feeding directly into energy management workflows. Local Law 84 requires annual energy benchmarking (historically via ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager). Together, these three laws create a compliance infrastructure for New York commercial building owners that requires continuous energy data management — precisely the category Clear Current serves.[^57]

The NYC market is currently prioritizing ESG/compliance-oriented platforms (IBM Envizi, Schneider Resource Advisor+, Measurabl) over billing audit tools. But LL97 fines are directly tied to energy intensity metrics that are affected by utility billing accuracy — a building that has been overbilled on energy may appear to be consuming more energy than it actually is, creating a misleading compliance picture. This connection between billing accuracy and compliance accuracy is an underexplored sales narrative for Clear Current in New York.

### The Cascade of Building Performance Standards Beyond NYC

NYC is the most aggressive jurisdiction, but state-level building performance standard (BPS) adoption is expanding:

- **California:** AB 2446 (2022) requires large commercial buildings to benchmark and report; SB 100 drives the overall clean energy mandate[^1]
- **Washington state:** Building performance standards under HB 1257 cover commercial buildings over 50,000 sq ft
- **Colorado:** Senate Bill 22-246 establishes commercial building benchmarking and performance standards
- **Illinois:** Chicago Climate Action Plan and state-level benchmarking requirements

Each jurisdiction that mandates benchmarking creates recurring software adoption demand. The market dynamics parallel the GDPR/data privacy software wave: a compliance deadline forces tool adoption, and the resulting data infrastructure becomes sticky.

## Part 10: Strategic Synthesis — Clear Current's Defensible Position

### The "Why Clear Current vs. Arcadia" Answer

The competitive differentiation question that investors will ask first and buyers will ask second is: why not just use Arcadia? The answer has three parts:

**1. Arcadia's product is built for corporate sustainability teams, not energy managers.** Arcadia's three enterprise products (Utility Bill Management, Energy Procurement Advisory, Supply Management) are oriented toward Scope 1–3 emissions tracking and renewable energy procurement — the priorities of a VP of Sustainability at a Fortune 500 company. Clear Current's design starts with the energy manager who receives 500 bills per month and needs to know which ones are wrong before approving payment. These are different users with different workflows.

**2. Arcadia has no campus chargeback.** University and hospital campus cost allocation — the distribution of central utility costs across 50–150 metered entities including departments, schools, grants, auxiliaries, and clinical programs — is the most technically demanding feature in the higher education energy management category. EnergyCAP's chargeback module is the reason universities don't switch platforms despite its UX weaknesses. Arcadia has built zero documented capability here. This is Clear Current's highest-value beachhead feature to build.

**3. Texas ERCOT expertise is a genuine moat.** Arcadia was built and is headquartered in Washington D.C., with a residential community solar orientation. Its commercial enterprise products were acquired from Atlanta-based Urjanet in 2022. Neither of these entities has deep ERCOT market expertise. The ERCOT deregulated market — with 100+ competing REPs, mandatory customer choice, TDSP pass-through complexity, and frequent billing disputes during customer switches — is where Clear Current's founders operate and where the most billing errors are concentrated.[^19]

### The Fundraise Story in One Paragraph

Clear Current Technologies is the only AI-native energy management platform that combines utility billing error recovery, tariff optimization, and campus cost allocation in a single workflow. The company has identified $10M in billing errors across 12 pilot programs with organizations ranging from single-campus universities to a single enterprise with 130 locations. Billing errors affect an estimated 30% of commercial accounts annually, yet the dominant incumbent (EnergyCAP, backed by Resurgens since 2021) does not have AI-native error detection and its most critical capability — campus chargeback — is also its most complained-about feature across 132 user reviews. The beachhead is U.S. universities, a $8–11 billion annual energy spend segment where 660+ district energy systems create billing complexity that no AI-native competitor addresses. The Texas ERCOT deregulated market provides home-court advantage in the most billing-complex energy market in the country. Series A proceeds will fund the first sales team hire, the campus chargeback module build, and the conversion of current pilots to the first $1M in ARR.
