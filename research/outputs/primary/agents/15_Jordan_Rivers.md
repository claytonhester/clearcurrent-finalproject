## 2. KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: The Incumbent Runs on Someone Else's Data

**Source:** Walt Taylor (Panda) + Adam Zavadsky (ENGIE Impact) + Arcadia's own technical documentation

ENGIE Impact — the dominant outsourced utility bill processor in multi-site commercial energy management — uses Arcadia to aggregate electronic utility bill data. This was confirmed on the record by Walt Taylor, who manages energy across 2,300 Panda locations and works directly with ENGIE, and independently corroborated by Arcadia's own developer documentation, which lists ENGIE Insight as a known indirect data source with a specific integration pathway. This means ENGIE's analytical ceiling is Arcadia's data quality ceiling. ENGIE's proprietary moat — which Adam Zavadsky explicitly described as the data — is partially resting on a third-party foundation. For paper bills (estimated at a substantial fraction of bills from ~30,000 U.S. municipalities with no digital infrastructure), ENGIE still relies on OCR and manual keying. Walt confirmed OCR "doesn't work 100% of the time" and that "catching 90% of errors is the realistic target."

**Why this matters for investors:** This is the structural vulnerability in the incumbent's position. The analytical gap between ENGIE's bill processing function and what clients actually need is not protected by proprietary data infrastructure — it's protected by switching costs and inertia. A platform that integrates directly with Arcadia's data layer while outperforming OCR on paper bills and layering genuine AI analytics on top has a credible technical path to displacement without needing to rebuild ENGIE's 20-year data collection operation from scratch.

### Finding 2: Threshold-Based Systems Have a Known, Documented Failure Mode

**Source:** Roger Goldstein (Panda) + Adam Zavadsky (ENGIE Impact) + Intermountain Health (Ross Snow)

Three independent sources — an enterprise buyer, the incumbent vendor, and the most sophisticated internal energy team in the research corpus — all confirmed the same structural limitation of current exception monitoring systems: they catch spikes, but they miss baseline drift. Roger Goldstein's most vivid example is the clearest: a single meter was found to be covering two outlets in a shared tenant space. For an entire year, Panda paid the neighbor's electricity. ENGIE's threshold system never flagged it because the charges had been consistent enough to establish a new baseline. Adam Zavadsky acknowledged the limitation directly: "We flag the anomaly, but cannot diagnose why it happened." At Intermountain — where SkySpark monitors 14 billion data points from building systems — a $40,000-to-$140,000 utility bill spike was missed by the third-party processor and caught only by an internal accountant.

**Why this matters for investors:** This is not an anecdote — it is a confirmed architectural failure in every existing solution. The failure mode has been validated by the incumbent (ENGIE), by a sophisticated enterprise buyer (Panda), and by the most well-staffed internal energy team in the research corpus (Intermountain). Pattern deviation detection — not just spike alerts — is a specific, demonstrable technical capability that no current market participant delivers. This is the product differentiation that can be quantified and defended.

### Finding 3: The Dual-Audience Problem Is Structurally Universal — And Unresolved

**Source:** Andee Chamberlain (Texas State) + Andi Ault (UT Austin) + Scott Czubkowski (Ascension/Medxcel) + AdventHealth (John Culver + Scott Sukits)

Across every vertical in this research corpus — higher education, healthcare, QSR — the same structural tension appears between two distinct energy data consumers: the energy operations professional who sees the data as demand, usage, and conservation metrics, and the finance professional who sees the same data as dollars, budgets, and variance. At Texas State, Andee Chamberlain explicitly described the internal competition over data: "I care more about the demand and the information about our usage, but the bill processing folks care more about the bottom line numbers, like how much did it cost." At UT Austin, Andi Ault confirmed from the finance chair that she does not do the technical analysis herself and confirmed with emphatic immediacy that a cross-collaborative tool where the energy expert analyzes and the finance user receives shareable reports is exactly what she needs: "Oh, absolutely. Absolutely." At Ascension, Scott Czubkowski described the layman's interface as the product itself — a hospital CEO asking "What is the cost of my utilities in St. John's in Tulsa, Oklahoma" without calling an engineer.

**Why this matters for investors:** This is not a UX preference — it is a structural market requirement that current solutions fail to meet. EnergyCAP serves energy experts. ENGIE Impact serves bill processors. Neither serves the finance user in the format she needs. The first platform that serves both audiences from a single data layer — without requiring the finance user to become a technical analyst — captures a buyer who currently has no adequate solution.

A platform that leads with "we solve the data quality problem first" — and then delivers AI analytics on clean, attributed data — is positioned against the AI hype cycle rather than riding it. That is a counterintuitive, credibility-building positioning for a sophisticated investor audience.

## 5. COMPETITIVE IMPLICATIONS

### Against ENGIE Impact (primary incumbent)

**What the research supports:**
ENGIE Impact's competitive position is more fragile than its market share suggests. Walt Taylor's on-the-record confirmation — independently corroborated by Arcadia's technical documentation — that ENGIE's electronic bill data aggregation runs on Arcadia means that ENGIE's core technical moat is partially borrowed. ENGIE's own account manager (Adam Zavadsky) acknowledged that the analytics gap between what ENGIE delivers and what clients actually need is real and intentional — they have not invested in client-facing AI because it requires "subject matter experts to collaborate." ENGIE's exception monitoring is threshold-based and confirmed by both the vendor (Zavadsky) and an enterprise client (Goldstein) to have a known failure mode.

**Recommended positioning:** Do not position as "we replace ENGIE." Position as "we are the intelligence layer that makes ENGIE's data actually useful" — and in parallel, pursue a licensing or partnership conversation with ENGIE directly (Marc Spieler's Track 3 GTM). For enterprise clients dissatisfied with ENGIE's analytics but reluctant to switch processors, Clear Current as an analytical add-on is the low-friction entry point. For mid-market clients who have not yet committed to ENGIE, Clear Current as a fully integrated alternative is the pitch.

**What the research does not support:**
The research does not establish that Clear Current's bill intake and processing infrastructure is superior to ENGIE's for high-volume paper bill processing. Walt Taylor's honest assessment that enterprise complexity at 2,300 locations "resists standardization" suggests that at the very top of the enterprise tier, ENGIE's operational infrastructure remains a genuine advantage.

---

### Against EnergyCAP

**What the research supports:**
EnergyCAP is the default platform for university energy managers. Andee Chamberlain at Texas State named it as her immediate frame of reference when presented with Clear Current's concept: "It sounds kind of like what we already have." However, she then described multiple areas where EnergyCAP falls short — district energy metering, blending internal sub-meter data with utility data, and creating billing from combined sources. The gap between what EnergyCAP promises and what it delivers on complex metering was described explicitly: "EnergyCAP tells us that they can do that stuff, but it's harder than it sounds."

**Recommended positioning:** In the higher education segment, the competitive positioning should be direct: "Here is what we do that EnergyCAP cannot" — specifically AI-driven pattern deviation detection, district energy data blending, and a dual-audience interface that serves both the energy manager and the finance user from the same dataset. EnergyCAP is a data storage and reporting platform; Clear Current is an intelligence platform. The AI chat interface is the clearest product differentiator — EnergyCAP does not have one.

**What the research does not support:**
Only one institution (Texas State) gave a detailed EnergyCAP critique. The corpus does not include a direct head-to-head feature comparison. The competitive teardown needs to be built from product-level primary research on EnergyCAP specifically.

---

### Against Energy Print

**What the research supports:**
Energy Print is Houston Methodist's current third-party analytics platform (Sean Sevy interview). Sean described its value clearly: benchmarking against peers, bill tracking, Energy Star integration, budgeting support. Its specific gap is equally clear: forecasting models assume a stable building portfolio, and Houston Methodist adds campuses and hospital wings regularly. "Standard trend-based forecasting doesn't account for growth."

**Recommended positioning:** Against Energy Print, the competitive opening is growth scenario modeling — a forward-looking forecasting capability that accepts new building parameters before the first bill arrives. This is a specific, customer-articulated gap that Clear Current should close. The secondary positioning is the dual-audience architecture and push-based alerts, which Energy Print does not appear to offer based on Sean's description.

**What the research does not support:**
Energy Print's pricing, contract structure, and other customer relationships beyond Houston Methodist are not in this corpus. A competitive teardown is needed before leading with this in investor materials.

**What the research supports:**
Arcadia is described in this corpus primarily as a data infrastructure player — the electronic bill aggregation backbone for ENGIE Impact. Peer AI's full feature set, pricing, and other customer relationships are not assessed in this corpus. The 9-month deployment at Ascension is a thin single-source data point.

## 7. OPEN QUESTIONS AND RESEARCH GAPS

### Critical Gaps — These Must Be Answered Before the May 2026 Fundraise

**Gap 1: The Full Recovery Case Study**
Marc Spieler raised this directly: "Have the utilities actually recognized the mistakes and issued refunds?" The research documents error detection across multiple pilots but does not document the full recovery cycle — error identified, utility contacted, credit issued, dollars returned. This is the single most important gap for investor credibility. Without at least two documented full-cycle recovery cases (with approximate dollar amounts), the $10M in identified billing errors claim lacks a conversion rate. Investors will ask: "Of the $10M you found, how much was actually recovered?"

**Gap 2: ARR Implications of the $10M in Identified Errors**
The prompt references $10M in identified billing errors across 12 pilots. This research corpus does not contain that figure — it must be coming from Clear Current's internal pilot data. The critical investor question is: what pricing model converts that $10M in error identification into ARR? Is it a percentage of savings found? A flat subscription? Per-location pricing? The research corpus provides Marc Spieler's property tax analogy (50% of savings found, recurring, no savings = no fee) as a strong model, but does not validate that clients accept this structure. Contract terms from even one or two pilots would answer this.

**Gap 3: The Enterprise Complexity Ceiling**
Walt Taylor was honest: at 2,300 Panda locations with 45 rate structures per utility in Alabama alone, the complexity resists standardization at the high end. The research does not establish where the complexity ceiling is — at what company size or location count does Clear Current's AI analysis accuracy begin to degrade? This matters for market sizing and for answering investor questions about why large enterprises won't just build this internally.

**Gap 4: Competitive Feature Comparison**
Phil Combs named Energy Toolbase as the closest existing competitor for tariff intelligence. Sean Sevy uses Energy Print. Scott Czubkowski uses Peer AI. EnergyCAP is the university incumbent. This research provides one-sided critiques of each — but no direct feature comparison, pricing comparison, or customer satisfaction data. A structured competitive analysis with pricing information is needed for the investor deck.

**Gap 5: Sales Cycle Length and Conversion Rate**
The research establishes that healthcare adoption is structurally slow (AdventHealth's 2.5-year BAS story, Ann Walston's year-long meter mapping). But for Clear Current's billing intelligence product specifically — which does not touch building controls networks — the sales cycle length is unknown. No interviewee was asked: "How long would it take you to decide to buy this?" That question needs to be asked directly in follow-up conversations.
