## 2. KEY FINDINGS

**Finding 1: There is a two-tier procurement market, and Clear Current's evidence base is concentrated in Tier 1.**

The research reveals a fundamental bifurcation. Ann Walston at BSMH represents sophisticated, internally-managed procurement: a formal committee (UPSC), quarterly meeting cadence, layered hedging strategy, external market consultant, commodity-level decision-making on natural gas and electricity separately. Sean Sevy at Houston Methodist represents broker-dependent procurement: Prism Energy handles contract execution; Houston Methodist receives the output but doesn't run the process. Roger Goldstein at Panda represents what I'd call "non-procurement" — the company uses NG Insight for bill processing but no hedging strategy or supplier RFP process is described at all; the annual rate class review is the sum of active procurement activity.

This matters because an RFP Builder module would serve Tier 1 buyers (BSMH-type) very differently than Tier 2 (Houston Methodist with a broker) or non-procurement operators (Panda). The product need is not uniform.

_Sources: Ann Walston Transcript; Sean Sevy Transcript; Roger Goldstein Transcript_

**Finding 2: The UPSC structure at BSMH is the closest thing in this corpus to a prototype procurement workflow — and it reveals a very specific sequence.**

Walston described the procurement cycle with unusual precision: (1) market monitoring → (2) RFP for supplier partners → (3) contract execution with selected suppliers → (4) hedging tranches layered in as market conditions develop → (5) quarterly UPSC check-in on positions. This is a multi-month workflow, not a single event. The current state at time of interview was between steps 3 and 4 — contracts just executed, hedge decision pending given favorable market conditions.

The timing also matters: Walston described being "locked in for the following year so that our budgets are known." This implies procurement decisions are sequenced ahead of the budget cycle. If budget finalization happens in, say, October for a calendar-year organization, the procurement window that feeds it would run roughly May through September.

_Source: Ann Walston Transcript, direct_

**Finding 3: "Cursory" is the word that matters most in Sean Sevy's entire interview.**

When Sevy describes Prism Energy's invoice review function, he uses the word "cursory" without prompting. This is not my characterization — it's his. A broker doing cursory bill reviews catches "calculation issues" and some tariff errors. It does not catch systematic meter misreadings, baseline drift, or slow-burn billing errors. The power factor case Sevy is currently working (79-81% vs. expected 93-94%) was flagged through the broker's review — but only because the number was so far outside normal that it crossed a threshold. The question is how many subtler errors don't cross that threshold.

This finding directly validates the "cursory is what you get when your fee is embedded in the supply contract" observation I bring to this analysis from 17 years in the field. Prism Energy's economics are tied to contract execution, not bill accuracy. Their incentive to do deep invoice auditing is structurally limited.

_Source: Sean Sevy Transcript, direct_

**Finding 4: The hedging strategy creates a procurement engagement trigger that is market-responsive, not calendar-driven.**

Walston's description of the UPSC reveals that procurement engagement is not purely seasonal — it's triggered by market movement. "Right now... the market is extremely favorable... we're looking at, is it the right time to make a purchase?" The quarterly meeting is the minimum cadence; additional activity happens when conditions warrant. This means an energy management platform that serves this buyer needs market-responsive alerts, not just calendar reminders. The question "should we lock in now?" requires real-time commodity price context, not just billing data.

This is a gap in what bill-processing-focused tools provide. NG Insight, Energy Print, and Get Choice are all bill processors. None of them, based on the evidence in this corpus, provide forward-looking commodity price intelligence that would inform a hedging decision.

_Sources: Ann Walston Transcript; Adam Zavadsky Summary (ENGIE's service stack adds procurement consulting but this is advisory, not automated)_

**Finding 5: Natural gas and electricity are managed on different procurement cycles, and the research captures both.**

Walston explicitly distinguishes: "we do that for both now for gas and electricity." But the timing and mechanisms differ. Natural gas can be hedged more directly through supplier contracts and financial hedges; electricity in deregulated markets is procured through supplier agreements that may or may not allow layered financial hedging depending on the ISO/RTO and contract structure. In PJM territory (where BSMH operates), capacity charges add a third procurement-adjacent variable — they're set through an annual capacity auction, not through negotiated supplier contracts. Walston's description of capacity charges going up "tenfold in the last two years" reflects PJM auction outcomes, not billing errors.

A single RFP Builder module that treats natural gas and electricity identically would miss this structural difference. The workflows diverge at the point of market structure.

_Sources: Ann Walston Transcript; Ann Walston Summary_

**Finding 6: The external consultant/broker relationship is the primary procurement intelligence source for most of these buyers — and it is not systematically captured anywhere.**

Walston uses a named external consultant for "market knowledge" who also manages invoicing through Get Choice. Sevy uses Prism Energy for contract brokerage and cursory bill review. Roger Goldstein contracts an external energy manager ("Walt") while outsourcing bill processing to NG Insight. In each case, the procurement intelligence — commodity price trends, contract terms, rate structure options — lives in the broker's head, not in a client-facing system.

This means the buyer's access to procurement intelligence is gated by the broker relationship. If the broker is unavailable, misinformed, or incentivized to recommend a particular supplier, the buyer has no independent source of data to check that recommendation.

_Sources: Ann Walston Transcript; Sean Sevy Transcript; Roger Goldstein Transcript_

**Finding 7: The RFP process at BSMH is a real event with real timing — and it concludes before the hedging strategy begins.**

The sequence Walston describes is: RFP first, then contract execution, then hedging. You cannot hedge without a contract with a supplier that enables the hedge. This means the RFP is a prerequisite for the hedging strategy, not a companion to it. The implication for product timing is that RFP support tools need to be deployed before the procurement window, not during it. If BSMH's hedging target is to be "locked in for the following year" before budget season, the RFP process needs to conclude by late summer/early fall at the latest.

_Source: Ann Walston Transcript, direct_

**Finding 8: Panda's procurement activity is almost entirely implicit — and the evidence suggests they may be systematically under-optimized on rate structures.**

Roger Goldstein described an annual rate class review across all utilities as something Panda does already. But with 2,600 locations across dozens of states and hundreds of utilities, the depth of that analysis is almost certainly limited. His description of this as a known activity ("a function we do already") combined with the 10,000 bills/month volume and two-person review team makes it implausible that any detailed rate class analysis is happening at the location level. The question he said he would ask a tool: "Look for what rate... utilities offer different rate classes for different industries and size buildings... are we on the best rate class that gives us the lowest cost?" — implies he knows this analysis is probably not being done as thoroughly as it should be.

_Source: Roger Goldstein Transcript, direct_

**Finding 9: The hospitality vertical has a structurally different procurement problem — and it is not well-served by any current tool.**

Randy Dawes' email responses reveal that Hyatt corporate does not touch invoices at managed or franchised properties. Procurement decisions at those properties are made by hotel owners and asset managers. Hyatt's corporate energy strategy is ESG/reporting-focused, not commodity-focused. This means the hospitality vertical requires a completely different buyer identification strategy: the decision-maker for procurement and billing in hospitality is a hotel management company or asset manager, not a brand's sustainability team.

This is a buying center insight that changes the sales motion entirely.

_Source: Randy Dawes Summary_

**Finding 11: The "structural energy price increase" macro context makes procurement timing and optimization more urgent than it has been in a decade.**

Zavadsky provided an unprompted and confident macro view: energy prices will rise structurally over the next 5-10 years regardless of short-term commodity fluctuation. This macro context is the most powerful urgency driver in Clear Current's sales narrative. It is not manufactured urgency — it is real, confirmed by multiple independent sources.

### 4A: PROCUREMENT-SPECIFIC SECTION OF THE SEASONAL ENERGY MANAGEMENT CALENDAR

The calendar should have procurement as a distinct row or swim lane, separate from consumption management. Here is the evidence-based structure:

**January – February: Post-Hedge Review / Budget Finalization**

- Procurement status: Prior-year supply contracts are in effect; hedges for current year should already be locked if the organization follows BSMH's model
- Activity: Review current positions against budget assumptions; identify any un-hedged tranches from prior fall decisions
- Platform engagement trigger: "Are our current hedged positions reflected in my budget? What exposure do I still have?"
- Evidence: Ann Walston described wanting to be locked in before budget finalization; BSMH's fiscal year budgeting implies positions need to be established by late prior year
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript_

**March – April: Natural Gas Market Window / Supplier Contract Execution**

- Procurement status: Spring often begins a new procurement evaluation cycle; natural gas markets in spring can offer favorable entry points before summer demand
- Activity: UPSC quarterly meeting; evaluate market conditions; potentially lock additional tranches; execute supplier contracts if prior RFP concluded
- Platform engagement trigger: Alert — "Natural gas futures pricing has moved X% this week. Your current hedge position is Y%. Evaluate whether to lock additional tranches before summer demand peak."
- Evidence: Walston was executing natural gas supplier contracts in April 2026 while evaluating whether to lock additional hedges given "extremely favorable" market conditions
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript (same-day reporting)_

**May – June: Electricity Procurement Window / Pre-Summer Positioning**

- Procurement status: For calendar-year organizations, mid-year is often when electricity supply contracts for the following year begin to be evaluated; summer demand drives electricity prices higher, creating urgency to lock before peak
- Activity: Rate class review (Goldstein's annual activity); evaluate electricity supplier options in deregulated markets; assess impact of upcoming PJM capacity auction on forward capacity charges
- Platform engagement trigger: Annual rate class review prompt; "Your electricity contract expires in X months. Current forward prices are Y. Compare against your existing rate."
- Evidence: Goldstein's annual rate class review; Walston's UPSC handles electricity separately from natural gas; PJM capacity auction timing affects spring/summer positioning
- _Sources: Roger Goldstein Transcript; Ann Walston Transcript_

**July – August: UPSC Quarterly Check-in / Mid-Year Position Review**

- Procurement status: Midpoint evaluation of hedge positions vs. market; opportunity to add tranches if market moved favorably or unfavorably from initial position
- Activity: UPSC quarterly meeting (second of four); review positions; evaluate whether to accelerate or defer additional hedging
- Platform engagement trigger: "Your Q2 hedge position review is due. Current market price vs. your locked positions: [comparison]. Recommended action: [hold/add/reassess]."
- Evidence: Walston described UPSC as meeting "about quarterly" with check-ins on "what is the market doing? Where are our positions? Do we need to do anything differently?"
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript_

**September – October: CRITICAL — Next-Year RFP Launch Window**

- Procurement status: This is the highest-activity procurement period for organizations following BSMH's model; RFPs for next-year supply contracts need to be issued now so contracts can be executed before year-end budget lock
- Activity: Issue RFP to natural gas and electricity suppliers; evaluate bids; select supplier partners; begin contract execution
- Platform engagement trigger: "Your current supply contracts expire in [X months]. Historical forward curves suggest initiating an RFP now. Would you like to generate a supplier RFP using your current usage profile and rate preferences?"
- Evidence: Walston's description implies the RFP concludes before hedging begins; hedging is designed to lock in the budget for the "following year"; budget lock implies pre-October completion
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript; analytical inference on timing_
- **Confidence level**: Moderately confident on timing; strongly confident on sequence (RFP → contract → hedge → budget)

**November – December: Contract Execution / Final Hedge Locking**

- Procurement status: Final window to lock remaining hedge positions before year-end; contracts should be executed; any un-hedged exposure becomes the next year's first-quarter procurement problem
- Activity: Execute supply contracts; finalize hedge positions; confirm budget alignment; UPSC quarterly meeting (Q4)
- Platform engagement trigger: "You have [X%] of your projected annual natural gas consumption un-hedged. Current forward prices are [Y]. Do you want to lock in the remaining tranche before year-end?"
- Evidence: Walston's model targets being "completely locked in and hedged" before the following year; this implies Q4 completion as the goal
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript_

- Decision required: Lock vs. wait on hedging
- Evidence: "The market is extremely favorable right now... we're looking at, is it the right time to make a purchase?" — Walston
- Output format: Market comparison dashboard with current position vs. current market price; tranche decision scenario comparisons
- _Source: Ann Walston Transcript_

### 4C: RFP BUILDER MODULE ASSESSMENT

**The question asked**: Is there enough primary evidence to recommend this as a product module, or is it too early-stage?

**My answer**: There is sufficient evidence to include the RFP Builder in the product roadmap — but as a **Tier 2 priority** behind anomaly detection and rate class optimization. Here is my reasoning:

**Evidence that supports the RFP Builder:**

1. A real RFP process exists and has been described in detail (BSMH). It is not hypothetical. It happens at a defined time. It uses usage data that the platform already has. — _Ann Walston Transcript_

2. The RFP is a prerequisite for the hedging strategy. You cannot hedge without a supplier contract. This means the RFP module is not a standalone feature — it is the gateway to the hedging workflow. — _Ann Walston Transcript_

3. The buyer who runs the RFP (Walston at BSMH) is also the buyer who would use Clear Current's platform. The target persona is confirmed. — _Ann Walston Transcript_

4. The UPSC committee structure means RFPs are produced collaboratively, not individually. A platform that facilitates multi-stakeholder RFP creation (facilities + finance + external consultant) serves a real workflow. — _Ann Walston Transcript_

5. The Project Scope document explicitly lists "RFP Builder & Tariff Comparison Tool" as an illustrative module concept, validating that John and Dan have already considered this feature direction. — _Project Scope_

**Evidence limitations and gaps:**

1. **Only one buyer described the RFP process in operational detail.** Walston is the sole source. Sevy delegates to Prism (broker-driven RFP); Goldstein's procurement is not sophisticated enough to involve a formal RFP; Dawes operates in a non-applicable structure (hospitality). Single-source evidence on a complex feature is insufficient for a Tier 1 priority.

2. **No evidence on what the RFP document actually contains.** Walston describes the process but not the artifact. What supplier evaluation criteria does BSMH use? What data inputs feed the RFP? What format do suppliers respond in? These are critical design questions that the research does not answer.

3. **No evidence on whether buyers would use a platform-generated RFP vs. their own templates.** Walston has been running this process for years; she likely has established templates and relationships. Would she use Clear Current's RFP Builder, or would she just use the platform's usage data to feed her existing process? This is unknown.

4. **Broker-mediated buyers (Houston Methodist) may not need an RFP Builder at all.** If Prism Energy handles the RFP process for Houston Methodist, the buyer at that organization is not the RFP author. The RFP Builder would require a different buyer persona than the platform's other features.

**My recommendation:**

Include the RFP Builder in the product roadmap at Tier 2, with this specification:

> _RFP Builder should be designed as a **data export and preparation tool first**, not a document creation tool first. The immediate value is: "here is your usage profile, consumption history, and rate structure data packaged for supplier outreach." The document builder layer — templates, evaluation criteria, supplier comparison — comes in a subsequent version. This sequencing respects the evidence: buyers need the data; they may have their own RFP templates already. Don't build the document layer until you have a second or third healthcare buyer confirming they would use it._

The feature earns its place in the roadmap because the evidence from Walston is real and operational. But it should not be the first feature built or the primary pitch to new prospects — it requires a buyer who already has enough procurement sophistication to run RFPs. That limits the TAM in the short term to Tier 1 buyers only.

## 5. COMPETITIVE IMPLICATIONS

### Against Energy Print (Houston Methodist's Current Tool)

Energy Print's identified gap is forecasting — specifically, it assumes a stable building portfolio and cannot model growth scenarios. This is real and Sevy named it explicitly. But the more relevant competitive gap for Clear Current is on the **procurement side**: Energy Print provides bill tracking and benchmarking; it provides no evidence of market intelligence, commodity price tracking, or contract/procurement support. If Clear Current can serve both the bill intelligence function AND the procurement intelligence function in a single platform, that is a materially better product for buyers like BSMH who need both.

The specific competitive move: if you're pitching a Houston Methodist or BSMH-type buyer who uses Energy Print, lead with "Energy Print shows you what happened. We show you what to do next — including whether to lock in your next procurement tranche today."

### Against Prism Energy / Embedded Brokers

This is the most direct and most important competitive frame from a procurement perspective. Prism Energy's invoice review is "cursory." This is not my word — it's Sevy's word, describing his own vendor. The structural reason is that Prism's economics run on contract execution; deep invoice auditing is not their incentive structure.

Clear Current should explicitly position around this: **the broker's financial interest is in placing the contract; Clear Current's financial interest is in finding what the broker missed.** These are not aligned incentives. This framing is respectful of the broker relationship (don't ask the customer to fire their broker) while positioning Clear Current as an independent verification layer.

The specific sales move for broker-adjacent buyers: "Your broker handles the procurement. We make sure what you agreed to in that contract is actually what you're being charged."

### Against ENGIE Impact / NG Insight

ENGIE Impact's moat is data. Their bill processing creates the data foundation that makes everything else possible. Clear Current cannot displace this in the short term — and shouldn't try. The channel play is partnership.

On the procurement consulting side, ENGIE's offering is advisory and human-driven. An AI-native procurement analytics layer (real-time commodity price alerts, automated tranche recommendations, usage-profile-driven RFP preparation) would be faster, cheaper to deliver, and scalable in a way that ENGIE's human advisory model cannot be. If ENGIE is considering expanding AI into client-facing procurement analytics, Clear Current as a white-labeled intelligence layer is a better product than building it themselves.

### Against Arcadia

Arcadia's competitive liability (as identified by Zavadsky) is data access — they don't process bills, so they don't have the underlying data. On the procurement side specifically, this limitation is even more acute: commodity price intelligence and contract management require not just bill data but forward-looking market data. If Arcadia lacks the bill data foundation, they certainly lack the market intelligence layer. Clear Current, if it builds the procurement intelligence function on top of its existing bill data foundation, is structurally better positioned than Arcadia for buyers who need both.

### Against Get Choice (BSMH's Current Processor)

Get Choice is Walston's third-party bill processor — it receives, analyzes, and pays invoices. It has an AI anomaly detection function. But based on Walston's description, Get Choice does not provide procurement intelligence, market price tracking, or RFP support. Its function is retrospective (analyze incoming bills) rather than forward-looking (guide procurement decisions). Clear Current's opportunity with a BSMH-type buyer is to layer forward-looking procurement intelligence on top of the bill processing infrastructure that Get Choice already provides — positioning as complementary, not competitive.

## 7. OPEN QUESTIONS AND RESEARCH GAPS

**Gap 1: The Natural Gas vs. Electricity Procurement Calendar**
The research gives detailed evidence on natural gas procurement (BSMH's April 2026 activity) but almost nothing on electricity procurement timing. Are electricity supply contracts typically negotiated on the same cycle? Different cycle? Does the deregulated vs. regulated market status of the buyer's state change the procurement window entirely? For a buyer in a regulated state (no supplier choice), there is no RFP process at all. This is a fundamental market segmentation question that the current corpus cannot answer.

_Recommended follow-up: Ask Walston or a similar buyer to describe the electricity procurement process separately from natural gas. The research conflates them._

**Gap 2: What Does the RFP Document Actually Contain?**
Walston described running an RFP for supplier partners, but the research does not capture what data, evaluation criteria, or contract terms are in that document. To build an RFP Builder module, Clear Current needs to know what suppliers actually look at when evaluating bids and what buyers prioritize in supplier selection. This is a critical product design question that requires a dedicated follow-up with at least two to three UPSC participants.

_Recommended follow-up: Request a sanitized copy of a prior natural gas or electricity RFP from a willing participant. The Ann Walston connection makes this a reasonable ask._

**Gap 3: What Is the External Consultant's Role in the UPSC, and Who Are the Market Knowledge Providers?**
Walston mentions an external consultant who participates in the UPSC and provides "market knowledge." This consultant is also connected to Get Choice for bill processing. Who are the typical providers of this market intelligence function? Are they commodity brokers, energy consultants, or financial advisors? Understanding this ecosystem is critical for both competitive mapping and channel strategy.

_Recommended follow-up: Ask Walston who the consultant is (or what category of firm) and whether the market intelligence function and the bill processing function are from the same vendor or different vendors._

**Gap 4: How Do Buyers Evaluate and Select Energy Suppliers?**
The RFP process produces a set of supplier proposals. How do buyers evaluate them? Is it purely on price? On contract flexibility? On credit terms? On the supplier's hedging capabilities? This is the evaluation criteria problem that any RFP Builder must solve in its design, and the research does not answer it.

**Gap 5: What Is the Hospitality Procurement Structure for Independent Hotel Owners?**
Randy Dawes confirmed that Hyatt corporate does not touch invoices at managed properties. But who does? The hotel owner? A property management company? An asset manager? This is a completely unmapped buyer persona in the research, and hospitality represents a large multi-site market where procurement support could be highly valuable.

_Recommended follow-up: Interview one hotel owner or asset manager who is directly responsible for energy bills at multiple managed properties. The Dawes interview established that this buyer exists and corporate Hyatt is not it._

**Gap 6: Are There States or Regions Where No Energy Procurement Decision Exists?**
In fully regulated utility territories, commercial customers have no supplier choice — they take the regulated rate. For buyers in these states, an RFP Builder is irrelevant; rate class optimization is the only procurement-adjacent lever. How large is this portion of Clear Current's target market? This is a market segmentation question that would shape how much development investment the RFP Builder deserves.

**Gap 7: How Long Does the Full UPSC RFP-to-Contract Cycle Take?**
Walston was executing contracts in April but didn't describe when the RFP was issued or how long the supplier evaluation process took. If the cycle takes 60-90 days, the engagement trigger for RFP preparation needs to fire significantly before the contract execution deadline. If it takes two weeks, the window is narrow and the trigger needs to be more precise.

**Gap 8: How Do Buyers Handle Multi-Year Contracts vs. Annual Contracts?**
Walston's hedging discussion focused on within-year tranches but didn't clearly describe whether the supply contracts themselves are annual or multi-year. Multi-year contract structures would significantly change the RFP cadence — a buyer locked into a three-year supply agreement doesn't need an RFP module every year. This affects both feature frequency of use and pricing model design.

**Gap 9: What Is the Dollar Volume of Procurement Decisions Made by These Buyers?**
Understanding the magnitude of the procurement decisions these buyers make would help size the value of procurement support features. Walston oversees 36 hospitals; their annual electricity and natural gas spend is not captured in the research. Knowing that number would change how aggressively Clear Current should invest in the procurement module.

**Gap 10: Does Clear Current Have Commodity Price Feed Access?**
The most urgent capability gap I identified — market-responsive procurement alerts — requires real-time commodity price data for natural gas and electricity. Does Clear Current currently have access to these data feeds? If not, what is the cost and complexity of acquiring them? This is a technical prerequisite question that would determine whether the procurement trigger features are buildable in the current product cycle.
