**Trigger (Reactive — Problem-Driven)**: A department chair complains that their energy bill doesn't reflect their building's actual usage. - Flagged anomalies: buildings where consumption is outside historical normal range (Norton's use case)

### 4.2 Regulatory Intelligence Journey Map — University-Specific Build

**What this journey is about**: A university finance officer submits her annual budget in March. She builds it using assumed utility rates. The actual rates from the City of Austin (or City of San Marcos) are often not finalized until after the budget is submitted. She is making a multi-million dollar financial commitment based on an assumption that may be wrong. Regulatory intelligence closes this gap.

**Trigger (Proactive — Planning-Driven, with a System-Initiated component)**: Budget season begins in October at UT Austin. The system detects that Austin Energy has filed a rate case with the Texas PUC, or has announced a rate adjustment hearing, or that preliminary rate language has been published. The system sends an alert: "Austin Energy rate change proceeding detected. Preliminary data suggests X% increase effective [date]. Budget deadline is [date]. Updated budget modeling available."

**Entry Point for Andi Ault**: An alert arrives — not a login. Andi does not log in to look for information; the system surfaces the information to her. This is the design principle her transcript establishes: push, don't pull.

**Critical design note**: Andi explicitly said she would not use a chat interface to find this information. The alert itself must contain enough summary information to be actionable — the rate change amount, the effective date, the deadline relative to her budget submission, and a link to a revised budget model with the new rate assumptions loaded. Requiring Andi to chat her way to this information defeats the purpose.

**What the journey looks like from Andee Chamberlain's perspective** (Texas State, energy ops): The Municipal Utility Problem. Texas State purchases electricity from the City of San Marcos, a municipal utility. There is no PUC rate case process in the same sense as a regulated investor-owned utility. Rate changes may be decided by a city council vote or municipal utility board action. The regulatory intelligence workflow for Texas State is different — it requires monitoring local government proceedings, not state PUC filings. This is a more difficult data problem. The corpus does not tell us whether Clear Current's regulatory intelligence capability is limited to IOU rate cases or extends to municipal utilities.

**User Actions at UT Austin**:

1. Receive alert from system about Austin Energy rate change
2. Open budget model with updated rate inputs pre-loaded
3. Review impact on total utility budget (5-year forecast is already built — this is a delta calculation)
4. Determine whether budget revision is feasible before submission deadline, or whether to note the uncertainty in budget assumptions
5. Share revised budget model with CFO (this is where "intuitive reports that are easy to share" — Andi's second stated need — is critical)

**Outputs Generated**:

- Rate change alert with summary, source, and effective date
- Updated budget model with new rate inputs
- Variance analysis: original budget vs. revised budget, total impact
- Exportable summary for CFO

**Natural gas hedging integration**: Andi described the fuel hedging decision as the one proactive lever the finance team has. A regulatory intelligence layer that tracks natural gas market prices and Henry Hub futures alongside utility rate cases could make the hedging decision more informed. At $20M annual natural gas spend, even a 1-2% improvement in hedging timing is $200K-$400K in avoided cost.

**Quote to anchor the regulatory intelligence journey**: _"Which we generally don't know until after we've turned in our budget, but you know, try to get some insight."_ — Andi Ault, UT Austin UEM Finance

The platform must normalize all of these into a single meter-level view. EnergyCAP promises to do this. Andee says it doesn't work cleanly. This is the specific technical challenge that Clear Current's data ingest architecture must solve before the validation journey can function.

## 5. COMPETITIVE IMPLICATIONS

### Against EnergyCAP

EnergyCAP is the incumbent. Every university energy manager in this corpus knows it and several use it. The competitive strategy is not to attack EnergyCAP on its strengths — it has 20+ years of market penetration, established integrations, and institutional familiarity. The strategy is to exploit the specific documented failures the research confirms:

**Failure 1 — District Energy Metering Integration**: Andee Chamberlain says directly that EnergyCAP can't cleanly blend internal sub-meter data with utility meter data. This is not speculation — it is a customer of EnergyCAP describing a gap. Clear Current's pitch in the university market is: _"We do what EnergyCAP promises but hasn't delivered on the district energy metering problem."_

**Failure 2 — The Bill Processing Module Is Unused**: Texas State doesn't use EnergyCAP's bill processing module. This means EnergyCAP's footprint at Texas State is narrower than it appears — they're using it as a database and reporting tool, not as a processing and validation tool. The bill processing workflow is open. Clear Current can enter this account specifically through the bill processing gap without needing to displace EnergyCAP's core database function immediately.

**Failure 3 — The UX and Chat Gap**: EnergyCAP was designed for a data entry and reporting workflow. It does not offer a conversational AI interface. Andee's questions — "Who's my biggest user? What's the EUI per building? What's our biggest bang for our buck?" — are natural language queries. EnergyCAP requires navigating report templates and data exports to answer them. Clear Current can answer them conversationally.

**The displacement path at a university**: Don't try to replace EnergyCAP on day one. Enter through the bill processing gap (especially at Texas State, where the bill processor has just left). Prove value through anomaly detection and error identification. Build the chargeback intelligence layer as a differentiated capability. Demonstrate AI-native query interfaces. Only then propose full displacement.

### Against WatchWire/Tango (and similar multi-site energy intelligence platforms)

WatchWire/Tango targets the commercial real estate and multi-site corporate market. For the university segment, their product faces the same district energy metering challenge as EnergyCAP — they are designed for buildings that purchase utilities from external providers, not institutions that generate and distribute their own. The research doesn't name WatchWire/Tango directly in the higher education interviews, which is itself informative: these platforms are not in the mental model of university energy managers the way EnergyCAP is. Clear Current's positioning against WatchWire/Tango in the university segment is similar: we understand the complexity of mixed metering environments that pure utility-purchaser platforms don't address.

### Against ENGIE Impact

ENGIE Impact operates as a managed services provider with its own platform. The risk with ENGIE Impact is not just software competition — it's the managed service model that captures the outsourcing impulse that Texas State is currently acting on. Andee said they're "probably going to outsource" the bill processing function. If ENGIE Impact or a similar managed services firm captures that outsourcing relationship, Clear Current loses the account entry point. Clear Current's competitive response: we give you the software control, auditability, and institutional memory that a managed service provider can't. When the outsourced provider makes an error or leaves, you have no data. With Clear Current, the data and the institutional knowledge stay with you.

### Against Arcadia and EnergyPrint

The research does not mention these platforms in any of the higher education interviews. Neither appears in any university energy manager's mental model based on this corpus. This could mean: (a) they are not active in the university market segment, (b) they serve a different buyer persona within universities, or (c) they simply weren't mentioned in these specific conversations. I would not draw strong competitive conclusions without more information.

### Against the "Texas Energy Consultants" analog (student/boutique AI projects)

Troy Nevels mentioned that UT Austin is already working with a student-formed company building weather-correlated load forecasting. This is not a sophisticated commercial competitor, but it represents a pattern: universities are willing to experiment with AI tools on specific problems through low-cost, informal arrangements. Clear Current needs a differentiated commercial proposition beyond "we use AI" — because cheap AI is increasingly available. The differentiation is breadth (bills + meters + tariffs + regulatory intelligence in one platform), reliability (not a class project), continuity (not subject to team graduation), and integration (with ERP systems, EnergyCAP, BAS/SCADA data).

## 7. OPEN QUESTIONS AND RESEARCH GAPS

### Gap 1: What Does EnergyCAP's Bill Processing Module Actually Do, and Why Isn't Texas State Using It?

The research establishes that Texas State uses EnergyCAP but not its bill processing module. It does not fully explain why — was it a cost decision? A capability gap? A workflow mismatch? Understanding the specific reason Texas State chose not to use the bill processing module (before the bill processor left) would clarify whether Clear Current is filling a known EnergyCAP gap or a user adoption gap. If it's an EnergyCAP capability gap, that's a product wedge. If it's an adoption problem, the challenge is different.

### Gap 2: How Does Municipal Utility Rate Monitoring Work in Practice?

Both Texas State and UT Austin North Campus purchase electricity from municipal utilities (City of San Marcos and City of Austin, respectively). Regulatory intelligence for IOU customers means monitoring PUC rate cases — a structured process with public filings. For municipal utilities, rate changes may be decided by city councils or utility boards with less formal advance notice. Does Clear Current's regulatory intelligence capability cover municipal utility proceedings? This is a critical product question for the university market, where municipal utility purchasing is common.

### Gap 3: What Does the ERP/Financial System Integration Actually Require?

Andi Ault identified financial system integration as her most important automation wish. She did not name the financial system. Knowing what ERP system Texas State and UT Austin use (likely Banner or Workday, given their size and sector) would allow Clear Current to evaluate whether a pre-built integration is feasible or whether API-based export is the more practical approach. This is a product roadmap question with direct implications for the university market.

### Gap 4: How Are Internal Sub-Meter Readings Currently Collected and Exported from District Energy Systems?

Andee describes the sub-metering problem in terms of its difficulty but does not describe the specific technical workflow: how does the data from Texas State's central plant sub-meters get exported, in what format, at what frequency, and with what error handling? The answer determines how Clear Current would ingest this data. Is it a SCADA historian export? A BAS data push? Manual meter reading entry? The gap between "data exists" and "data is available in a format Clear Current can process" is where many energy platform implementations fail.

### Gap 5: What Would the Chargeback Policy Change Process Look Like at Texas State?

The research establishes the data gap (not enough granular consumption data) and the behavioral problem (square-footage billing doesn't incentivize conservation). But it doesn't explore the political and process dimension: who would need to approve a change to consumption-based chargeback at Texas State? What departments would resist it? What data would the energy team need to make the case to administration? Understanding the internal change management challenge would help Clear Current design a product that serves not just the technical need but the institutional adoption journey.

### Gap 6: What Is the Procurement Path for New Software at Texas State and UT Austin?

Neither interview explored how a university would actually purchase a new energy management platform. Is this a procurement-office decision? An IT decision? An energy department decision with finance sign-off? Does it require a competitive RFP? Understanding the procurement process is essential for go-to-market strategy. Universities are often subject to state procurement rules (particularly relevant for Texas State and UT Austin as state institutions) that can dramatically lengthen sales cycles.

### Gap 7: What Happened to the UT Austin Energy Management Program?

Troy Nevels mentioned that UT Austin "used to have a pretty robust energy management team" that was disbanded after a change in leadership a few years ago, and that they are "bringing that energy management group back now." This is an important organizational context: UT Austin's building-level energy management capability has been hollowed out and is being rebuilt. This means (a) there's institutional memory loss that a platform could help recover, (b) there are new hires who will be building workflows from scratch and might be more open to a new platform than entrenched incumbents, and (c) the energy management capability needs are currently underserved. Troy is a seven-month employee rebuilding a team — this is exactly the kind of institutional moment when new tools get adopted.

### Gap 8: Is There a Research Connection Between the "Texas Energy Consultants" Student Project and Clear Current?

Troy mentioned that a student-formed company called "Texas Energy Consultants" is building weather-correlated load forecasting for UT Austin and presenting results the following week. This is either a potential partner (if Clear Current could integrate their weather correlation work), a potential competitive signal (if this type of tool becomes commercially available), or simply a reference point. Worth following up to understand what they built and whether UT Austin found it useful.

### Gap 9: What Is the Competitive Set That These University Energy Managers Are Actually Aware Of?

When Andee Chamberlain evaluated Clear Current's product, she compared it to EnergyCAP. She did not mention Arcadia, WatchWire, EnergyPrint, ENGIE Impact, or any other platform. When Troy Nevels described the market, he mentioned Optimum (for chiller optimization specifically) but no billing intelligence platforms. This suggests that in the university segment, the competitive mental model is EnergyCAP plus specialized point tools, with no clear awareness of the broader energy intelligence platform market. This is both an opportunity (no entrenched alternative to EnergyCAP) and a challenge (no category awareness to leverage in sales conversations).

### Gap 10: How Would Carbon Charge Billing Complexity Affect Texas and University Buyers Specifically?

Troy raised carbon taxation in northeastern states as a growing billing complexity layer. Texas is not currently subject to state-level carbon pricing. But federal carbon pricing discussions continue, and several large Texas universities have their own sustainability commitments that function as internal carbon costs. The regulatory intelligence roadmap should account for how carbon charge complexity would layer onto these buyers as (if) carbon pricing expands to Texas.
