## 2. KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: ESG Compliance Is Present But Operationally Separated From Energy Billing

**Source:** Randy Dawes Summary (Hyatt); AdventHealth Summary; Ann Walston Summary

Across every organization in this corpus where ESG commitments are formally documented — Hyatt (World of Care, 2030 targets), AdventHealth (HHS Pledge, net-zero 2050), and BSMH (ENERGY STAR program) — the ESG reporting function is **structurally separated** from the energy billing and invoice processing function. Hyatt uses EcoTrack for sustainability data, a third-party aggregator for consumption data, and property owners bear invoice responsibility — three different parties for one energy picture. AdventHealth uses Watershed for GHG accounting and ENGIE Impact for bill payment — two different vendors. BSMH uses Get Choice for bill processing and Energy Star as the performance framework — again, separate.

**Implication:** Clear Current cannot assume that solving the billing intelligence problem automatically captures the ESG reporting opportunity. These are separate workflows, often owned by separate people, fed by separate systems.

### Finding 3: ENERGY STAR Is the Most Operationally Embedded ESG Framework in Healthcare

**Source:** Ann Walston Transcript and Summary; AdventHealth Summary (passing reference)

Ann Walston's use of ENERGY STAR at BSMH is the most operationally sophisticated ESG framework described by any interviewee. Every hospital has a 1-year, 5-year, and 10-year ENERGY STAR score target. Scores update monthly as bills are processed. The single certified energy manager's travel schedule and building-level roadmap work is organized around ENERGY STAR trajectory. Ann's own professional recognition is an ASHE Sustainability Champion award.

This is not aspirational sustainability marketing. ENERGY STAR score is the actual performance measurement system, updated with real billing data, tracking against multi-year targets. **The monthly bill is the input that updates the ESG score.** This is the most direct connection in the entire corpus between billing data quality and sustainability reporting outcomes.

**Implication:** For healthcare buyers, accurate billing data is not just a cost control issue — it is a sustainability performance measurement issue. An error in a monthly bill that goes uncorrected distorts the ENERGY STAR score for that hospital, which affects the energy manager's performance tracking against a 10-year target. This linkage should be explicit in Clear Current's healthcare pitch.

### Finding 4: Hyatt's Metrics Fragmentation Problem Is an ESG Data Quality Problem

**Source:** Randy Dawes Summary (verbatim response)

Randy's most substantive finding — that "different properties report energy in different units, making it impossible to create a common language around consumption that resonates at the property level" — is fundamentally an ESG data quality problem disguised as a unit conversion problem. When Hyatt corporate reports its 27.5% Scope 1/2 reduction target, the underlying data must be normalized across 1,450+ properties in 80+ countries, each potentially reporting in kWh, MMBTU, therms, GJ, or other units. The corporate system manages the conversion, but the person actually consuming the energy cannot understand what their consumption means.

This is a known, documented challenge in ESG reporting — the "metric normalization" problem affects every organization trying to aggregate consumption data across a heterogeneous portfolio. Randy named it without using ESG language to describe it.

### Finding 5: The Owner-Operator Structure Creates an ESG Accountability Gap Unique to Hospitality

**Source:** Randy Dawes Summary

Hyatt's owner-operator model creates a structural ESG problem that has no parallel in healthcare or higher education: the entity with the emissions reduction commitment (Hyatt corporate, with World of Care 2030 targets) does not own the buildings generating the emissions, does not pay the utility bills, and does not control the energy management decisions at most properties. The entity that does control those decisions (the property owner) has no direct stake in Hyatt's corporate sustainability targets.

This is the hospitality-specific version of the split incentive problem — widely discussed in commercial real estate ESG contexts (it is one of the primary challenges GRESB addresses) but not named as such by Randy. **Third-party data aggregators feeding EcoTrack are the technical workaround for this accountability gap**, but they do not solve the incentive misalignment.

### 4.3 Assessment: Standalone ESG Module vs. Embedded ESG Capabilities

This is the central analytical question and requires genuine judgment, not just evidence compilation.

**The case for embedded ESG capabilities (not a standalone module):**

The evidence in this corpus consistently shows that ESG is not a separate workflow for most operational buyers — it is a _consequence_ of getting operational data right. Ann Walston's ENERGY STAR score updates monthly from billing data. AdventHealth's Watershed inputs depend on accurate address-to-meter attribution. Hyatt's EcoTrack depends on third-party aggregators pulling reliable consumption data from properties. In every case, ESG performance measurement is downstream of billing data quality.

If Clear Current's core capabilities — invoice processing, anomaly detection, rate intelligence, meter attribution — are functioning correctly, the ESG outputs (consumption by facility, Scope 1/2 emissions by site, ENERGY STAR-compatible intensity metrics) flow naturally. Building a separate ESG module risks creating a siloed experience where operational users ignore it and sustainability users don't understand the billing layer.

John Culver's quote — "tell us why we should care and what we can do to change it" — does not describe a separate sustainability dashboard. It describes intelligence embedded in the billing interpretation workflow that has sustainability implications.

**The case for a standalone ESG module (or at minimum, a named ESG feature set):**

The ESG buyer and the operational buyer are different people. Randy Dawes is not the property engineer. Allegra Wiesler (AdventHealth's sustainability director, who did not attend the interview) is not Scott Sukits. At large organizations, the person who publishes the sustainability report and manages CDP disclosure does not spend time in the billing portal. They need outputs formatted for their specific workflow: prior-year energy consumption by site, normalized to common units, with Scope 1/2 attribution, exportable to their GHG accounting platform.

If Clear Current wants to capture the corporate sustainability buyer — the person making the platform selection decision at a company like Hyatt or AdventHealth — it needs to speak their language, meet them in their workflow, and produce outputs compatible with their systems (Watershed, EcoTrack, or custom GHG tools). That requires something more than embedding sustainability language in operational features.

**My recommendation, grounded in this evidence:**

**Build embedded ESG capabilities first, with a named ESG output layer as a Phase 2 module.**

_Phase 1 (embed now):_ Every billing module should produce data in formats that are ESG-reporting ready — consumption by site, normalized to kWh and therms, with date ranges compatible with annual inventory periods. Anomaly alerts should note when a billing error would affect sustainability metrics ("This meter failure has distorted your ENERGY STAR score for the past six months — here is the corrected consumption data"). Rate intelligence should flag when tariff structures are relevant to Scope 2 location-based vs. market-based emissions calculations (renewable tariff riders, RECs).

_Phase 2 (standalone module when evidence is stronger):_ Once buyers in this vertical confirm that a sustainability-team-facing dashboard would drive a separate purchasing decision — evidence this corpus does not yet contain — build the module. It should: aggregate clean consumption data across the portfolio, calculate Scope 1/2 emissions using EPA eGRID factors (or user-specified factors for renewable contracts), track intensity metrics against multi-year targets, and export in formats compatible with GHG accounting platforms (CSV at minimum; API to Watershed and similar platforms if deal flow justifies the integration investment).

_The trigger for Phase 2:_ A direct interview with a corporate sustainability director (Allegra Wiesler at AdventHealth, or equivalent at Hyatt corporate) who confirms that ESG reporting functionality would drive a separate purchase decision, not just enhance an existing operational purchase.

## 7. OPEN QUESTIONS AND RESEARCH GAPS

### Critical Gap 1: No Corporate Sustainability Director Was Interviewed

Every ESG-relevant interviewee in this corpus is an operational buyer (VP Facilities, Director of Control Systems, Finance Director) — not a corporate sustainability director. Allegra Wiesler at AdventHealth was scheduled and did not attend. The sustainability person at Hyatt who manages World of Care reporting was not interviewed. No one from BSMH's sustainability leadership was reached.

This matters because the ESG reporting buyer — the person who decides which platform produces the GHG inventory, who signs off on CDP disclosures, who manages the ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager account — is almost certainly not the same person who manages utility bill payment. Clear Current needs to understand: does this buyer make platform decisions independently? Does she have budget? Does the ESG reporting need create a separate procurement event, or does it ride on the operational platform selection?

**Research recommendation:** Direct interviews with Allegra Wiesler (AdventHealth), Hyatt's World of Care program lead, and the sustainability director at any large health system that publishes a CDP disclosure or GHG report.

---

### Critical Gap 2: Regulatory ESG Compliance Was Never Surfaced

No interviewee mentioned: NYC Local Law 97, California SB 253 (CCDAA), California SB 261, EU CSRD, SEC climate disclosure rule, or any state-level building performance standard. Given that:

- Hyatt has properties in NYC subject to LL97 penalties that began in 2024
- JLL manages commercial real estate portfolios in NYC and other LL97-affected cities
- Large health systems in California with >$1B revenue are subject to SB 253 starting with 2026 reports
- AdventHealth operates across 9 states and signed the HHS Health Sector Climate Pledge

...the absence of regulatory compliance language suggests either (a) research questions did not probe for it, or (b) these organizations genuinely have not yet integrated regulatory ESG compliance into their energy management decision-making.

**Research recommendation:** Ask directly: "Are you facing any mandatory sustainability reporting requirements — regulatory, investor-driven, or otherwise — with specific deadlines or financial penalties for non-compliance?" This question was apparently not asked in any of these interviews.

---

### Critical Gap 3: The ESG Data Flow Chain Was Never Mapped End-to-End

We know AdventHealth uses Watershed for GHG accounting and ENGIE for bill payment. We do not know: how does the data get from ENGIE to Watershed? Is it manual? Is it automated? Does someone at AdventHealth reconcile those two systems, and how often? What happens to the GHG inventory when a billing error like a meter failure occurs?

This mapping — utility bill → bill processor → GHG accounting platform → sustainability report — is the value chain Clear Current must fit into. We have fragments of it from multiple sources but not a complete picture from any single organization.

**Research recommendation:** A specific interview focused on: "Walk me through exactly how your energy consumption data gets from your utility bills into your GHG inventory or ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager account. Where are the manual steps? Where do errors typically occur?"

---

### Critical Gap 4: Does ENERGY STAR Score Distortion From Billing Errors Actually Matter to Buyers?

This is the central ESG value proposition I've built in this analysis — accurate billing data protects ESG performance metrics. But I have not confirmed that any buyer _actually thinks about it this way_. Ann Walston never made this connection. John Culver never made this connection. No one said "the reason I need accurate bills is that my ESG score depends on them."

There are two possible explanations: (a) the connection is real but buyers haven't articulated it yet — meaning Clear Current can teach them to value it, or (b) buyers don't think accurate billing data is important to ESG reporting because they use manual overrides or data adjustments when errors are detected — meaning this value proposition is weaker than it appears.

**Research recommendation:** Specifically ask Ann Walston or a comparable ENERGY STAR-focused buyer: "When you had the major meter failure that resulted in overbilling, did it affect your hospital's ENERGY STAR score? Did you submit corrected data to Portfolio Manager? How did you handle that from a sustainability reporting standpoint?"

---

### Critical Gap 5: The Scope 3 Question at Hospitality Scale

Hyatt's 53% Scope 3 reduction target per square meter by 2030 is the most ambitious ESG commitment in this corpus. Scope 3 for a hotel operator would include guest travel, supply chain, and potentially energy consumption at franchised/managed properties (Scope 3 Category 15: investments, or Category 1: purchased goods/services from energy vendors). We have no information about how Hyatt currently measures or reports its Scope 3 — whether it uses spend-based methods, activity-based methods, or supplier-specific data.

This matters for Clear Current because: if Hyatt's Scope 3 reporting includes the energy consumption at managed/franchised properties (which is highly likely given the "per square meter" intensity metric), then the data quality challenge Randy described — different units, fragmented accountability — is not just an internal communication problem. It is a Scope 3 data quality problem with reportable consequences.

**Research recommendation:** A targeted conversation with Hyatt's Global Sustainability team (not the Design & Construction team Randy leads) specifically about how they collect and validate Scope 3 data from managed and franchised properties.

---

### Critical Gap 6: Is the Healthcare ENERGY STAR Certification Process a Compliance Event or Just a Benchmark?

Ann Walston's ENERGY STAR program sounds operationally embedded, but there is a meaningful difference between:
(a) Informal tracking: BSMH monitors Energy Star scores internally against targets but doesn't formally certify buildings or report externally
(b) Formal certification: specific hospitals apply for and receive ENERGY STAR certification, which involves EPA verification of energy data and third-party audit

If it's (b), then the billing data quality requirement is much more stringent — EPA certification requires verified, accountant-reviewed energy consumption data. A billing error during the certification period would cause a certification application to fail or require restatement. This is a higher-stakes version of the ESG data accuracy problem.

**Research recommendation:** Ask Ann directly: "Do your hospitals hold formal ENERGY STAR certifications, or do you use ENERGY STAR Portfolio Manager for internal benchmarking? If formal, what does the verification process look like?"

---

### Critical Gap 7: Clear Current's Own ESG Footprint

For a company positioning itself as an ESG enabler, Clear Current will be asked about its own sustainability commitments, particularly in the May 2026 fundraise context. ESG-focused investors and institutional buyers will want to know: does Clear Current publish a sustainability report? Does it have its own Scope 1/2 emissions inventory? Has it committed to science-based targets?

As a seed-stage 2024 startup, the answer is almost certainly "not yet" — and that's fine. But John and Dan should be prepared for this question and have a credible "this is our plan" answer. The ESG module they build for customers should be informed by the experience of actually doing it themselves.

**Recommendation:** Consider completing a simple Scope 1/2 inventory for Clear Current (it will be tiny for a software company, primarily Scope 2 from cloud infrastructure and Scope 3 from employee commuting and business travel) and committing to annual reporting as a signal of authenticity to ESG-focused buyers.
