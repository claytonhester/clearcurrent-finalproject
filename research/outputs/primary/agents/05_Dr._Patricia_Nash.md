## 2. KEY FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The Finance Persona Is Confirmed — And It Is Structurally Reactive**

Andi Ault (UT Austin) stated this directly: "For me, the finance is really reactive." She elaborated that the one proactive lever she has is fuel hedging — everything else is building historical data into pro formas and forecasts. Ann Walston at BSMH confirmed an identical pattern without being asked: her entire procurement strategy is oriented around price predictability because she has so few levers to control costs in real time. The implication for Clear Current's product: the finance buyer is not looking for a tool that helps her _do_ energy management. She is looking for a tool that makes cost predictability more achievable and surfaces surprises before they appear in the budget variance report.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript/summary; Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 2: The Budget Calendar Has a Structural Vulnerability Clear Current Can Address**

At UT Austin, the budget is submitted in March. The City of Austin often finalizes rate changes _after_ that submission. This means Andi is forecasting against rates she doesn't yet know. She built a five-year forecast alongside the annual budget — with a known information gap built in. This is not a minor inconvenience. This is a structural planning failure that creates variance the moment the fiscal year begins.

At Texas State, the budget planning cycle runs January through May, with a September 1 fiscal year start. The same structural problem exists: gas purchasing decisions are made in March-May, often without certainty about future rate movements.

At BSMH, Ann's UPSC committee meets quarterly specifically to manage this uncertainty through layered hedging — acknowledging that the budget will never perfectly reflect actual energy costs.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript/summary; Texas State summary; Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 3: Anomaly Flagging Is the Finance Persona's Top-Stated Use Case — But Her Framing Is Different from the Ops Persona**

When asked how she would use Clear Current, Andi Ault's first and most specific answer was: "Definitely flagging anomalies would be the first process so that that is streamlined and featured and flagged rather than looking for it. That would be time saving."

This sounds like what James Norton at Texas State said (alert when a building's usage spikes) — but it is functionally different. Norton wants anomaly detection to identify equipment failures or unauthorized space use changes. Andi wants anomaly detection to catch billing errors and rate irregularities without having to go looking for them. The trigger is different; the output need is different; the downstream action is different. Norton wants to call maintenance. Andi wants to call the utility — or hand the finding to someone who will.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript; Texas State summary_

**Finding 4: The Identified-vs-Realized Savings Gap Is the Single Biggest Commercial Risk to the Finance Buyer Relationship**

Marc Spieler raised this directly: "Has the utility recognized that they made a mistake and actually given the credit back to the company?" Ann Walston provided the most vivid real-world evidence: over 10-12 years, she encountered two major meter failures. In the first ($5-6M), she got partial recovery. In the second ($1M+), full recovery required board-to-board negotiation — and she called this "incredibly rare." The line I keep returning to is her description of the mechanics: the last portion of the money had essentially gone into line losses in the settlement system, was technically irretrievable, and was only returned because of a pre-existing board-level relationship with the utility.

The implication for how Clear Current sells to finance buyers is severe: if a CFO or VP of Facilities asks "what happens after you find an error," and the answer is "we surface it and you have to fight the utility for your money," that is a very different value proposition than "we recover dollars for you." Clear Current must be clear — in every sales conversation with a finance buyer — exactly where its responsibility ends and the customer's begins.

_Sources: Marc Spieler transcript/summary; Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 5: The Finance Buyer Wants Output, Not Interface — Shareable Reports, Not Chat**

Andi Ault was explicit: "I think having maybe more intuitive reports created based around that so it's easy to share to minimize my time creating." When Clayton asked if she would want to chat with the system, she reframed: flagging anomalies and getting shareable reports. She did not express interest in querying the data herself.

When the interview explored cross-collaborative design — energy expert interacts with the tool, outputs shared to finance — her response was "Oh, absolutely. Absolutely." Not just agreement: emphatic, immediate confirmation.

This is the single clearest product design signal in the corpus for the finance persona: she does not want to be the power user. She wants to be the recipient of curated, translated, actionable outputs generated by either the AI or the energy expert.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript/summary_

**Finding 6: Rate Complexity Creates a Specific Finance-Side Billing Validation Problem That Is Distinct from Ops-Side Anomalies**

Andi described a pain point that does not appear in any other interview: the interaction between fixed costs and volumetric costs in purchased energy bills creates month-to-month rate variation that looks like an anomaly but may be structural. Her example: "Fixed costs versus the volumetric costs — that skews your rate maybe from month to month based on your actual consumption. So maybe flagging where somewhere like do we have the right meter? Are we being charged correctly? Why is this cost dramatically higher every single month than the equivalent?"

This is different from a billing error. It is a structural rate complexity issue that requires understanding the tariff structure to distinguish "we're being charged correctly for this rate design" from "something is wrong here." A tool that can parse tariff structure and explain why the blended rate is what it is — and whether that's correct — addresses a finance-specific pain point.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript/summary_

**Finding 7: The Finance Buyer's ROI Question Is "Where Do I Invest?" Not "What's Wrong?"**

Ann Walston's primary stated use case was not anomaly detection: "In all reality, I would be looking for where to invest the dollars that I have to work on energy consumption reduction. Where's the most impact?" She mentioned ROI explicitly: "What is the appropriate ROI on that investment? Is it on LEDs? Is it on tariff studies? So where's our greatest opportunity to move the needle with that investment?"

This is substantively different from Andi Ault's use case. Andi wants to catch billing errors and rate anomalies. Ann wants a prioritized capital allocation decision support tool. Both are finance-adjacent personas, but they represent different product moments: Andi is the monthly monitoring user; Ann is the strategic planning user. Clear Current needs both features but should not conflate them in sales conversations.

_Sources: Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 8: Financial System Integration Is Ranked Higher Than Energy System Capability by the Finance Persona**

When Andi Ault was asked what one task she wished could be automated, she said: "Really our financial systems more than our utility systems. Our financial systems are pretty outdated. Just getting that back-end data isn't always the easiest. The ability to build budgets and forecasts within our financial system — or have it be integrated with our financial system — would be very helpful."

This is a remarkable finding. The person who manages energy finance at UT Austin — one of the most sophisticated energy operations in the country — would rather have her energy data flow cleanly into her financial system than have better energy analysis tools. The pain is on the ERP integration side, not the analytics side.

No specific ERP systems were named by any interviewee. The Project Scope document implies Banner/Workday/PeopleSoft for higher education. Marc Spieler named SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics, JD Edwards, and QuickBooks in a different context (enterprise billing flow). These are different systems serving different parts of the same pain.

_Sources: Andi Ault transcript/summary; Marc Spieler transcript/summary; Project Scope_

**Finding 9: Pricing Stability Premium Is Explicitly Accepted by Finance Buyers**

Ann Walston stated: "We value predictability and pricing stability. Sometimes we pay a little bit of a premium for that, and we're okay with that." This is a direct statement that BSMH will pay more for certainty. This applies to energy procurement (hedging at a premium to avoid market exposure) — but it also signals something about software procurement. A finance buyer who values predictability will prefer a fixed subscription fee to a percentage-of-savings model, even if the percentage model offers higher potential value. The certainty of a known annual cost is worth a premium over the uncertainty of a variable fee.

Marc Spieler confirmed this dynamic from the vendor side: "As soon as they see you're on a trajectory to save them 20 million bucks, they're going to tell you 4 million bucks is way too expensive. They want to switch over to the fixed cost."

_Sources: Ann Walston transcript/summary; Marc Spieler transcript/summary_

**Finding 10: Billing Error Recovery Is Politically Complex and Requires Institutional Relationships — The Tool Alone Is Not Sufficient**

Ann Walston's billing error recovery narrative is the most important evidence in this corpus for how Clear Current should position to CFOs and finance executives. She recovered $1M+ — which she described as "incredibly unheard of... incredibly rare" — only because BSMH had board-level relationships with the utility. The final disputed amount required a board-member-to-board-member conversation to resolve. The underlying technical dispute about line losses was essentially irresolvable on the merits; it was resolved through relationship capital.

The implication: Clear Current cannot promise finance buyers that "we find errors and you get your money back." What it can promise is "we find errors and document them with sufficient specificity that you have the strongest possible basis for recovery." The recovery itself is not guaranteed. The documentation and evidence preparation is what Clear Current controls.

_Sources: Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 11: Healthcare Faces a Sector-Specific Capacity Charge Problem That Cannot Be Solved Through Demand Management**

Ann Walston described a tenfold increase in capacity charges in PJM territory driven by data center construction. She explicitly acknowledged that hospitals cannot shed load to manage demand peaks: "I'd really like you to do it after five — that's really a tough thing to manage versus when I worked outside of healthcare, we literally could push and pull our operations to manage the capacity charge." The 15-minute interval demand peak mechanic that sets annual capacity charges is financially significant and structurally unmanageable for hospitals.

The finance implication: in healthcare, the value proposition for capacity charge intelligence is not "reduce your peak." It is "understand your capacity exposure before the bill arrives, quantify the annual financial impact, and document when capacity charges represent an anomalous event vs. expected baseline." This is a monitoring and documentation play, not an optimization play. Finance buyers in healthcare will buy monitoring. They will not buy false promises of demand reduction.

_Sources: Ann Walston transcript/summary_

**Finding 12: The Single-Energy-Manager Staffing Model Creates a Portfolio Prioritization Gap That Finance Cannot Fill Alone**

BSMH has one certified energy manager for 36 hospitals. He physically travels to individual sites. Ann's use case — "where do I invest my dollars for maximum impact" — is structurally unanswerable with current tools because no system aggregates Energy Star score trajectory, billing anomaly frequency, capacity charge exposure, and equipment age signals into a ranked portfolio view. This gap exists at the intersection of finance and operations: the finance persona (Ann) has the budget authority; the ops persona (the single energy manager) has the technical knowledge; neither has the data synthesis to answer the prioritization question.

Clear Current's opportunity is to be the system that answers the question neither persona can answer alone.

_Sources: Ann Walston transcript/summary_

### 4.2 Finance Persona Section — Product Module Recommendations

**Module 1: AI Budgeting Command Center**

_Problem Definition:_ Finance users managing institutional energy spend face a structural forecasting challenge: they must submit annual budgets before utility rates are finalized, before regulatory cases are resolved, and before weather patterns are known. The result is that budgets are built on assumptions that are wrong before the fiscal year begins. This is not a failure of the finance team — it is a structural information gap. _(Evidence: Andi Ault transcript, budget timing problem; Ann Walston UPSC hedging strategy)_

_Target User Persona:_ Senior Associate Director of Finance (UT Austin model), VP of Facilities Management (BSMH model), Energy Finance Analyst. These are people who manage energy spend as a cost center, not people who optimize building systems.

_Business Value:_ Reduces budget variance by providing rate intelligence earlier in the planning cycle. Enables five-year forecasts with quantified uncertainty ranges rather than point estimates. Closes the regulatory intelligence gap between when rates are filed and when they are finalized.

_Frequency of Use:_ High-intensity seasonal use (October-March for higher education; year-round for healthcare given quarterly UPSC cadence), with monthly maintenance during the fiscal year for variance tracking.

_Strategic Differentiation:_ EnergyCAP stores historical data and generates projections, but does not monitor regulatory rate cases or provide forward-looking market intelligence. No current tool combines rate case monitoring, hedging strategy support, and budget variance reporting in a single finance-oriented interface.

_Finance Persona Design Requirements:_

- NO chat interface as primary entry point — push notifications and pre-generated reports
- Export to common financial system formats (CSV at minimum; API integration with SAP/Oracle/Workday as roadmap)
- Tiered output: executive summary + detailed analysis from same data
- Quote: "The ability to build budgets and forecasts within our financial system — or have it be integrated with our financial system — would be very helpful." (Andi Ault)

**Module 2: Anomaly Investigation Assistant — Finance Layer**

_Finance Persona Design Requirements (distinct from Ops layer):_

The anomaly investigation assistant has two user personas with different needs. The ops user (James Norton archetype) wants to know about equipment failures and usage spikes in real time. The finance user (Andi Ault archetype) wants to know about billing errors, rate irregularities, and fixed/volumetric cost blending anomalies on a monthly bill review cycle.

The finance user does NOT want to investigate the anomaly. She wants the anomaly flagged, the financial implication quantified, and the finding packaged into a shareable report. The investigation is done by either the AI or the energy expert; the finance user receives the translated output.

Specific finance-side anomaly types to detect (from Andi Ault's explicit description):

- Fixed/volumetric rate blending that skews the average rate month-over-month in ways that may or may not indicate an error
- Comparable-building rate deviation: "Why is this cost dramatically higher every single month than the equivalent?" — implies a cross-portfolio benchmarking capability
- Wrong meter or wrong rate schedule: flag when a facility appears to be on an incorrect rate structure given its known usage profile

_Quote for module documentation:_ "Fixed costs versus the volumetric costs — that skews your rate maybe from month to month based on your actual consumption. So maybe flagging where somewhere like do we have the right meter? Are we being charged correctly?" (Andi Ault)

**Module 3: Savings Opportunity Ranking Engine — Finance Layer**

_Finance Persona Design Requirements:_

Ann Walston's primary stated use case is ROI-based capital allocation: "Where are the where's the most impact? Where's our greatest opportunity to move the needle with that investment?" She has capital allocated for energy efficiency investment and needs to know where to deploy it.

This module must answer the question the VP of Facilities cannot currently answer: rank all facilities by efficiency opportunity, accounting for Energy Star score trajectory, billing anomaly frequency, capacity charge exposure, and equipment age signals. The output is a prioritized work queue.

The finance user's specific version of this module is a capital allocation decision support tool:

- Input: available capital budget, efficiency investment categories, facility portfolio
- Output: ranked list of facilities by expected ROI on efficiency investment, with supporting evidence
- Format: executive-ready, exportable, comparable across sites

This is different from what a single energy manager can produce because it synthesizes financial and operational data at portfolio scale — which a human cannot do across 36 hospitals with available staffing.

_Quote: "In all reality, I would be looking for where to invest the dollars that I have to work on energy consumption reduction. Where's the most impact?" (Ann Walston)_

**Module 4: Executive Brief Generator**

_This module has no explicit primary source requesting it — it is an inference from the design principles Andi Ault articulated._ I will flag this as inferred rather than directly evidenced.

However, the design requirements are clearly implied:

Andi Ault described "providing reports to our CFO so that they have information they need for decision making." She described preparing information at different levels for different audiences. She described wanting "intuitive reports that are easy to share" to minimize her time creating them. ### 4.3 CFO-Facing Business Case Language

**The Core Business Case — How to Frame It**

The finance buyer's business case is not: "Our AI finds errors in your energy bills." That framing is accurate but insufficient — and Marc Spieler identified exactly why: the buyer will ask "has the utility actually given the money back?" and if the answer is uncertain, the case collapses.

The correct framing for the finance buyer is: **"Clear Current gives your finance team the forward visibility and documentation capability that closes the gap between what you're spending and what you should be spending — whether that gap is a billing error, a wrong rate schedule, an avoidable demand charge, or a budget that was built before rates were finalized."**

This framing has three components that map to the evidence:

1. **Forward visibility** — addresses Andi Ault's regulatory intelligence gap (budget submitted before rates are finalized)
2. **Documentation capability** — addresses Ann Walston's recovery problem (you need documented evidence to recover dollars; the documentation is what Clear Current controls)
3. **Closing the gap** — is broader than "finding errors" and encompasses rate optimization, tariff selection, and capital allocation

**The Three Categories of Value — Finance Language**

_Category 1: Cost Avoidance (Proactive)_
Clear Current's regulatory intelligence and rate trend features allow finance teams to build budgets with better rate assumptions, reducing budget variance in the first year of a rate change. Value = (annual energy spend) × (average rate change surprise as % of spend) × (budget accuracy improvement factor). This is conservative, documentable, and does not require utility credit recovery.

_Category 2: Error Recovery (Reactive)_
When Clear Current identifies billing errors — wrong rate schedule, meter multiplier errors, capacity charge anomalies — and the customer successfully recovers those dollars, that is direct, one-time financial recovery. Ann Walston's experience shows that recovery is hard and rare at the $1M+ level without board-level relationships. Clear Current's value here is making the documentation case as strong as possible and providing the data that supports the recovery conversation. The business case should be honest: "We can't guarantee recovery, but we can give you the strongest possible evidentiary basis for the claim."

_Category 3: Capital Allocation Optimization (Strategic)_
Ann Walston's use case: if Clear Current can rank efficiency investment opportunities by expected ROI across a facility portfolio, the finance team can allocate capital more effectively. Even a 10-15% improvement in capital allocation efficiency across a large portfolio produces significant returns — and this is a value category that does not depend on utility cooperation for realization.

**The ROI Calculation for Finance Buyers**

Based on the evidence in this corpus, I can construct a reasonable (not precise) ROI framework:

- UT Austin spends approximately $20M annually on natural gas alone. Total energy spend including purchased electricity is likely $30-50M annually. A 1% improvement in billing accuracy or rate optimization = $300-500K annually.
- BSMH with 36 hospitals: a healthcare system of this scale likely spends $50-150M annually on utilities. Ann's two billing error incidents alone — if Clear Current had been in place — represent potential $6-7M in recoverable value, though recovery was partial.
- The single energy manager serving 36 hospitals is not able to do systematic billing validation. The cost of not having better tools is measured in the claims that are never made, not just the ones that are partially recovered.

**The Pricing Psychology Implication**

Ann Walston: "We value predictability and pricing stability. Sometimes we pay a little bit of a premium for that, and we're okay with that."

Marc Spieler: "As soon as they see you're on a trajectory to save them 20 million bucks, they're going to tell you 4 million bucks is way too expensive."

These two data points together suggest: the finance buyer who is most committed to budgetary predictability will prefer a flat annual subscription to a percentage-of-savings model — even if the subscription is priced at a premium to the expected savings share.

Clear Current's pricing structure for the finance persona should be: fixed annual subscription, with transparent pricing that a finance buyer can include in their annual budget without uncertainty. The savings-sharing model can work for initial pilots (it reduces perceived risk), but the path to recurring revenue runs through a subscription that the finance buyer can plan around.
