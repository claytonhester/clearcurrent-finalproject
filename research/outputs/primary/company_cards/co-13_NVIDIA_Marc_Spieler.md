## THEIR BIGGEST STRUGGLES (verbatim evidence)

Marc's struggles are strategic and ecosystem-level, not operational energy management. His insights reflect what he has observed across hundreds of enterprise energy buyers, including NVIDIA's own profile as a large energy consumer.

**1. The complexity of large-scale energy billing makes manual auditing impossible**
_"A lot of these companies have just started just paying their bills without even checking for errors anymore. Like it's just so hard to do that and it costs so much money that they just pay the bill and say whatever."_
Marc frames this as industry-wide and implies NVIDIA, as a large energy purchaser, is not exempt.

**2. Spreadsheets are the dominant "solution" and they always have errors**
_"Your dad's the king of this stuff, right? He's got these elaborate spreadsheets that nobody else could possibly figure out to do all this calculation... when he and I go in there and we start working stuff, we always find bugs. Right. Or something or whatever, you know, and it's because he's one man and he's got 1000 things on his plate."_
Marc understands intimately that the manual spreadsheet workflow produces systematic inaccuracies.

**3. Savings-sharing deals become politically untenable as savings scale**
_"As soon as they see you're on a trajectory to save them 20 million bucks, they're going to tell you 4 million bucks is way too expensive. They want to switch over to the fixed cost."_
Marc has watched this pricing problem destroy deals. He flags it as a structural risk for Clear Current's commercial model.

**4. No one knows whether utilities are underbilling or overbilling — and nobody is looking**
_"If I'm Target or if I'm Kroger and my bill's cheaper than if I look at my bill and I audit it and they charged me $1,000,000 last month and it should have been 1.3 — chances are I'm not going to say anything."_
Marc identifies the asymmetric information problem: companies only complain when overbilled, never when underbilled, so the true error rate in both directions is invisible.

**5. The go-to-market for AI energy startups stalls because they target the wrong buyer**
_"You potentially go to a utility and said listen, take off the people's names. Let us audit 100 of your bills. We'll come back and tell you if they're inaccurate."_
Marc sees startups — including those in NVIDIA's own Inception program — go after end customers exclusively, when the utility side and the billing software stack represent faster, higher-leverage distribution paths.

**6. Startups using NVIDIA technology fail to activate the co-marketing advantage they've already earned**
_"Go find out which cloud you're running on... Are you using NVIDIA or whatever... Get involved in their startup programs... could you go to those companies and say, hey, we're a startup in your program — here's the problem we solve. Can you introduce us to your CFO?"_
