## THEIR BIGGEST STRUGGLES (verbatim evidence)

**1. Finance is structurally reactive with almost no proactive levers**
_"For me, the finance is really reactive. We can take other measures that are proactive like for instance hedging our fuel. We buy $20 million in natural gas every year plus or minus depending on pricing at the time."_ Hedging is the only intentional forward-looking tool she has. Everything else — bill validation, variance review, cost tracking — happens after the fact.

**2. Budget submitted before external rates are known**
_"We generally don't know [City of Austin rates] until after we've turned in our budget, but you know, try to get some insight."_ The organization is forecasting in the dark on a key cost input every single year. This is a structural gap, not a one-time failure.

**3. Fixed vs. volumetric cost blending distorts rate accuracy month to month**
_"Fixed costs versus the volumetric costs — that skews your rate maybe from month to month based on your actual consumption. So maybe flagging where we have the wrong meter, are we being charged correctly? Why is this cost dramatically higher every single month than the equivalent place?"_ This is a finance-specific error type — not a wrong meter read, but a structural rate anomaly created by how fixed and variable charges interact at different consumption levels.

**4. Financial systems are outdated and disconnected**
_"Our financial systems are pretty outdated. Just getting that back-end data isn't always the easiest. The ability to build budgets and forecasts within our financial system — or have it be integrated with our financial system — would be very helpful."_ Her #1 automation wish was not about energy tools at all — it was about the financial infrastructure that energy data has to feed into.

**5. Metering granularity limits financial analysis — and upgrading has unclear ROI**
_"We don't have great granularity. It's just building-level metering because nobody's looking at those statements except for us — we're billing ourselves. Could we have better granularity? If we funded more metering we could, but what is the cost benefit?"_ The finance team cannot do sub-building cost attribution on E&G buildings because the metering infrastructure does not support it, and the business case for expanding metering has never been made.

**6. Anomalies require active hunting; nothing surfaces them automatically**
_"Flagging anomalies would be the first process so that that is streamlined and featured and flagged rather than looking for it. That would be time saving."_ The current workflow requires a human to go looking for problems. There is no automated alert or exception reporting layer — anomalies are found, if at all, through manual report review.
