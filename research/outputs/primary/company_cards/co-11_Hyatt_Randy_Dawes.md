> _"Given the structure of Hyatt, we have multiple partners within the operators and owners of hotels. With the magnitude of these differences the collection of the data is listed as a requirement to each hotel."_

No single party owns the full energy picture. Corporate mandates reporting; properties execute it differently; third-party aggregators serve as the bridge. The accountability gap is structural.

**2. Metrics normalization — data is transferable, but not meaningful**

> _"The world today has many different metrics for measuring these critical items. Thinking how can we get everyone on the same platform for these metrics, similarity would allow for easier understanding of what each unit of water means, and what each unit of energy means. This is the single most substantive finding. Corporate can move the data; they cannot make it legible to the person actually using the energy. The gap is translation, not transmission.

**4. Corporate has no visibility into billing errors or rate discrepancies**

> _"At the corporate level we do not review these or get access to that."_

Billing accuracy problems are invisible to Hyatt corporate by design. They live entirely at the property or ownership level. This is not a gap Hyatt corporate is trying to close — it's a structural handoff.

**5. No common language between corporate goals and property-level behavior**
Implied throughout: Hyatt's 2030 goals (27.5% Scope 1+2 reduction, 53% Scope 3 reduction per square meter) are corporate commitments, but the people consuming the energy at individual properties don't see those goals translated into terms that drive behavior.
