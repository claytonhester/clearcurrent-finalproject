## THEIR BIGGEST STRUGGLES (verbatim evidence)

**1. District energy metering is broken — and EnergyCAP can't actually fix it**
Texas State generates steam and chilled water at central plants and meters each receiving building. Blending that internal sub-meter data with utility meter data from the City of San Marcos is unsolved.

> _"EnergyCAP tells us that they can do that stuff, but it's harder than it sounds."_
> _"Metering is really hard. Especially in district energy systems like most universities have. Hospitals and universities are kind of typically district energy places. And it's really hard to meter, to sub-meter, all of that stuff."_

**2. Meter data quality is a constant, manual battle**
Meters drop off, feeds fail, installations degrade. There is no automated system catching these failures.

> _"It's not like once you install a meter and program it, it's always going to be perfect. That's just not the reality. The operations change. Maybe it wasn't installed correctly, maybe the data drop fails. It's just a constant, like trying to keep up with, you know, did we get that data?"_

**3. Bill processing expertise just walked out the door**
Their long-tenured bill processing employee left after 10–15 years. They are not currently using EnergyCAP's bill processing module and are scrambling to decide whether to outsource.

> _"It's really complex. Things change. And then our system of gathering that data and putting it into our database is also a little wonky."_
> _"We had a weird multiplier happen in one of our bills. That's why you keep auditing your bills... We've been spending 3,500 gallons every month, and now it's 35,000. Something's wrong."_

**4. Energy ops and finance want different things from the same data — and neither wins**
Andee wants demand and usage intelligence. Finance wants cost and variance. They are pulling from the same dataset in opposite directions.

> _"One of my issues is being on the utility side. I care more about the demand and the information about our usage, but the bill processing folks care more about the bottom line numbers, like how much did it cost and how much did it cost last month? And so we're competing about what kind of data we get from that."_

**5. Cost allocation is based on square footage, not actual consumption — killing conservation incentives**
Departments have no reason to conserve because they don't pay for what they actually use.

> _"If we had better information about usage in our buildings, we can charge based on usage... What happens from that is you can encourage efficiency or conservation. It's hard to drive that if you're charging people based on square footage."_

**6. No system to correlate events with energy spikes or prepare in advance**
Carl has no visibility into how a Saturday concert or large campus event will affect consumption until after it happens.

> _"If I knew, if I had event schedules and know that this stuff is coming up, we could prepare for it. If the music department's gonna have a concert and we're gonna have estimated X amount of people on campus, that would give me a huge amount of information on how to prep for it."_
