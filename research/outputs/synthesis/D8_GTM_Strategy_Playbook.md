## SECTION 3: Sales Process & Cycle

### Who Is Actually in the Room

**What the research says:** Phil Combs provided the most operationally specific buying hierarchy in the corpus, based on 20 years of enterprise energy sales:

> **Phil Combs, Senior Digital Services Engineer, Trane Technologies:** _"If those three entities are in the CEO's and the board's ear, and they provide a compelling story of why, then the CEOs and the board will do it. But if you and I go and talk to them, maybe, but probably not."_

The three entities: energy/sustainability managers, directors of facilities, and finance. The C-suite does not self-initiate energy software evaluation. They approve it when the three-stakeholder coalition builds the case.

**Practical implication for Clear Current:** Every sales process needs three simultaneous conversations — the energy manager champion (product value, anomaly detection), the finance gatekeeper (ROI, budget integration, the Andi Ault budget gap problem), and IT security (data handling, SOC 2 status). Missing any one of these creates a deal that stalls after verbal commitment.

### The Buying Stages (Evidence-Grounded)

**Stage 1 — Trigger (0–2 weeks):** A billing event, contract expiration, or budget cycle creates a felt need. Clear Current's job at this stage is to be findable at the moment of the trigger. For universities: this means being in the awareness phase before January (budget season) and before March (budget submission). The GTM calendar must reverse-engineer from these deadlines.

**Stage 2 — Champion Engagement (2–6 weeks):** The energy manager or facilities director sees a demo. The demo must surface a plausible error or opportunity within the first 15 minutes using the prospect's own data or a realistic synthetic analog. Phil Combs' bar is the right standard: _"faster and more insightful than my well-structured Excel workbook."_ If the demo does not beat Excel in the room, the champion has no internal case to make.

**Stage 3 — Internal Coalition (6–12 weeks):** The champion builds the internal case. Clear Current's job is to arm them with materials that speak to finance (ROI, dollar magnitude, payback period) and IT (security documentation, data handling policy, SOC 2 status). Phil Combs named IT as a blocking force — prepare for this gate explicitly. The IT security conversation is not a sales conversation; it is a compliance documentation review. Prepare a one-page data handling summary that answers the questions IT will ask before they ask them.

**Stage 4 — Procurement (12–24 weeks for universities):** Universities above certain dollar thresholds require competitive solicitation. Texas State's EnergyCAP contract at $50K–$75K/year may sit below that threshold, but $120K/year may trigger it depending on institutional rules. Know the threshold before proposing. The University of Wisconsin RFP (issued September, October deadline) is the documented template — it confirms that universities issue formal RFPs for energy management software.

**Stage 5 — Pilot-to-Paid Conversion (90-day pilot + contract):** The conversion event should include both the energy manager champion and the finance gatekeeper. The presentation structure: (1) errors or opportunities identified with dollar magnitude, (2) what the regulatory intelligence brief would have told them before their March budget submission, (3) the subscription ROI at $120K/year against the identified value. Do not let the pilot end without scheduling this meeting.

### Deal Killers — Documented

1. **IT says no:** Phil Combs' direct evidence. Pre-empt with SOC 2 documentation before the IT gate is raised. Do not wait to be asked.
2. **Finance doesn't see the number:** If the energy manager is the only person who sees the pilot output, the budget owner has no reason to fund the subscription. Every pilot deliverable must include a dollar-denominated executive summary.
3. **Procurement cycle extends beyond the fundraise window:** If a university requires competitive solicitation, the earliest realistic signed contract may be after May 2026. Get LOI or commitment letters as fundraise evidence if the full contract cannot close in time.
4. **The savings-sharing ceiling:** Marc Spieler's documented warning: _"As soon as they see you're on a trajectory to save them 20 million bucks, they're going to tell you 4 million bucks is way too expensive."_ If using contingency pricing as the pilot entry, define the fee structure before the savings magnitude is visible to the customer. Cap the contingency or pre-define the subscription transition point.

> **Marc Spieler, Senior Managing Director, Global Energy Industry, NVIDIA:** _"The concept's great. The question is, how often are these bills wrong? If the answer is not very often, then you know. But based on what you're telling me, they're wrong a lot and you've had some big wins. My thing is go demonstrate those wins."_

### Timeline Reality Check

The GTM Doc documents $100K–$500K enterprise SaaS deals at 6–12 months, with university procurement extending toward the ceiling. John's observed five-month revenue recognition lag is the floor. The sequencing problem is real: if pilots initiated in the last six months close on the documented timeline, they close after May 2026. This means the fundraise evidence package must include pilots that are already 4–6 months in (capable of May closing) plus LOI or commitment letters from later-stage prospects.

## SECTION 4: Pricing Model Recommendation

### The Evidence Base

**EnergyCAP benchmark:** $40–52/meter/year, documented. Texas State actual spend: $50K–$75K/year for 1,500 meters. This is the incumbent pricing anchor every university buyer already knows.

**Clear Current implied pricing:** $120K/year at 130 locations implies approximately $923/location/year. Against EnergyCAP's per-meter pricing at 1,500 meters, Clear Current is priced 2–4x higher on a per-meter basis.

**UtiliSave contingency benchmark:** 40% of recovered errors for 36 months — documented. This is the high end of the contingency fee market.

**Marc Spieler's savings-sharing warning:** As recovered value becomes visible, percentage-based fees become politically uncomfortable. The transition from contingency to subscription must be designed into the contract, not left to renegotiation.

### Recommended Pricing Architecture

**Tier 1 — Pilot (90 days, higher ed ICP):**

- Fee: 20% contingency on billing errors identified and documented during the pilot window
- Rationale: Zero upfront commitment, outcome-dependent, below UtiliSave's 40% benchmark, buyer-friendly for a 90-day window
- Cap: Maximum contingency fee of $30K during pilot (prevents sticker shock when errors are large; preserves the subscription conversion)
- Conversion: At day 75, present the subscription economics — if pilot fee exceeded $15K, the subscription saves money on an annualized basis at $120K/year vs. continuing contingency

**Tier 2 — Annual Subscription (post-pilot, standard):**

- Fee: $80K–$150K/year flat, depending on portfolio size and meter count
- Anchor: $120K/year for a 100–150 location portfolio (higher ed: typically 80–120 buildings)
- Value framing: Not per-meter pricing (EnergyCAP's frame). Position as a percentage of utility spend: at $10M annual energy spend, $120K is 1.2% — recoverable from a single identified error cluster in most portfolios
- Include: Rate Analyst (billing audit), Energy HQ dashboard (anomaly alerting), regulatory intelligence brief (quarterly or triggered by rate case filing)

**Tier 3 — Enterprise Expansion:**

- Fee: $200K–$400K/year for portfolios above 200 locations or above $25M annual energy spend
- Add-ons: Deal Center integration for procurement workflow, custom regulatory monitoring scope, dedicated customer success resource
- Gate: Requires a complete documented error recovery proof point before pricing this tier — Spieler's identified-vs.-realized problem applies most sharply here

**Pricing rule (mandatory):** Do not structure contingency fees where the customer can see the total savings magnitude before the fee structure is fixed. Marc Spieler's trap is real and documented.

**What interviewees said about willingness to pay:** No interviewee named a price point directly. Indirect evidence: Texas State pays $50K–$75K/year to EnergyCAP for a tool with documented UX complaints. If Clear Current can demonstrate 2x the value at 2x the price ($120K), the ROI case is defensible — but only with documented error recovery.

## SECTION 6: Data Onboarding Playbook

> **Source:** This section integrates Nina Patel's data architecture analysis with primary interview evidence. The five obstacles are drawn from documented interview findings, not general SaaS onboarding literature.

### The Onboarding Reality

**What is happening and why it matters:** The onboarding friction at every institution in the corpus is structural, not technical. It is not about API availability — it is about the accumulated complexity of years of disconnected meter data, bill formats, and internal cost allocation logic that predates any software platform. Winning on onboarding means being the vendor that treats this complexity as a service, not a prerequisite the customer must solve before the product works.

> **Andee Chamberlain, Texas State University:** The data gathering system is described as _"a little wonky"_ — with recurring data drop failures: _"Maybe the data drop fails. It's just a constant, like trying to keep up with, you know, did we get that data?"_

This is a product design problem and a sales problem simultaneously. If Clear Current's onboarding requires clean, consistent data feeds before it can deliver value, Texas State is a bad-fit customer. If Clear Current's onboarding is designed to work with messy, incomplete, inconsistently formatted data, Texas State is a perfect-fit customer.

### The Five Onboarding Obstacles (Evidence-Based)

**Obstacle 1: Bill format variation across utilities**

_Source:_ Phil Combs (primary), Adam Zavadsky (primary), Nina Patel data analysis.

> **Phil Combs, Trane Technologies:** _"Utilities are still so varied and different. It can be frustrating because what they put on a bill and the format they put it on a bill, it can just vary... [earlier AI tools] really stumbled with it."_

ENGIE Impact manually processes approximately 30,000 municipality-issued paper bills — the scale of format variation is structural, not edge-case. Phil's own assessment of modern LLMs: _"I have no doubts — as things have gone along now it's light years different in the capability to not only do the OCR part, but then the insights it'll get."_

_Playbook response:_ Lead onboarding with the bill upload experience. Demonstrate that the system handles messy, multi-format bills in the first session — before the customer has to trust the platform with their full portfolio. The first 30 minutes of onboarding should produce a visible output from a real bill. This is the confidence moment that determines whether the energy manager becomes an internal champion or a skeptic.

_CC Engine maturity note:_ AI-assisted invoice ingestion exists and is shipped. The onboarding playbook should lean into this as a demonstrated advantage over EnergyCAP (which requires structured data upload) and against ENGIE Impact (which uses human-reviewed OCR for paper bills). Do not overstate automation for resolution — the product assists manual completion; it does not file disputes unattended.

**Obstacle 2: Meter-to-site mapping at scale**

_Source:_ Ann Walston (Bon Secours) data architecture analysis — most severe in healthcare, moderate in higher ed.

Ann Walston documented that establishing which meter feeds which wing of which hospital took _over a year_ for Bon Secours. The Texas State district energy system has the same underlying complexity: steam and chilled water produced centrally, distributed to 98 buildings, with sub-meters that must reconcile with central plant production data.

_Playbook response:_ Do not assume clean meter-to-site mapping exists. Build a site association intake process that begins with the customer's existing documentation (floor plans, utility account numbers, prior EnergyCAP exports) and supplements with Clear Current's site association logic. The CC Engine's associate-invoices-with-sites capability is the lead automation story here — but scope this as an ongoing refinement process, not a one-time onboarding step. Set the expectation: full meter mapping accuracy improves over the first 90 days as the system learns the customer's portfolio.

_Product context alignment:_ Site association is the lead automation capability in CC Engine maturity — actively improved for scale and edge cases. Frame it honestly in the onboarding conversation: "We will get you to 90% mapping accuracy in 30 days and 98%+ by day 90 as we process more of your historical bills."

**Obstacle 3: Dual-audience data access and permissions**

_Source:_ Andee Chamberlain and Andi Ault (primary), cross-confirmed.

The energy manager and the finance gatekeeper need different views of the same underlying data. EnergyCAP doesn't solve this — it's described in G2/Capterra reviews as serving neither audience well from a single platform.

_Playbook response:_ During onboarding intake, ask explicitly: "Who in your organization needs to see this data, and what question are they trying to answer?" Then configure two named views: the operations view (demand, usage, anomalies — for Andee) and the finance view (cost, budget variance, regulatory forecast — for Andi). This is not a product development requirement — it is a configuration and presentation decision. Make it a formal onboarding step, not an afterthought.

_Product tie-in:_ Energy HQ for the operational view; Rate Analyst outputs formatted as finance-ready budget numbers. The chat layer can serve both personas with different prompt starting points — include persona-specific suggested prompts in the onboarding setup.

**Obstacle 4: Data pipeline reliability and staff turnover**

_Source:_ Andee Chamberlain (primary) — the bill processing employee at Texas State recently departed, exposing a data pipeline gap.

Customers with staff turnover in energy-adjacent roles (bill processors, facilities coordinators) have interrupted data pipelines. The new person doesn't know the system. The data stops flowing. The platform produces stale or incomplete outputs.

_Playbook response:_ Build a data health dashboard (a simple internal view, not customer-facing at first) that flags when a customer's data feed has gone stale — no new bills ingested in 30 days, for example. Make data health a customer success metric, not just an IT monitoring metric. When a feed goes stale, the customer success team contacts the customer before they notice the gap themselves. This is a retention behavior, not a technical feature.

**Obstacle 5: The identified-vs.-realized gap in onboarding expectations**

_Source:_ Marc Spieler (primary) — the single most important commercial risk in the research corpus.

> **Marc Spieler, NVIDIA:** _"Has the utility recognized that they made a mistake and actually given the credit back to the company, or they still — they've identified the issue, they can justify the issue, but nobody's gotten a refund on their payment yet?"_

This is an onboarding expectation problem as much as a product maturity problem. If the customer expects the platform to autonomously dispute billing errors and recover credits, and the product currently assists that process (human-in-the-loop for resolution), the gap between expectation and delivery creates churn.

_Playbook response:_ Set expectations explicitly in the onboarding conversation: "We will identify every material billing error in your portfolio and document it with enough specificity to dispute it. The dispute process currently involves your team contacting the utility directly, with our documentation as the evidence package. We will walk you through that process for the first dispute." This framing is accurate, honest, and positions the human-in-the-loop behavior as a feature (you stay in control) rather than a limitation.

_Hard product rule:_ Do not describe autonomous dispute filing or closed-loop credit recovery as a shipped capability. The product assists; it does not do this unattended. This must be consistent in every onboarding conversation and in every pilot kickoff.

## SECTION 7: The First 90-Day Sales Playbook

> **What this section is:** Week-by-week guidance for what John and Dan should actually do to convert pipeline to pilots to paying customers before the May 2026 fundraise. This is structured around the research-validated sales cycle stages and the university buying calendar.

**Precondition before starting the clock:** Close one documented error recovery loop. This is not a 90-day task — it is a prerequisite for weeks 1–4 to work. Before John makes a single outbound call, there must be one case study that answers Spieler's question: error identified → utility contacted → credit issued → dollars returned to customer. If this does not exist, weeks 1–4 will produce interest but not pilots.

---

### Weeks 1–2: ICP Qualification and Pipeline Prioritization

**Monday of Week 1:** Map every current pilot against the ICP criteria defined in Section 1. Score each on: (1) meter count (500+ preferred), (2) district energy system (yes/no), (3) EnergyCAP incumbent (yes/no), (4) budget season timing (is March budget deadline imminent?), (5) identified error value to date. Stack-rank the pilots by conversion readiness.

**Task:** Identify the one or two pilots that are closest to the conversion event — the ones where the billing audit has already surfaced an error cluster with documented dollar magnitude. These are the priority closes.

**Task:** For every pilot where the finance gatekeeper has not yet seen the output, schedule a joint meeting with the energy manager champion and the finance gatekeeper before the end of Week 2. Present the billing audit finding in dollar terms. This is the conversion setup meeting.

**Task:** Pull the two or three prospects who are in early pipeline but not yet in a pilot. Do they map to the ICP? Do they have a January–March budget trigger that makes them ready now? If yes, schedule a discovery call in Week 2.

**Target: End of Week 2:** ICP-scored pilot list, at least two joint champion-finance meetings scheduled, at least two prospect discovery calls booked.

---

### Weeks 3–4: Champion Arming and IT Gate Preparation

**Task:** For each priority pilot, prepare a one-page billing audit summary formatted for the finance gatekeeper — not for the energy manager. The format: dollar value of identified errors, methodology in two sentences, what the regulatory intelligence brief would have said before their March budget submission, subscription ROI at annual contract value. No technical language. No product screenshots. Just the business case.

**Task:** Prepare the IT security documentation package before being asked. Contents: data handling policy (one page), SOC 2 status (current status or roadmap with timeline), utility data classification (what data is stored, where, how long), access controls (who at Clear Current can see customer data). Phil Combs documented this gate explicitly. Do not let IT kill a deal that the champion has already committed to.

**Task:** Ask each energy manager champion directly: "What would your IT team want to know about our platform?" Use their answer to customize the security package. This question demonstrates enterprise readiness and surfaces the specific objection before it becomes a deal blocker.

**Target: End of Week 4:** Finance-formatted billing audit summaries prepared for all priority pilots. IT security package complete. At least one pilot where the finance gatekeeper has received the summary and responded affirmatively.

---

### Weeks 5–8: Conversion Events and Contract Structuring

**Task:** Schedule the pilot conclusion meeting for every 90-day pilot ending before May. This meeting must include: (1) energy manager champion, (2) finance gatekeeper, (3) ideally, the facilities director if they are in the buying coalition. Agenda: (1) errors/opportunities identified with dollar magnitude, (2) regulatory intelligence brief preview, (3) subscription proposal.

**Task:** Present the subscription economics explicitly: "At $120K/year, the cost of the subscription is equivalent to [X% of identified errors in the pilot]. If we identify one error cluster of comparable magnitude per quarter, the subscription pays for itself in the first quarter." Make the math explicit. Don't assume they'll do it themselves.

**Task:** For pilots where the contract value will trigger university competitive solicitation, pivot immediately to LOI/commitment letter — ask the champion to provide a letter of intent to enter a formal agreement, contingent on completing procurement requirements. This is fundraise evidence even if the formal contract cannot close by May.

**Task:** For prospects who are in discovery (not yet in a pilot), propose a free 30-day billing audit on 3–5 bills as a no-commitment entry point. The deliverable: one Rate Analyst output with a documented finding. The conversion: if the finding has dollar value, propose the 90-day pilot structure. This is the lowest-friction entry point with no pricing objection.

**Target: End of Week 8:** At least two pilot conclusion meetings completed, at least one subscription proposal delivered, at least one LOI or commitment letter in hand.

## SECTION 8: Key Metrics to Track

> **Standard:** These metrics are chosen because they directly map to the questions investors will ask in May 2026 diligence and the GTM hypotheses that need to be validated or refuted within 60 days. They are not vanity metrics.

---

**Metric 1: Pilot-to-Paid Conversion Rate**

_Why it matters:_ Marc Spieler's identified-vs.-realized question becomes an investor question in diligence. If 12 pilots produce zero paying customers, the product-market fit story collapses. If 12 pilots produce 3–4 paying customers at $120K/year, the ARR story begins.

_Target:_ ≥33% pilot-to-paid conversion within 90 days of pilot conclusion. SaaStr benchmark (cited in GTM Doc) is 70%+ for strong PMF signals — treat 33% as the floor for fundraise viability.

_Leading indicator:_ Track the joint champion-finance meeting rate. If the energy manager champion has not introduced the finance gatekeeper to the platform within 60 days of pilot start, the deal is unlikely to convert. This is an early warning signal, not a lagging indicator.

---

**Metric 2: Days to First Documented Finding**

_Why it matters:_ The billing audit entry point only works if the platform can surface a finding before the customer's attention moves elsewhere. If the onboarding process takes 45 days to produce the first finding, the champion loses momentum.

_Target:_ ≤30 days from pilot kickoff to first documented billing error or opportunity, with dollar magnitude. This should be a product design constraint, not just a sales aspiration.

_Leading indicator:_ Track the percentage of pilots that produce a Rate Analyst output with a dollar finding within the first 30 days. Any pilot below 30 days is a onboarding process success; any pilot above 45 days needs a process investigation.

---

**Metric 3: Identified-vs.-Realized Dollar Ratio**

_Why it matters:_ This is Spieler's question operationalized. Clear Current currently identifies errors. The ratio of identified error value to actually-recovered (credit issued or adjustment confirmed by utility) value is the most important commercial credibility metric in the portfolio.

_Target:_ At least one documented recovery (credit issued or adjustment confirmed) by May 2026. The absolute dollar amount matters less than the proof that the loop closes. One $15K recovery is worth more for fundraising than $10M in identified-but-unrealized errors.

_Track:_ By customer and by error type. Which error categories actually result in utility acknowledgment and credit? Which are identified but disputed? This intelligence shapes product prioritization.

---

**Metric 4: Sales Cycle Length by ICP**

_Why it matters:_ The 6–12 month benchmark for $100K–$500K enterprise SaaS is documented, but Clear Current's actual cycle may differ — shorter if the billing audit entry point accelerates trust, longer if procurement gates extend the timeline.

_Target:_ Track days from first discovery call to signed contract for every deal that closes. Target: ≤120 days for university ICP (given the May fundraise constraint). If the actual cycle is longer, the Series A narrative must acknowledge it and explain the pipeline stage that is in progress.

_Leading indicator:_ Track days from discovery call to joint champion-finance meeting. If this step takes more than 30 days, the deal cycle will exceed 120 days. This is the earliest actionable warning signal.

---

**Metric 5: IT Security Gate Frequency and Resolution Rate**

_Why it matters:_ Phil Combs documented the IT gate as a deal killer. If it is appearing in 50% of prospects but only being resolved in 20% of cases, SOC 2 preparation becomes the most urgent product-adjacent investment.

_Target:_ Track every deal where IT security was raised as an evaluation criterion. Track how it was resolved (documentation sufficient, additional review required, rejected). Use this data to build the security FAQ that prevents the gate from slowing future deals.

_Decision trigger:_ If IT gate kills more than one deal before May, accelerate SOC 2 Type II preparation to a 90-day priority regardless of other roadmap constraints.

---

**Metric 6: Regulatory Intelligence Engagement Rate**

_Why it matters:_ Regulatory intelligence is the confirmed white space and the documented retention mechanism. But it only creates retention value if customers actually engage with it. Track whether customers are reading the regulatory briefs, querying the system for rate case status, and connecting the output to their budget process.

_Target:_ ≥60% of active customers engage with at least one regulatory intelligence output per quarter. Define "engage" as: opening the dashboard alert, submitting a chat query about a utility's rate case, or referencing the output in a renewal conversation.

_Leading indicator:_ Track the chat query types from active customers. Are they asking regulatory questions ("What is Austin Energy's current rate case status?")? If yes, the retention mechanism is activating. If all queries are billing-focused, the regulatory intelligence value is not yet being communicated.

---

**Metric 7: Finance Gatekeeper Engagement Rate per Active Pilot**

_Why it matters:_ The dual-audience problem is structural and confirmed by two independent primary sources. If the finance gatekeeper is not engaging with the platform within 60 days of pilot start, the subscription conversion requires one person to carry the budget decision alone — and that person rarely has budget authority without finance alignment.

_Target:_ Finance gatekeeper identified and engaged (email, meeting, or platform access) in ≥75% of active pilots within 45 days of pilot kickoff.

_Leading indicator:_ Ask the energy manager champion at the 30-day check-in: "Has your finance or budget team seen the billing audit output?" If the answer is no, do not wait — offer to join a 20-minute meeting where you present the finance-formatted summary directly.
