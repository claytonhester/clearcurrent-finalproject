## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY — THREE DECISIONS THIS DOCUMENT SHOULD FORCE

**What is happening:** Clear Current has completed 12 pilots and identified $10M in billing errors. The research corpus — nine primary interviews across higher education, healthcare, energy services, restaurants, and CRE — reveals a clear hierarchy of opportunity that is not evenly distributed across verticals. The evidence points to three strategic choices that should be made before the May fundraise.

**Why it matters for Clear Current:**

1. **Higher education is the beachhead.** Not because it is the largest market, but because it has the highest combination of buyer readiness, named incumbent frustration, documented deal size, and product-market fit with what Clear Current ships today. Two Texas universities are already in conversation. The buying cycle is calendar-predictable. The competitive gap against EnergyCAP is specific and evidenced.

2. **Healthcare is the long-term prize, not the first win.** The financial scale is larger ($265M at a single system per Scott Czubkowski at Medxcel/Ascension), but the sales cycle is longer, IT security gates are more severe, and proof-of-recovery documentation — which Clear Current does not yet have — is a hard prerequisite. Healthcare is an 18-month story, not a 90-day story.

3. **The identified-vs.-realized gap is the single most important thing to close before the fundraise.** Marc Spieler (NVIDIA) asked directly: "Has the utility recognized the mistake and actually given the credit back?" That question will be asked by every investor who reviews the $10M figure. The answer needs to be yes, with documentation, before May.

## SECTION 2: BUYER PERSONA MATRIX

### What is happening and why it matters

The energy management buying decision is never made by one person. Every interview in this corpus confirmed some version of a multi-stakeholder decision process — and Phil Combs (Trane) gave the most precise description of the hierarchy: "If those three entities are in the CEO's and the board's ear — energy/sustainability managers, facilities directors, finance — then the CEOs and the board will do it. But if you and I go and talk to them, maybe, but probably not." Understanding **who initiates, who blocks, and who approves** determines Clear Current's sales motion in every vertical.

A second structural insight from the corpus: the "Fake Energy Manager" persona — described by Don Johnson as someone "with the energy manager title who is really just operating the building control system" — changes the access strategy. You cannot sell to a person who doesn't know what they need. You have to surface the problem before the conversation.

---

### Persona 1: Energy Operations Manager

**Who they are in the corpus:** Andee Chamberlain (Texas State, Energy & Water Conservation Manager), James Norton (Texas State, systems monitoring), Phil Combs (Trane, CMVP/CEM — a sophisticated variant of this persona).

**Buying behavior:**

- Rarely initiates formal software procurement independently; typically influences through a facilities director or sustainability lead
- Budget authority is typically limited ($10K–$50K discretionary); larger purchases require facilities director approval
- Actively uses and evaluates tools — the fastest path to a champion

**Evidence-based buying triggers:**

- A billing error they can't explain and can't track: _"We had a weird multiplier happen in one of our bills"_ — Andee Chamberlain, Texas State
- Staff departure creating an operational gap: Texas State's 10–15 year bill processing employee just left — confirmed in the summary as an acute buying trigger
- A specific question they cannot answer: _"If somebody had an event at our university event center on a Saturday, how did that impact our consumption?"_ — Andee Chamberlain

**Access strategy:**

- Phil Combs named TEMA (Texas Energy Managers Association) and the Association of Energy Engineers as the right networking channels — peer-to-peer trust, not cold outreach
- Event-based demos at TEMA meetings, AEE conferences
- Suggested prompts in the **Chat** tier (CC Engine) that surface the exact questions this persona cares about: anomaly root cause, event-correlated consumption, meter-level variance
- **Rate Analyst** and **Energy HQ** are the primary product surfaces for this persona

**Budget authority:** Influencer, not final approver. Gets the champion; needs executive sponsor for the deal.

---

### Persona 2: Finance / Budget Owner

**Who they are in the corpus:** Andi Ault (UT Austin, Sr. Associate Director, Finance), with secondary confirmation from Ann Walston (Bon Secours Mercy Health) who described finance as reactive and hedging as the one proactive lever.

**Buying behavior:**

- Does not buy software proactively; has budget assigned to them and needs a problem surfaced before they act
- Andi Ault's framing is the clearest evidence in the corpus: _"Flagging anomalies would be the first process so that that is streamlined and featured and flagged rather than looking for it"_ — she wants the **Dashboard (push to user)** tier, not the chat interface
- Budget authority is high for operational line items once a business case is established; procurement still routes through a separate process

**Evidence-based buying triggers:**

- Rate changes that land after budget submission: _"The City of Austin finalizes rates after budget submission"_ — Andi Ault — this is the exact regulatory intelligence use case
- An anomaly large enough to attract audit scrutiny: the $5–6M Bon Secours billing error required board-level intervention
- M&A or portfolio change management where the utility bill picture becomes too complex to manage manually: confirmed at AdventHealth (John Culver, Scott Sukits)

**Access strategy:**

- The finance persona responds to outputs, not interfaces. The selling message is: "You don't have to learn a new tool. Your anomalies are pushed to you. Your budget variance is explained before you ask for it."
- **Dashboard tier** (CC Engine push layer) is the primary product surface for this persona
- **Regulatory intelligence** framing — the specific gap between 5.9% actual CAGR and 3% budgeted escalator — lands directly on this persona's pain. At UT Austin's $20M annual gas spend, that is approximately $580K/year in potential budget underestimation from the assumption error alone
- Financial system integration (Andi Ault's #1 automation wish) is a roadmap item that unlocks this persona's full adoption

> **Product tension to flag:** Andi Ault explicitly said she wants outputs integrated with her financial system, not a standalone dashboard. The current product is a standalone platform. This does not prevent a sale, but it is a gap that lengthens the adoption arc. [EXTENDS ROADMAP] — financial system API integration.

### Persona 4: Procurement / IT

**Who they are in the corpus:** Phil Combs described IT as a blocking force ("You've got guys like me that would love to use this, and then IT says no, no, no"). AdventHealth confirmed: 2.5 years to get BrainBox AI through IT approval. Marc Spieler flagged data security as a top-of-mind concern.

**Buying behavior:**

- Procurement and IT do not buy software — they approve or block it
- SOC 2 Type II is the named certification that satisfies IT gatekeepers (secondary research)
- Healthcare IT security posture is the most severe, particularly post-Johnson Controls hack

**Evidence-based buying triggers:** N/A — this persona does not initiate. They respond to a champion who has already built the business case.

**Access strategy:**

- Pre-empt the IT security gate: have SOC 2 documentation, data handling policies, and a security overview ready before the first IT meeting
- Build a "Security FAQ" for the sales process — specifically addressing utility data classification as competitively sensitive
- Marc Spieler's framing: the energy bills themselves contain sensitive business intelligence. Clear Current's data handling posture needs to be enterprise-credible before healthcare or large-enterprise conversations proceed

### Persona 5: C-Suite / Board

**Who they are in the corpus:** Marc Spieler (NVIDIA) described the C-suite dynamic most precisely. Scott Czubkowski (Ascension/Medxcel) described his organization's executive query use case.

**Buying behavior:**

- Does not initiate software evaluation; funds it when three entities (energy managers, facilities directors, finance) align and bring a compelling story
- C-suite framing: "Is my building using more energy than a sister building? What would be my projected utility bill for next month?" — _Scott Czubkowski_ — the executive query use case is real but requires the underlying data infrastructure to already be running

**Evidence-based buying triggers:**

- A documented, board-visible billing error (Bon Secours: $5–6M error required board-level intervention)
- A board-level ESG/carbon commitment requiring operational data to fulfill
- A CFO-level budget variance that cannot be explained

**Access strategy:**

- The C-suite is the ultimate sponsor, not the entry point
- Clear Current's **Energy HQ** surface (portfolio visibility) is the C-suite-appropriate view — not a chat interface
- Prepare an executive brief (one page: what your portfolio is spending, what it should be spending, what we found, what it cost) as the escalation document when a champion needs to take the deal upstairs

### The Identified-vs.-Realized Problem (Address This First)

Before sequencing deals, one critical prerequisite must be acknowledged. Marc Spieler asked the most important question in the corpus: _"Has the utility recognized that they made a mistake and actually given the credit back to the company, or they still — they've identified the issue, they can justify the issue, but nobody's gotten a refund on their payment yet?"_

The $10M in identified billing errors across Clear Current's 12 pilots is a strong proof point — but only if it is labeled correctly. If even one or two of those errors has resulted in a utility-confirmed credit or refund, document it: error identified → utility contacted → credit issued → dollars returned. That is a complete case study. Without it, the $10M figure is a lead, not a close.

> **This does not mean the product is broken.** It means the narrative is incomplete. The CC Engine's honest maturity description is "assist manual completion" for error resolution — that is the right characterization.
