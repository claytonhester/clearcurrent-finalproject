## WHAT IS HAPPENING ACROSS THE YEAR — EXECUTIVE ORIENTATION

Before the month-by-month detail, here is what the evidence says about the structural rhythm of commercial energy management:

**Four hard seasonal beats drive almost all decision-making:**

1. **Budget season (Oct–Mar, varying by vertical)** — Finance personnel are dominant; output is a dollar forecast with rate assumptions baked in; the structural vulnerability is that utility rates are still in flux when budgets must be submitted.

2. **Procurement season (Mar–Jun)** — Commodity contracts for natural gas and electricity are executed; hedging tranches are layered; broker relationships are most active. Ann Walston (Bon Secours Mercy Health) was executing natural gas supplier contracts the day of her interview.

3. **Demand season (Jun–Sep)** — Peak summer demand sets demand charges that persist for 12 months in many rate structures; billing error exposure is highest; operational staff are in firefighting mode.

4. **Compliance and reporting season (Jan–Apr, Sep–Oct)** — Carbon disclosures, Energy Star submissions, GRESB assessments, and building performance standard compliance deadlines concentrate in Q1 and fall; they trigger data collection and audit workflows that surface Clear Current's invoice-level evidence base.

**The buyer is reactive throughout all four beats.** Don Johnson (Trane Technologies) stated this directly: _"Most customers are still reactive... even hospitals."_ Phil Combs added the mechanism: _"tyranny of the urgent."_ EnergyCAP's own 2026 State of the Industry survey found 76% of commercial energy managers expect utility costs to rise yet remain reactive on cost reduction. The calendar below maps where proactive positioning by Clear Current interrupts that reactive cycle most effectively.

### JANUARY

**What is happening in the market**
January is the operational hangover from December's fiscal close and the opening gun of Q1 carbon reporting season. Energy managers in every vertical are simultaneously closing year-end accounts, beginning or deepening budget-build processes, and managing peak heating loads in cold climates. For higher education on a September 1 fiscal year (Texas State, UT Austin), January is midway through the fiscal year — the team is pulling historical consumption data to inform mid-year adjustments and to begin building next year's budget baseline.

**By vertical:**

- **Higher Education (Sept 1 FY):** Budget planning season begins. Andee Chamberlain (Texas State) described January–February as the period when her team pulls historical energy data to build budget narratives. Meter-level data reconciliation is active. EnergyCAP reporting cycles begin.
- **Higher Education (July 1 FY):** Mid-year budget review. Rate variance from fall utility adjustments becomes visible in the December/January bills. Finance flagging unexpected cost variances.
- **Healthcare (Oct 1 FY):** Q1 operational execution. Winter heating loads drive natural gas spend; hospitals in cold climates (PJM region, Midwest) are managing heating-season peak demand. BSMH's capacity charges — now at historically elevated PJM levels following the data center-driven auction spike — begin showing up at full annual rate.
- **Hospitality:** Calendar year begins fresh. Q4 close reconciliation happening. January occupancy is typically low (leisure travel trough), which depresses electricity demand but makes heating costs the dominant energy line item.
- **Restaurant/Retail:** Post-holiday operational reset. Q4 close; January electricity usage low but heating bills elevated in northern markets.
- **Commercial Real Estate:** CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) submission preparation begins. 2025 data must be collected, audited, and submitted by the CDP deadline (typically April). This triggers invoice-level data pulls across the entire portfolio.

**Budget/procurement activity:**
Higher education budget build begins (Sept 1 FY vertical). Healthcare Q1 budget vs. actual review. Corporate and CRE: Q4 actuals triggering variance investigations that surface billing error opportunities.

**Regulatory milestones:**
Active utility rate cases in many states are in the 275-day review window. Ohio PUC's mandatory timeline means cases filed in prior spring are reaching final decisions in January–February. Any case with a July 1 or January 1 effective date is concluding now. PJM capacity auction results from the prior year's auction (run in December/January) are being incorporated into utility tariffs.

**Billing error exposure:**
High. December and January bills combine peak heating loads with year-end meter reads — a historically high-error period. Texas State's "weird multiplier" billing error (referenced by Andee Chamberlain in the Texas State summary) is the type of anomaly that surfaces in high-load months.

**Clear Current recommended positioning:**

- **Rate Analyst:** Lead with anomaly detection on December/January bills — highest error exposure window. Frame as "close the year right."
- **Energy HQ:** Portfolio variance dashboard for healthcare and CRE clients — surface which sites had the largest YoY cost variance and why.
- **Outreach target:** Higher education energy managers (Sept 1 FY) beginning budget season; CRE sustainability managers beginning CDP data collection.

### JUNE

**What is happening in the market**

A single hot day with a poorly managed peak can cost a commercial facility tens of thousands of dollars in locked demand charges for the following year. The energy manager's job in June is not administration — it is active demand response, load curtailment, and peak management. This is the highest financial exposure window of the year.

**By vertical:**

- **Higher Education:** Summer operations begin. Most campuses are running at reduced academic load, but HVAC is fighting summer heat. Research buildings remain fully operational. UT Austin's 60 MW CHP system is at its most valuable in summer — self-generation reduces grid demand exposure.

- **Hospitality:** Peak summer occupancy begins. Hotels in warm climates run near-full HVAC continuously. Energy costs are at annual maximum; June bills often surface the first demand charge exceedances.
- **Restaurant/Retail:** June is the opening of the QSR electricity peak season. Walt Taylor (Panda Restaurant Group) described this explicitly: "electricity peaks from June through September, driven almost entirely by air conditioning load." _(Walt Taylor summary, summary-sourced)_

**Regulatory milestones:**

### DECEMBER

**What is happening in the market**
December is operationally complex and strategically quiet. Year-end closings, holiday operations, and the polar vortex risk window dominate. For energy software vendors, December is a low-outreach month — buyers are closing books, not evaluating new tools. But December is when the data that will drive Q1 audit work is being generated, and polar vortex events create the kind of bill shock that triggers reactive buying behavior in January–February.

**By vertical:**

- **Higher Education:** End-of-semester. Building loads drop with student departures. End-of-FY (Dec 31) for calendar-year budget supplementary funds. Winter recess = anomalous low consumption that can mask or reveal metering errors.
- **Healthcare:** Q1 of FY (Oct 1 FY). Winter operations full load. Polar vortex risk highest — Scott Czubkowski's $7.2M three-day gas cost event was a December-type scenario. Hospitals cannot curtail; they pay whatever the market clears.
- **Hospitality:** Holiday travel peak. Hotels at high occupancy; heating costs elevated; energy management secondary to operations.
- **Restaurant/Retail:** Holiday peak operations. December is the highest-revenue month for most QSR chains — energy management is fully deprioritized.
- **CRE:** Year-end close. Final ENERGY STAR data submissions. Property managers reconciling year-end utility costs vs. tenant charge-backs.

**Budget/procurement activity:**
All verticals: year-end close. Corporate budget for next year is typically approved. Energy commodity markets most volatile. PJM capacity auction results announced (December/January) — setting the capacity charge inputs for the coming delivery year.

**Regulatory milestones:**
PJM capacity auction (December/January). Year-end regulatory decisions in several states. California CPUC often releases major decisions in December.

**Clear Current recommended positioning:**

- **Rate Analyst:** December is a data-generation month, not a sales month. Focus on customer success — ensure existing customers have December bills ingested and anomalies flagged before the January billing review.

- **Outreach strategy:** Minimal new outreach. Prioritize customer health and retention. January re-engagement campaigns should be prepped in December for launch the first week of January.

### The Identified-vs-Realized Risk — A Calendar Caveat

Marc Spieler's question — _"Have utilities actually issued refunds based on errors Clear Current found, or is the $10M still theoretical?"_ — applies to the calendar as much as it applies to the product. Every "billing error detection" touchpoint recommended above is based on Clear Current's current capability to **spot** errors, not to **resolve** them. The calendar above correctly positions Rate Analyst as the detection and intelligence tool. It does not promise that Clear Current autonomously disputes the error and recovers the credit — because the product context is explicit: "Resolve errors: assists manual completion — not hands-off resolution by default."

The sales calendar is honest about this. The pitch is not "we recover your money." The pitch is "we find what you're missing, and we tell you why it matters before your next bill arrives." The proof that demand converts to close — that a detected error leads to a recovered credit — is the case study John and Dan need to produce before the May fundraise. The calendar creates the outreach moments; the case study creates the credibility that converts those moments into signed contracts.
