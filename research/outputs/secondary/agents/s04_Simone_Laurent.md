## 2. KEY FINDINGS

**FINDING 1 — Rate case filings are 100% public from Day 1, but zero commercial energy software systematically monitors them**
The DSR states explicitly: "The entire process is publicly accessible. Pennsylvania requires utilities to notify customers in billing notices at least 60 days before filing; Ohio makes the docket accessible from filing day and the 275-day clock is public; the PUCT publishes 'Notice of Proposed Rate Change' documents that any company can subscribe to."
The actionable gap is not regulatory — it is that no commercial energy software translates these filings into facility-level financial impact models. Energy Toolbase, EnergyCAP, WatchWire/Tango, and Schneider Resource Advisor are all confirmed not to offer this capability.
_Source: DSR Section 1, "What Competitors Currently Offer (and Don't)"_

**FINDING 2 — Commercial electricity rates are rising at 5.9% CAGR, nearly double the 3% escalator in most corporate budgets — a structural, systematic budget error**
Arcadia's 2026 Commercial Electricity Rate Report (analyzed 81 utilities, 5 building types, 30,000+ tariff combinations across 2020–2025) found 97.5% of commercial facilities experienced electricity rate increases, 71% outpaced inflation, and the median CAGR was 5.9%.
The EIA confirms: commercial sector average retail price rose from 12.75 cents/kWh in 2024 to 13.63 cents/kWh in December 2025 — a 7.83% year-over-year increase.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnotes 17–19 (Arcadia report) and footnotes 20–23 (EIA)_

**FINDING 3 — The PJM capacity market surge creates an unbudgeted $110,000 annual liability per MW of commercial load — invisible to organizations using flat escalator budgeting**
The 2026–2027 PJM Base Residual Auction cleared at $329.17/MW-day, a 22% increase from the prior year. Arcadia estimates annual capacity costs per MW will rise from approximately $10,000 in 2024 to more than $120,000 by 2027 — a 12x increase in a single cost component. This affects all commercial customers in PJM territory (13 eastern states from Illinois to New Jersey). No flat-rate budget escalator captures this exposure.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnotes 24–26 (PJM auction data) and footnote 17 (Arcadia report)_

**FINDING 4 — The budget timing gap is structurally documented: the PPL Pennsylvania rate case creates a precise collision between university budget deadlines and rate case timelines**
PPL Electric filed its rate case October 2025. Pennsylvania's 9-month regulatory process puts the final PUC decision at July 1, 2026 — the exact date a university's new fiscal year begins. Universities (documented with Boston University) lock their FY budget in March — four months before the final rate decision. The university is legally obligated to operate on a budget set without knowledge of the rate that will govern 12 months of energy spend.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnote 9 (PPL PA PUC press release) and footnote 32 (Boston University budget process)_

**FINDING 5 — Ohio's regulatory structure generates near-continuous rate case activity — not a 3-5 year cycle — creating persistent monitoring demand**
Major Ohio utilities file base rate cases every 3–5 years, but multiple overlapping proceedings (ESP reviews, reconciliation cases, special riders) create near-continuous regulatory activity. AES Ohio filed November 2020, approved December 2022 (25 months including settlement), then filed again November 2024, settled August 2025, final order November 2025 (~9% increase). AEP Ohio filed a 2025 base case settled January 2026. FirstEnergy had two simultaneous rate cases decided December 2024. This is not a periodic event — it is a continuous stream.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnotes 1–5 (PUCO, AES Ohio, AEP Ohio documentation)_

**FINDING 6 — Illinois moved to annual rate adjustment proceedings under CEJA 2021 — making regulatory monitoring a year-round operational requirement, not an episodic one**
Illinois' 2021 Clean Energy Jobs Act eliminated the traditional multi-year rate case cycle for ComEd and Ameren. These utilities now submit annual reconciliation petitions. The ICC approved $606M for ComEd and $308.6M for Ameren in December 2025, both spread through 2027. Illinois commercial customers now face annual rate adjustment proceedings — generating continuous monitoring value in one of the country's largest commercial energy markets.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnote 6 (NPR Illinois / ICC)_

**FINDING 7 — NYC Local Law 97 creates hard compliance deadlines with specific, calculable financial penalties — the strongest buying urgency in the CRE vertical**
LL97 applies to buildings over 25,000 gross square feet. Penalty: $268 per ton of excess carbon emissions. First annual report due May 1, 2025 (extended to December 31, 2025). Current compliance trajectory: 88% of covered buildings on track for 2024 limits, only 35% aligned with 2030 limits. Projected consequence: approximately 13,500 of 50,000 covered properties out of compliance by 2030, generating $900 million in annual fines. This is a hard legal mandate with calculable per-building penalty exposure, not soft ESG pressure.
_Source: DSR Section 4, footnotes 76–79 (LL97 mandate documentation)_

**FINDING 8 — NYSEG's proposed 39.4% delivery revenue increase would translate to a 33.5% increase in a typical total bill — the largest documented rate increase in the research corpus**
New York rate cases are among the most complex in the country, with 1,600+ information requests and 12–18 month timelines. NYSEG has requested a 39.4% increase in delivery revenues. At 12–18 months of advance public docket access, a commercial customer in NYSEG territory monitoring the PUC docket would have over a year of lead time to model this exposure. Without monitoring, they will receive it in their bill.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnotes 10–12 (NYPSC documentation)_

**FINDING 9 — The Texas regulatory intelligence opportunity is structurally different and potentially more valuable: ERCOT market complexity creates continuous supplier contract and TDU tariff monitoring needs**
Texas (ERCOT) operates under deregulation — no traditional rate cases. The monitoring opportunity is ERCOT capacity auction results, TDU delivery charge updates (CenterPoint updates March and September), and supplier contract expiration intelligence. CenterPoint received PUCT approval in early 2026 to recover Hurricane Beryl and Winter Storm Enzo costs over 14 years — a multi-year tariff rider affecting all commercial customers. ERCOT wholesale prices are forecast up approximately 45% in 2026 versus 2025 at summer peak hours.
_Source: DSR Section 1, footnotes 13–16 (PUCT, ERCOT, CenterPoint documentation)_

**FINDING 10 — EnergyCAP, WatchWire/Tango, Energy Toolbase, and Schneider Resource Advisor all confirmed as not offering forward-looking regulatory intelligence — the white space is validated across all four named competitors**
The DSR explicitly documents: "EnergyCAP, WatchWire/Tango, and Schneider Resource Advisor offer utility bill management, benchmarking, and historical analytics, but none provide forward-looking PUC docket monitoring or rate change impact modeling as a product feature." Energy Toolbase covers 5,500+ rate records updated within 1–2 business days of changes, but does not monitor PUC dockets for _proposed_ changes and is positioned for solar/storage developers, not commercial energy managers.
_Source: DSR Section 1, "What Competitors Currently Offer (and Don't)," footnotes 29–31 (Energy Toolbase)_

**FINDING 11 — GRESB's October reporting cycle creates a predictable, annual buying season for CRE energy management software — with 1,002 fund managers participating in 2025 at an all-time high**
GRESB 2025 Real Estate Assessment attracted 1,002 fund managers submitting 2,382 assessments. Data submission closes September, results released October — making January–June the highest-intent period for CRE energy management software purchasing (assessment of prior year gaps, preparation for next cycle). This buying cycle is predictable and recurring, not event-driven.
_Source: DSR Section 4, footnotes 65–66, 71–74 (GRESB 2025 data)_

**FINDING 12 — The regulatory intelligence module addresses the identified vs. realized gap directly: rate case intelligence is binary and verifiable — a rate change either did or did not occur, and the financial impact can be calculated exactly**
Unlike billing error recovery (which requires a utility to issue a credit — Marc Spieler's open question), regulatory intelligence delivers value in the prediction stage: "Your Q3 energy budget will be insufficient because PPL's rate case decision lands July 1 and you locked your budget in March." This value is realized at budget-setting time, not after a utility dispute is resolved. It closes the identified vs. realized gap by shifting the value delivery to a point before any disputed transaction occurs.
_Source: RSB Finding 7 (Marc Spieler investor question) + DSR Section 1 (rate case timing mechanics)_

**What it does NOT do:** Does not monitor PUC dockets for _proposed_ changes; does not alert commercial buyers to rate cases in progress; is positioned entirely for solar/storage developers building project proposals — not for commercial energy managers tracking budget exposure.
**Why this matters for Clear Current:** Energy Toolbase is the closest analog to a rate intelligence function in the market, and it specifically does not do what Clear Current's regulatory intelligence module would do. The gap is not just absence — it's a deliberately different product design for a different customer. Phil Combs (Trane) named Energy Toolbase as the competitive reference for tariff analysis, which means the comparison customers will make is "Clear Current vs. Energy Toolbase" — and Clear Current's forward-looking PUC monitoring is differentiated from Energy Toolbase's backward-looking rate database.

## 5. DELIVERABLE BUILD GUIDANCE

### Regulatory Intelligence Product Module Specification

**Module Name:** Regulatory Intelligence Monitor (or "Rate Case Intelligence" — test both in customer conversations)

**Primary Value Proposition (investor-grade language):**
_"Commercial energy buyers face a structural information asymmetry: rate case filings are public from Day 1, but no existing software translates them into facility-level financial impact models. The result is a systematic budget error — organizations using a 3% annual escalator face actual rate increases of 5.9% CAGR, with PJM capacity surcharges adding unbudgeted six-figure liabilities for mid-market commercial accounts. Clear Current's Regulatory Intelligence Monitor closes this gap by monitoring PUC dockets across five states, modeling rate change impacts at the meter level, and surfacing findings to finance and operations teams months before changes take effect."_

**Module Construction Specifications (enriched from DSR data):**

_Tier 1 — PUC Docket Monitoring Engine_

- Monitor filings in Ohio (PUCO), Illinois (ICC), Pennsylvania (PUC), New York (NYPSC), and Texas (PUCT) as initial five-state coverage
- Ohio: 275-day statutory timeline creates predictable intelligence windows; monitor ESP reviews and reconciliation cases, not just base rate cases
- Illinois: Annual reconciliation petition monitoring (CEJA structure) — monitoring is year-round, not episodic
- Pennsylvania: Flag cases at Day 0 of filing; 9-month timeline means 9 months of advance intelligence
- New York: 12–18 month process; 1,600+ information requests generate multiple monitoring events
- Texas: Different architecture — monitor TDU tariff filings (March/September CenterPoint updates), ERCOT capacity auction results, special tariff riders (e.g., Beryl/Winter Storm Enzo recovery riders)
- Data sources: All public. PUC dockets, utility press releases, PUCT Notice of Proposed Rate Change documents.

_Tier 2 — Rate Change Impact Modeling_

- Link docket filings to account-level tariff data (what rate schedule is this customer currently on?)
- Model the proposed rate change against 24 months of historical usage and demand data
- Generate a dollar-denominated impact forecast: "If this rate case is approved as filed, your annual energy cost increases by $X"
- Flag cases where capacity charge surcharges would create step-change cost increases (PJM model: $329.17/MW-day × annual load = total capacity exposure)

_Tier 3 — Budget Alignment Alerts_

- Map customer fiscal year budget deadlines against rate case decision timelines
- Higher education: Flag cases with final decision dates after March budget lock date
- Corporate: Flag cases with final decision dates after Q4 budget approval cycle
- Example: PPL PA case (filed October 2025, decision July 1, 2026) would generate an alert to any Pennsylvania university in November 2025: "Your FY2027 energy budget will be locked before the final rate decision. Here is your exposure range based on the utility's filed request and historical settlement patterns."

_Tier 4 — Regulatory History and Pattern Database_

- Document historical settlement patterns by utility (do AES Ohio cases settle above or below filed request? By how much?)
- Document timeline variances (Ohio statutory 275 days but AES Ohio took 25 months including settlement)
- Create confidence intervals for rate change forecasts, not just point estimates

**Required Data Inputs:**

- PUC docket monitoring (public)
- Customer account tariff data (from billing audit engine — existing Clear Current capability)
- Customer historical usage and demand data (from billing audit engine)
- Customer fiscal year calendar (onboarding input — one question)

**Required Software Capabilities:**

- Natural language processing sufficient to parse utility rate case filings (lengthy regulatory documents)
- Tariff database with current rate structures by state/utility/rate class
- Financial impact modeling at account level
- Push notification to finance persona (budget timing alert format)
- Dashboard view for energy operations persona (regulatory activity map)

**Competitive Differentiation Statement:**
The distinction between Energy Toolbase's backward-looking rate database (what rates _are_) and Clear Current's regulatory intelligence module (what rates _will become_ and when) is the core product differentiation. Energy Toolbase updates its database within 1–2 business days of a rate change becoming _effective_. Clear Current's module provides intelligence 9 months before a rate change becomes effective. The timing gap is the product.

### The Budget Timing Gap — Quantified for Investor Narrative

**The Core Calculation:**

A university with $20M in annual energy spend (Andi Ault's UT Austin context) using a 3% budget escalator is setting aside $600,000 for year-over-year energy cost increases. Actual commercial electricity CAGR is 5.9% (Arcadia, confirmed directionally by EIA 7.83% YoY December 2025). The budget gap on the escalator assumption alone is:

- Budgeted increase: $600,000 (3% × $20M)
- Actual increase at 5.9% CAGR: $1,180,000
- **Unbudgeted exposure from escalator gap alone: $580,000 per year**

Add a rate case scenario (Pennsylvania PPL, ~9% increase as filed):

- Budgeted increase: $600,000
- Actual increase at 9% rate case approval: $1,800,000
- **Unbudgeted exposure from rate case: $1,200,000 per year**

The UT Austin scenario is real: $20M annual gas spend alone (per Andi Ault primary interview, RSB Section 02). The electricity budget would be separate and larger. A 5.9% vs. 3% escalator mismatch on a $30M total energy budget creates approximately **$870,000 in annual budget gap**.

**The Timing Mechanics (PPL PA case as the cleanest example):**

- University submits FY2027 energy budget: March 2026
- PPL rate case final decision: July 1, 2026
- Rate effective: July 1, 2026 (same day as new FY)
- **Consequence:** The university operates a 12-month budget set using rates that did not exist when the budget was approved, and which were knowable (within a range) from the October 2025 filing date. The information was public for 5 months before budget lock. The tool did not exist to surface it.

**The PJM Capacity Shock Case (applicable to 13-state territory):**
For a large hospital or university with 1 MW average demand in PJM territory:

- 2024 capacity cost: ~$10,000/year (per Arcadia/DSR)
- 2027 projected capacity cost: $120,000+/year (per PJM auction clearing price)
- **One-line-item budget exposure: $110,000 per year, invisible to flat-escalator budgeting**
  A 500,000-square-foot university building might carry 1–2 MW of peak demand. A hospital system might carry 5–10 MW. The aggregate unbudgeted exposure at the institution level is in the millions.

Every PUC docket is accessible from Day 1. But no commercial energy management platform today aggregates these filings, translates them into facility-level financial impact models, and surfaces them to the finance team before the budget is locked. The result: median commercial electricity rates are rising at 5.9% CAGR while corporate budgets use 3% escalators. PJM capacity charges are rising 12x in three years while facility managers assume steady-state. A single utility's rate case can add $1.2 million in unbudgeted energy expense at a mid-sized university — and the filing is public nine months before the rate takes effect. ## 7. GAPS AND OPEN QUESTIONS

**GAP 1 — The 3% Budget Escalator Claim Needs Independent Corroboration**
_Why it matters:_ The most powerful investor narrative claim — "budgets use 3% while rates rise at 5.9%" — traces exclusively to Arcadia's 2026 report. Arcadia has a commercial interest in emphasizing rate volatility. Before this becomes a centerpiece investor narrative claim, we need either: (a) a CFO survey or corporate budgeting industry data confirming 3% as a common practice, (b) corroboration from Phil Combs or Andi Ault in a follow-up question ("what escalator does your institution use in budget planning?"), or (c) reframing to compare EIA's documented 7.83% YoY increase against whatever escalator customers report using.
_Recommended action:_ Add one question to the next primary interview with any finance-side contact: "What percentage escalator does your organization use when projecting energy costs in the annual budget?"

**GAP 2 — The LL97 Non-Compliance Projection Source Is Unidentified**
_Why it matters:_ "65% of covered buildings not aligned with 2030 limits" and "$900M in annual fines projected" are powerful statistics that will be used in investor materials. They need to be traceable to a named primary source (NYC DOB, NYC DEP, Urban Green Council, or a peer-reviewed study).
_Recommended action:_ Search Urban Green Council publications and NYC DOB LL97 compliance reports to identify the primary source for these figures. If they trace to a credible government or independent research source, they become HIGH confidence.

**GAP 3 — No Primary Interview from a University with Active Rate Case Exposure**
_Why it matters:_ The PPL PA / university budget timing collision is documented with secondary evidence (Boston University budget process + PPL PA rate case timeline). But we have no primary interview from a Pennsylvania or New York university finance director who has actually experienced the pain of a rate case decision landing after budget lock.
_Recommended action:_ If there is an opportunity for one additional interview before the April 13 deadline, a Pennsylvania or upstate New York university energy manager would provide the primary voice evidence to complement the secondary documentation.

**GAP 4 — The PJM Capacity Cost Baseline ($10,000 in 2024) Is Sourced Exclusively to Arcadia**
_Why it matters:_ The $10,000 to $120,000 per MW calculation is the most dramatic financial impact figure in the corpus. The $120,000 figure is independently verifiable (PJM auction clearing price × 365 days). The $10,000 baseline requires verification against the PJM auction clearing price for 2023–2024. If the $10,000 baseline is accurate, the 12x calculation stands. If it is significantly different, the narrative changes.
_Recommended action:_ Verify the 2023–2024 PJM Base Residual Auction clearing price against PJM's own published auction results, which are primary public records.

**GAP 5 — CSRD Financial Penalties Are Not Quantified in the Corpus**
_Why it matters:_ CSRD is mentioned in the RSB as a buying trigger but no penalty figures are cited. Unlike LL97 ($268/ton, calculable) or SB 253/261, CSRD enforcement is member-state-dependent and varies significantly across the EU. For U.S. companies with EU operations or listings, CSRD compliance is relevant but the penalty exposure is less calculable.
_Recommended action:_ For the investor narrative, position CSRD as a buying trigger for U.S. multinationals with EU operations but do not attempt to quantify penalty exposure without access to specific member state enforcement rules. Do not overstate CSRD urgency for Clear Current's current target customers (higher education, commercial real estate, U.S. corporates) — most are not CSRD-subject in the near term.

**GAP 6 — SB 253/261 Penalty Enforcement Mechanism Is Unclear**
_Why it matters:_ The RSB mentions SB 253 and SB 261 as buying triggers but does not document the penalty enforcement structure. SB 253 penalties are described as "not yet finalized" in the DSR Section context — the regulatory enforcement rules were still being developed as of research completion.
_Recommended action:_ For investor materials, present SB 253/261 as creating compliance reporting obligations (clear) with penalties "to be determined by enforcement rules" (accurate) rather than citing specific penalty figures that may be contested.

**GAP 7 — No Documentation of Whether Any Clear Current Pilot Customer Has Received Rate Case Intelligence as a Value Delivery**
_Why it matters:_ The entire regulatory intelligence module is positioned as "nobody has built this yet." But Clear Current itself has not built it yet either (it's a proposed module). The investor narrative needs to distinguish between: (a) Clear Current's existing billing error audit capability (which has $10M in identified errors across pilots), and (b) the regulatory intelligence module, which is a proposed product feature.
_Recommended action:_ Be precise in investor materials about which capabilities are live (billing error audit) versus roadmap (regulatory intelligence monitor). The regulatory intelligence module's business case is extremely strong on the secondary evidence alone — it does not need to be presented as already built.

**GAP 8 — ERCOT Wholesale Price Forecast (+45% in 2026) Sources to a Commercially Motivated Firm**
_Why it matters:_ The 45% ERCOT wholesale price forecast comes from Energy Ogre (DSR footnote 15), a Texas retail electricity advisory firm with commercial interest in emphasizing price volatility (it encourages customers to use their procurement advisory services).
_Recommended action:_ Corroborate ERCOT price forecasts against ERCOT's own published seasonal assessment documents, which are primary public records. If the directional forecast is consistent, use ERCOT's own documentation as the primary source.
