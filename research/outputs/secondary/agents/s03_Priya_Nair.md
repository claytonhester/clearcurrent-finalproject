## 2. KEY FINDINGS

**Finding 1: The 80% overpaying statistic is fatally compromised and must not be used.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Claim 1 analysis. National Utilities Refund (NUR) is the origin — a contingency-fee auditing firm whose statistic describes its own client base (companies already suspicious enough to seek an audit), not a representative sample. No methodology, no sample size, no independent verification. The Claim Verification Report assigns **LOW credibility** and recommends against use in investor or business presentations. This is the correct call. I agree unequivocally.

**Finding 2: The 83% RI PUC finding is the single most defensible billing error statistic in the entire corpus.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, References 5 and 6 (YouTube — RI PUC audit coverage and PUC demands audit video). The Rhode Island Public Utilities Commission ordered an independent audit of Rhode Island Energy's billing systems after discovering that "83% of bills that were flagged for issues within their billing system in December were likely sent out anyway." This is a government regulatory enforcement finding. The Claim Verification Report traces it to PUC documentation. It is not a vendor claim. It is not a survey. It is a regulator finding that the utility's own internal quality control was failing at an 83% rate on known problem bills. **This is the strongest single statistic in the corpus for investor use.**

**Finding 3: The FERC/FirstEnergy $108M finding is government-documented and fully defensible.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Reference 4 (Energy and Policy Institute, citing FERC 2022 audit). A 2022 FERC audit found FirstEnergy's Ohio subsidiaries "may have mischarged customers" by misclassifying $108 million in corporate overhead as recoverable costs. The Public Utilities Commission of Ohio upheld this finding in 2024. This is a federal regulatory enforcement action on a documented, dollar-quantified mischargement. It is not an estimate. It is not a survey. It is an audit finding. **HIGH credibility.**

**Finding 4: The Harvard Law (2025) structural asymmetry paper is real and specifically relevant — but its citation chain requires careful handling.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Claim 1 analysis, Reference 8: "Extracting Profits from the Public: How Utility Ratepayers Are Paying for Excessive Executive Compensation" — Harvard Environmental Law Initiative, March 2025. The paper is cited as documenting "structural information asymmetry" between utilities and customers: utilities generate the information regulators use to set rates, which creates persistent conditions for billing and rate errors to go undetected. The paper notes that "independent audits, not regulatory proceedings, are how most mischarged costs are discovered." The Claim Verification Report provides a direct PDF link (eelp.law.harvard.edu). This is a peer-reviewed legal analysis from Harvard's Environmental Law Initiative — a credible academic source. **This is the structural/theoretical anchor for why billing errors persist systemically.** However: I note the title suggests a focus on executive compensation rather than billing errors per se — the relevant claim (information asymmetry, independent audits as discovery mechanism) is one finding within a broader paper. Users should cite it at the specific finding level, not as a general "Harvard says billing errors are common" claim.

**Finding 5: The 5–15% billing error cost figure is directionally useful but source-compromised.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Claim 4. The 5–15% range comes from 360 Energy, Conservice, and National Utility Refund — all audit firms with direct financial incentive to characterize error rates as high. The Claim Verification Report correctly notes a critical definitional issue: "additional costs annually" from billing errors is not the same as a frequency rate. A 5–15% cost overrun could be produced by one large error on one major account, not widespread small errors. The directional claim is corroborated by regulatory findings but the specific number is not independently verifiable. **MEDIUM as directional; LOW as a precise statistic.**

**Finding 6: Arcadia's 20% meter-tariff manual review rate is a more useful and less conflicted proxy than most error rate statistics — but it is still Arcadia's own claim.**

This suggests systematic tariff complexity and mis-classification at scale. The conflict of interest is present — Arcadia profits from the complexity — but it is softer than an auditor's contingency-fee incentive. More importantly, Arcadia is describing the complexity of its own operations at scale, not making a marketing claim about the broader market. A competitor's operational disclosure is more credible than a vendor's market research. **MEDIUM-HIGH as a proxy statistic.**

**Finding 10: The 360 Energy ~30% client error rate is a more conservative and slightly more defensible alternative to the 80% figure — but it is still conflicted.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Claim 1 analysis, Reference 9 (360energy.net). 360 Energy states that "nearly 30% of our 360 Energy clients face billing errors annually." The Claim Verification Report notes this figure is "notably more conservative than NUR's 80% claim." It remains conflicted — 360 Energy is an audit firm and this figure also describes their client base (already-suspicious accounts). However, 30% is a meaningfully different magnitude from 80%, suggesting at minimum that the true rate is probably somewhere between 30% and the much lower rate that would exist in a truly random sample of all commercial buildings. **LOW-MEDIUM as a market-wide estimate; flaggable as an industry-sourced figure with disclosed conflict.**

**Finding 11: No U.S. government agency publishes a commercial utility billing error rate.**
Source: Claim Verification Report, Claim 4 analysis. The EIA, FERC, NARUC, and state PUCs track rate cases and regulatory complaints but do not publish a systematic error rate for commercial billing. This is stated explicitly in the Claim Verification Report. This is an important finding for investor materials: the absence of a government-published error rate is not evidence that errors are rare — it is evidence that systematic measurement has not been done. The regulatory enforcement actions that have been documented (FirstEnergy, RI Energy, PPL) occurred through complaint-driven investigation, not systematic audit. The true error rate is probably higher than what reactive complaint-driven enforcement reveals.

**Finding 12: The structural framing — information asymmetry + reactive enforcement = systematic under-detection — is the intellectually honest investor narrative.**
Source: Synthesized from Harvard Law (2025) [Claim Verification Report, Ref. 8], RI PUC finding [Ref. 6], and Claim Verification Report's broader methodological observation. The argument runs: utilities generate the information regulators use to set rates (Harvard Law structural asymmetry) → most billing errors are discovered by independent audits rather than regulatory proceedings (Harvard Law) → 83% of bills the utility itself flagged as problematic were sent to customers anyway (RI PUC) → no systematic government measurement of error rates exists (Claim Verification Report, Claim 4) → therefore the true error rate is structurally unobservable and almost certainly underreported. This is the intellectually honest framing and it is well-supported.

**My recommendation:** Do not use $10M as a stand-alone claim. ## 7. GAPS AND OPEN QUESTIONS

### Gap 1 — THE MOST IMPORTANT: Identified vs. Realized Recovery (Clear Current Specific)

**What we don't know:** Whether any utility has issued a credit or refund based on an error Clear Current identified in its pilot work. The $10M is identified value; realized value is unknown from the documents.

**Why it matters:** This is the difference between a promising demo and a proven product. Every sophisticated investor in the May 2026 raise will ask this question. The billing error opportunity case is intellectually solid with current evidence. But the question "has your software actually gotten money back for a customer?" is the conversion-from-pilot question, and no secondary research can answer it.

**Who can close it:** John Reuter and Dan Schreiber only. This is Ask 1 in the Synthesis Brief's Section 08, and it remains open.

### Gap 2: No Published Government Error Rate for Commercial Billing

**What we don't know:** What percentage of commercial utility bills, drawn from a random representative sample, contain a material billing error in any 12-month period.

**Why it matters:** Every statistic currently available (80%, 30%, 5–15%) comes from biased samples. None can be used as a market-level fact.

**What the gap means in practice:** Clear Current cannot say "X% of commercial bills contain errors." It can say "documented government enforcement actions at major utilities confirm billing errors are real, material, and persist despite utility quality control systems." That framing is accurate and compelling — but it is a softer market size argument than a precise error rate would provide.

**Could this be closed?** Not easily or quickly. A properly designed independent study would require sampling commercial utility accounts without a prior suspicion of error — a methodology no profit-motivated audit firm would fund. Academic or DOE-funded research could produce it, but not on a May 2026 timeline.

### Gap 3: Harvard Law (2025) Paper Scope

**What I cannot confirm from the available documents:** Whether the Harvard Law ELI paper's primary thesis is specifically about billing errors or about a broader utility regulation critique (the title — "Extracting Profits from the Public: How Utility Ratepayers Are Paying for Excessive Executive Compensation" — suggests the latter).

**Why it matters:** If the billing error asymmetry finding is a secondary observation within a paper primarily about executive compensation, citing it as the structural authority on billing errors requires careful qualification. The Claim Verification Report uses it specifically for the asymmetry/independent audit finding, which is appropriate. But in investor materials, the citation should be specific to the finding, not used as a headline: "Harvard Law's Environmental Law Initiative found that utilities generate the information regulators use to set rates — a structural asymmetry that makes independent audits, rather than regulatory proceedings, the primary mechanism for discovering mischarged costs."

**How to close it:** Read the actual paper (PDF linked in Claim Verification Report, Reference 8). Confirm the specific finding and its context within the paper's broader argument. This is a 30-minute task that should happen before the May raise.

### Gap 4: What Billing Error Categories Clear Current Actually Detects

**What we don't know from secondary research:** Whether Clear Current's product detects true billing errors (incorrect charges), tariff misclassification (correct charges on wrong rate structure), demand charge management opportunities (avoidable charges), or all three — and in what proportion across the $10M identified.

**Why it matters:** This is the definitional problem that undermines the 80% overpaying claim. Clear Current needs to be precise about what it finds. "Billing errors" (utility made a mistake) is a different claim from "billing optimization opportunities" (utility charged correctly but customer could have been on a better rate). Both are valuable product features. They should not be conflated in investor materials.

### Gap 5: No Data on Billing Error Rate in Deregulated vs. Regulated Markets

**What we don't know:** Whether the billing error rate (or complexity) is higher in deregulated ERCOT/PJM markets than in traditional regulated utility territories.

**Why it matters:** The Synthesis Brief makes the case (validated by Marc Spieler) that deregulated markets create billing complexity that's hard for competitors to replicate. Texas ERCOT with 100+ REPs, 87% commercial switching rate, and overlapping billing cycles during supplier transitions is described as particularly error-prone. But neither document provides data on whether error rates or billing complexity actually differ between deregulated and regulated markets.

**How to close it:** This could be partially addressed by a targeted interview with a commercial energy buyer who has accounts in both deregulated and regulated markets, or by requesting FERC's complaint data by utility territory type.

### Gap 6: Demand Charge Billing Errors vs. Demand Charge Management
