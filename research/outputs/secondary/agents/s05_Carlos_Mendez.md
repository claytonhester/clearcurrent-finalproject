**Ownership and trajectory:** Acquired by Resurgens Technology Partners in March 2021 when the company had approximately 2,100 customers. Has grown to 10,000+ users by 2026. The growth is real but the platform architecture "reflects its origins — a utility bill accounting system that has been extended, not rebuilt" (GTM Doc, Part 4). UNM: $9,600/year via Lobo Energy for a smaller campus (GTM Doc, Part 2, citation 16). Implementation costs: $5,000-100,000+ depending on complexity (GTM Doc, Part 4, citation 28). **Key weaknesses (documented):**

- Campus chargeback is the stickiest feature and the worst UX — user reviews on G2/Capterra describe it as "hard to understand," lacking documentation, and requiring extensive training (RSB, Section 03, 132 reviews cited)

- UX described as "outdated, un-intuitive, glorified spreadsheet" across documented reviews (RSB, Section 03)
- When the market demands a capability EnergyCAP lacks, their response is acquisition (Wattics), not build — meaning Clear Current should expect an acquisition attempt if campus chargeback AI becomes a visible category requirement (GTM Doc, Part 4)

**Strategic implication:** EnergyCAP's reseller channel (YearOut Energy, Lobo Energy) is both a retention mechanism and a vulnerability. Resellers maintain the customer relationship and control renewal conversations. If Clear Current can establish direct relationships with energy managers at EnergyCAP reseller customers before renewals, the displacement timing is predictable.

## 5. DELIVERABLE BUILD GUIDANCE

### ICP Definition by Vertical

**ICP-1: Large Research University (Primary Beachhead — HIGH confidence)**

_Profile:_ Flagship or large regional research university. 500+ metered points. Operates district energy system (steam and/or chilled water central plant). September 1 fiscal year. EnergyCAP (or comparable aging platform) as current energy management system, often via reseller. Dedicated energy manager with "Campus Energy Operations Manager" or equivalent title — but frequently understaffed relative to meter count. Separate Finance Director/Sustainability function with independent reporting requirements and budget responsibility.

_Primary evidence:_ Texas State University (Andee Chamberlain, 1,500 meters, 98 buildings, EnergyCAP via YearOut); UT Austin (Andi Ault, 1,000+ meters, district energy, PEER Platinum, $20M gas spend) — RSB Section 02.

_Entry point:_ Billing error audit — the "weird multiplier" event that Texas State described is the prototype trigger (RSB, Section 03). Frame as a no-risk audit engagement that finds the errors EnergyCAP isn't catching. Entry via the energy operations team, not finance.

_Expansion path:_ Anomaly detection/alerting (James Norton at Texas State confirmed this is the highest-value follow-on feature — RSB, Section 02), then campus chargeback (the displacement of EnergyCAP's stickiest but worst-UX feature), then regulatory intelligence (Andi Ault's budget timing gap — RSB, Section 03).

_Sales cycle estimate:_ 9-12 months from first meeting to signed contract for formal procurement. Can compress to 6-9 months if entering via a warm referral from a peer university or ESCO relationship. Budget cycle timing: must be on radar by October-November to be included in January-February budget planning discussions (RSB, Deliverable 2 analysis).

_ICP disqualifier:_ Universities that purchase EnergyCAP directly through a system-wide contract (University of Texas System, CSU System) — these have centralized procurement that extends the sales cycle to 18+ months and requires a different entry strategy.

### Sales Motion and Channel Strategy Assessment

**Direct Founder-Led Enterprise Sales (Confirmed Right Motion for Now)**

The GTM Doc (Part 1) states this is "the highest-friction but highest-margin path available" and "the right path for a pre-revenue SaaS company." This is the author's analytical judgment, not a documented finding. However, it is directionally correct and consistent with what Marc Spieler validated (land-and-expand motion, need to prove value before scaling). The specific reason it is right for Clear Current: campus chargeback and regulatory intelligence are capability-selling, not product-selling — they require a conversation to establish the pain, not a self-serve evaluation. That conversation must be founder-led at this stage because the product explanation requires deep domain credibility.

**The ESCO Channel Question — My Core Assessment**

This is the most underanalyzed question in both documents and the one where I have the most to add from my own analytical framework.

Don Johnson at Trane is the only channel voice in this research. His comments establish two things: (1) Clear Current's billing intelligence is complementary to Trane's building operations work, not competitive (RSB, Section 03); (2) Trane represents a portfolio of 100+ commercial energy buyers across TX/NM/El Paso who are "reactive" and who Don has relationships with (RSB, Section 02). What Don's comments do NOT establish: whether Trane would actively sell Clear Current, the economics of such a relationship, or Trane's organizational incentive to do so.

Here is my assessment of the ESCO/channel question based on 16 years of watching this play out:

**When ESCO channels work for energy software:**

- The ESCO has a recurring service relationship (M&V, performance contract monitoring, guaranteed savings contracts) that creates ongoing touchpoints with the buyer
- The software extends the ESCO's service capability rather than replacing a service the ESCO bills for
- The ESCO is large enough to have a dedicated software/technology practice that can train and support the product
- The vendor is small enough that the ESCO adds meaningful sales reach without the vendor being able to build that reach itself

**When ESCO channels fail or dilute margin:**

- The ESCO positions the software as a tool in their own service delivery (meaning they use it but the client doesn't pay separately for it — they just pay the ESCO's bundled service fee)
- The ESCO's sales team lacks technical depth to explain a billing audit platform's specific error detection logic
- The software's value proposition (finding billing errors the ESCO didn't find) creates awkward incentives — Trane doesn't want to lead with "we've been missing these errors in our M&V work"
- The ESCO controls the contract renewal and captures the relationship, leaving the software vendor with limited leverage

**The specific Trane dynamic:** Phil Combs at Trane described spending "10-15 minutes per meter doing manual data pulls" for M&V work (RSB, Section 02). This is a workflow problem Clear Current solves. But Phil also described Trane internally restricting AI tools that employees want to use (RSB, Section 03). This is a company that has a complex relationship with software tools — it both sees the value and controls employee access. A channel partnership with Trane would require navigating Trane's own internal technology governance before it could add sales velocity.

**My recommendation:** Do not build a formal ESCO channel strategy for the May fundraise period. Instead, use the Trane relationship (Phil Combs, Don Johnson) for two specific near-term purposes: (1) referral introductions to commercial energy buyers Don is already serving who are not yet Clear Current pilots — these are warm enterprise sales leads, not reseller transactions; (2) co-marketing proof points that a major Trane M&V engineer (Phil) sees the regulatory intelligence module as "super powerful." Formal channel economics should be a 18-24 month conversation after Clear Current has proven the direct sales model and has at least 5-10 paying customers to bring to that negotiation with leverage.

### Pricing Model Recommendation

**The Three-Tier Structure (from GTM Doc, Part 5) is directionally right but needs one adjustment.**

The proposed tiers are:

- Audit (Entry): 20-30% contingency on recovered errors
- Optimize (Core SaaS): $75-100/meter/year
- Platform (Enterprise): $120K-250K/year flat license

My assessment of each tier:

**Tier 1 (Contingency Entry): Viable for initial pilots, dangerous as a scaling model.**

The UtiliSave benchmark of 40% for 36 months (GTM Doc, Part 5, citation 44-45) documents that the market accepts contingency pricing. A 20-30% rate is below market, which is appropriate for a seed-stage company that needs volume of proof points. The risk Marc Spieler identified is real: as discovered savings scale to meaningful numbers, the percentage model becomes uncomfortable for the buyer. At $120K/year in ongoing savings, 25% = $30K/year to a software vendor for what increasingly feels like a monitoring subscription. That discomfort is the designed-in transition trigger to Tier 2. **The important operational requirement:** Clear Current must track and report realized recoveries (utility credits actually issued), not just identified errors, for this model to function. The RSB is explicit that this tracking does not currently exist (Finding 7).

**Tier 2 ($75-100/meter/year): May be underpriced relative to Gridium's benchmark.**

Gridium prices at $470-760+/month per building (RSB, Section 05). At an average of 5-10 meters per building, that implies $470-760+/5 to 10 = $47-152/meter/month, or $564-1,824/meter/year — far above EnergyCAP's $40-52/meter/year AND above the proposed $75-100/meter/year for Clear Current. Gridium's high per-building price signals that sophisticated commercial buyers will pay significantly above EnergyCAP rates for a platform with better analytics. **I recommend considering $100-150/meter/year for Tier 2** — still below Gridium but positioned as the premium alternative to EnergyCAP with a clear value justification (error recovery ROI).

**Tier 3 ($120K-250K/year flat): Underprices system-level university contracts.**

The University of Wisconsin RFP range is $500K-$2M for a system-level contract (GTM Doc, Part 2, citations 11-12). UT System (16 institutions) and CSU System (23 campuses) level contracts could represent $500K-$1M+ ARR from a single procurement event (GTM Doc, Part 5). **The $120K anchor that John is using is appropriate for a single-campus university (Texas State, UT Austin scale). For system-level contracts, the ceiling should be $500K-$1M.** The pricing model for system contracts needs to be articulated before any RFP response.

**The missing pricing element: the transition mechanism between Tier 1 and Tier 2.**

Neither document specifies how the contingency-to-subscription transition is engineered. My recommendation: build a 90-day proof-of-value period at contingency pricing, with a documented trigger at 90 days that converts to annual subscription. The trigger condition should be: "once we have identified and recovered [dollar threshold] in billing errors, the engagement converts to subscription pricing at [$X]/year." This creates a predictable, contractually-defined transition rather than a re-negotiation conversation at the 12-18 month mark when the customer has already received value and may resist paying ongoing fees.
