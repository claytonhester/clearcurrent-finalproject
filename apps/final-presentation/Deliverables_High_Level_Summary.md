## D0 — Executive Decision Brief

This is the master summary document — the "so what" of everything we found. Before writing any individual deliverable, we needed to identify the 5 things our research actually *proves*, meaning findings that showed up in multiple independent interviews and were backed by real government or industry data. It anchors everything that follows and is the most important document if John or Dan only have 15 minutes.

**Top findings:**
- Every single interviewee — across industries, roles, and company sizes — independently described a reactive operating posture. Energy managers are not proactive by choice; they're buried. This means Clear Current's product *must* surface insights before users come looking, not wait to be asked.
- Billing errors aren't a software bug — they're a structural feature of how utilities work. FERC caught $108M mischarged at a single utility. Rhode Island's PUC found that 83% of bills the utility itself flagged as wrong were *still sent to customers.* This is government-documented.
- The same problem showed up across every interview in two different forms: operations people care about demand and usage, finance people care about budget and bottom line. No current tool serves both audiences. Clear Current's architecture is built for exactly that split.
- There is literally zero competition in regulatory intelligence — monitoring rate cases, PUC filings, utility board decisions before they hit your bill. One buyer (UT Austin) told us she has to submit her energy budget in March but the City of Austin doesn't finalize rates until after that deadline. Nobody solves that. Clear Current can.
- Arcadia (the best-funded AI-native competitor) just completed a major strategic pivot 14 months ago. They're distracted migrating old customers and have zero university clients. The window is real, but it won't stay open forever.

## D1 — Engagement Trigger Map

This deliverable answers a simple but critical question: *when do energy managers actually pick up the phone?* The answer isn't "when they're looking for better software." It's when something hurts. We mapped every specific event — bill shocks, equipment failures, contract renewals, budget deadlines — that forces a buyer to take action. The insight is that these triggers are predictable and repeatable, not random.

**Top findings:**
- The highest-volume trigger across every single vertical is a monthly bill arriving with something that doesn't make sense. Texas State found a 10x error on a wastewater bill (3,500 gallons suddenly billed as 35,000). A hospital system had a bill jump from $40K to $140K with no explanation. These aren't flukes — they happen constantly.
- Most energy buyers don't actually understand what a demand charge is or why it makes up 30–70% of their bill. Don Johnson from Trane Technologies said "even hospitals just pay the bill." The trigger isn't awareness — it's confusion. Clear Current's entry point is the moment they see the number and can't explain it.
- Contract renewal windows (especially for natural gas — March through June) are when buyers are most open to new tools. Ann Walston at Bon Secours Mercy Health was literally executing natural gas supplier contracts on the day of her interview.
- Budget season is a moment of vulnerability. Finance teams are building next year's energy budget without knowing what rates will be. That gap is a repeatable, annual entry point for Clear Current.

## D2 — Seasonal Energy Management Calendar

This is a month-by-month playbook for *when* to reach out, *what* to lead with, and *why* based on what energy managers are doing at that point in the year. Energy management is not flat — it has hard seasonal rhythms, and a sales team that ignores those rhythms will be pitching the wrong thing at the wrong time.

**Top findings:**
- Every industry vertical has a "budget timing gap" — a moment when financial commitments are locked before the utility rates that determine those budgets are finalized. That gap is Clear Current's most defensible entry point, and it exists every single year on a predictable calendar.
- June through August is peak demand season across all verticals. Bills are highest, complaints are highest, and buyers are most in pain. This is when the product should be most visible.
- September through October is when compliance and sustainability reporting happens (GRESB, Energy Star, carbon disclosures). This creates a data collection workflow that naturally surfaces the kind of invoice-level evidence Clear Current provides.
- Higher ed budgets lock in January–March; healthcare locks in July–August; restaurants and hotels lock in October–November. Each vertical needs a different outreach calendar, and we built it.

## D3 — Customer Journey Maps

We mapped five complete workflows — from the moment a buyer first feels a problem to the moment they either fix it or give up. These are built almost entirely from direct quotes and experiences shared in our interviews. The maps show where current tools fail, where buyers get frustrated, and exactly where Clear Current slots in to relieve the pain.

**Top findings:**
- Almost every organization we talked to relies on a single experienced person to catch billing errors — someone who's been there 10–15 years and just *knows* when something looks off. Texas State's bill processor just left after 15 years. The institutional knowledge walked out the door with her. Clear Current is what you put in place so that knowledge doesn't disappear.
- Multi-site organizations (hospitals, universities, restaurant chains) are drowning in complexity. AdventHealth processes 1,000–2,000 utility bills *per day* across 73 different electric providers in 9 states, each with different terminology. There's no human system that can audit that. Clear Current can.
- Panda Restaurant Group paid a neighbor's electricity bill for an entire year because the wrong meter was assigned to their account. By the time anyone noticed, the error had become the new baseline, and the threshold-alert system didn't fire. This is what "backward-looking" software misses.
- Recovering money from a utility — once you find an error — is extremely hard. Ann Walston at Bon Secours recovered over $1M from a utility, but it took board members calling each other. Clear Current finds the error. The recovery itself still requires human follow-through. That's an honest gap, and we named it.

## D4 — Product Module Recommendations

Based on what we heard in interviews and cross-checked against industry research, we identified five core product capabilities that buyers are explicitly asking for — even if they don't know a product exists to solve them. This was written for Dan (CPO) to use as a roadmap and for John (CEO) to use as an investor story.

**Top findings:**
- **Billing Error Audit Engine** is the wedge — the most urgent, most universal problem. Every vertical needs it. It's the thing that gets someone on the phone.
- **Regulatory Rate Intelligence** is the most defensible white space in the entire market. No competitor offers it. The use case is documented and quantified. For UT Austin, the gap between their 3% budgeted energy escalator and the actual 5.9% commercial CAGR represents $580K per year in unplanned variance — on just one campus.
- **Multi-Site Bill Association** (linking the right invoice to the right building automatically) sounds boring but is deeply painful for large portfolio operators. AdventHealth and Bon Secours both described bills being paid by the wrong cost center. This is an automation story with measurable ROI.
- The **dual-audience design** — a conversational tool for the energy analyst and a push-notification dashboard for the finance director — is the single clearest product differentiator. No competitor has built this. Every competitor serves one audience and frustrates the other.
- The gap to close before the fundraise: investors will ask whether the $10M in identified billing errors has actually been recovered as cash credits. "Identified" and "recovered" are different things. The most powerful thing Clear Current can do right now is complete one end-to-end recovery loop and document it.

## D5 — Market Opportunity Heatmap

We scored five verticals — higher education, healthcare, restaurants, commercial real estate, and energy services — across five dimensions: how serious the problem is, how ready buyers are to purchase, how big the deals could be, how crowded the competitive landscape is, and how accessible the data is. This tells John and Dan where to focus first.

**Top findings:**
- **Higher education is the beachhead** — not because it's the biggest market, but because it has the best combination of everything: real buyer pain, a predictable buying calendar, a frustrated incumbent (EnergyCAP), no AI-native competition, and two existing relationships already in place at Texas State and UT Austin.
- **Healthcare is the long-term prize, not the first win.** The scale is enormous — Scott Czubkowski at Medxcel/Ascension manages $265M in annual utility spend across roughly 100 hospitals — but the IT security requirements are severe and the sales cycle is 18+ months. This is a year-two story.
- **Multi-site restaurants** (think Panda Express, McDonald's, Starbucks) have a specific and underserved billing problem around ratchet rates and sewer billing. The sales motion is different (franchise operations vs. central procurement), but the pain is real and documented.
- **CRE and energy services firms** are better treated as channel partners than direct customers. A firm like JLL managing 1,000 buildings is a multiplier, not an end user.
- The total addressable spend from U.S. commercial buildings is $241B annually. Clear Current doesn't need much of it — even capturing identified savings on a small slice is a nine-figure opportunity.

## D6 — Competitive Intelligence Brief

We researched every major competitor — EnergyCAP, Arcadia, ENGIE Impact, WatchWire — and mapped where they're strong, where they're weak, and what specifically needs to be true for Clear Current to win. This document is written to hold up in investor diligence, not just to sound good in a pitch.

**Top findings:**
- **EnergyCAP** is the dominant incumbent in higher ed and public sector. Their users are frustrated (132 G2/Capterra reviews describe it as "outdated," an "un-intuitive glorified spreadsheet"), but they stay because switching is painful. The win condition is: better chargeback UX + natural language interface + one documented university win. The barrier isn't loyalty — it's switching cost.
- **Arcadia** ($649M raised, last valued at $1.5B) is the most credible AI-native competitor — but they just completed a 14-month-old enterprise pivot, have zero university customers, have no natural language interface, and are currently distracted migrating old customers off a legacy platform. This window is real. It will close.
- **ENGIE Impact** is not really a software competitor — they're a consulting company that uses software as a retention tool. They actually use Arcadia's infrastructure. They're an enterprise outsourcer, not a product company.
- The market is consolidating (WatchWire was acquired by Tango in 2023, Arcadia acquired RPD Energy in 2025), but nobody is building the analytical intelligence layer. Incumbents are buying distribution; nobody is buying brains. That's the gap Clear Current fills.

## D8 — GTM Strategy Playbook

This is the operational 90-day plan — who to target, how to reach them, what to say, and in what order. It names specific ICPs (ideal customer profiles), maps the buying committee at a university and at a hospital, and gives Clear Current a sales motion grounded in what buyers told us directly.

**Top findings:**
- The ideal first customer is a large research university with 800–2,000 meters, a district energy system, EnergyCAP as the incumbent, and a recently vacated or newly filled energy manager role. Two of those are already in conversations.
- The sales motion cannot start with "here's our software." It has to start at the trigger moment — a bill anomaly, a contract renewal, budget season — and enter at the exact moment the buyer is in pain. Don't pitch; show up when it hurts.
- The buying committee at a university is five people: the energy manager (champion), the plant engineer (technical validator), the finance director (budget gatekeeper), procurement (compliance gate), and IT security (blocking force). Each needs a different message. The demo has to speak to the champion and the finance director at the same time.
- The single most important GTM action before May: close one full error recovery loop. Identify error → contact utility → credit issued → dollars returned. Document it. That's the case study that converts a pilot into a fundraise.
- The competitive window against Arcadia is real but time-bounded. 12–24 months of distraction. Higher ed is the vertical where they have zero presence. Build the beachhead before they look up.
